Eating Disorder Recovery Curriculum

A Trauma-Informed, Multimodal Treatment Guide

14 Sessions · Alynne Davis, MA, LCMHC · Individual & Group Adaptable

Curriculum Overview

This 14-session curriculum provides a comprehensive, trauma-informed approach to eating disorder recovery. It integrates Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT/CBT-E), Expressive Arts Therapy, Somatic Experiencing, and Internal Family Systems (IFS) to address the whole person — mind, body, and creative spirit.

Each session includes a therapist guide with clinical talking points, an in-session exercise with step-by-step instructions, a client take-home activity, and group adaptation notes. Sessions are designed to be flexible and paced to the client’s nervous system.

Modality Key

CBT / CBT-E Expressive Arts Somatic Experiencing IFS
Session 1

Welcome & Assessment

Building safety, understanding the story, and establishing a foundation for healing.

CBT-E Somatic Experiencing

Session Goals

  • Establish therapeutic rapport and a felt sense of safety
  • Gather a compassionate history of the client’s relationship with food and body
  • Introduce the CBT-E case formulation model
  • Assess current nervous system baseline through somatic check-in

Therapist Guide

Talking Points:

  • Normalize the courage it takes to seek support for eating concerns
  • Explain that eating patterns are not “failures” — they are often the body’s attempt to cope, regulate, or survive
  • Introduce the idea that food behaviors live in the body as much as the mind
  • Briefly overview the four modalities that will be woven throughout treatment

Clinical Notes:

  • Watch for signs of shame or minimization when discussing food history
  • Notice the client’s nervous system state: are they in a window of tolerance? Hyperaroused? Shut down?
  • Begin the CBT-E formulation collaboratively — this is a shared map, not a diagnosis delivered
  • Use open-ended, non-judgmental language: “Tell me about your relationship with food” rather than “Describe your eating disorder”

In-Session Exercise: Nervous System Baseline Check-In

15 minutes
  1. Invite the client to find a comfortable position. Ask them to notice where their body makes contact with the chair or floor.
  2. Guide a slow body scan: “Starting at the top of your head, just notice what’s here. No need to change anything. Just notice.”
  3. Ask: “If you could describe how your body feels right now in one word, what would it be?”
  4. Introduce the window of tolerance concept using a simple drawing: a river with two banks (too much / too little activation).
  5. Ask the client to place a mark where they feel they are right now. This becomes their baseline reference point.

Client Take-Home

Food Story Timeline

This week, create a simple timeline of your relationship with food. Start as far back as you can remember. You don’t need to write paragraphs — just words, phrases, or even colors that capture different seasons of your food story.

Journaling Prompt: “What did food mean in my family growing up? What messages — spoken or unspoken — did I receive about eating, body size, or appetite?”

Group Adaptation

In a group setting, replace individual food history with a group check-in: each member shares one word about how they feel arriving today. Conduct the nervous system baseline exercise together. Skip the detailed CBT-E formulation (save for individual sessions) and instead offer a group psychoeducation overview of treatment philosophy.

Session 2

Understanding Your Nervous System & Food

Learning the language of your body and how dysregulation shapes eating patterns.

Somatic Experiencing CBT Expressive Arts

Session Goals

  • Introduce polyvagal theory in accessible, non-clinical language
  • Help clients understand how nervous system states drive eating behaviors
  • Build foundational body awareness through somatic practices
  • Connect the dots between stress, safety, and food

Therapist Guide

Talking Points:

  • Explain the three nervous system states in plain language: “safe and connected” (ventral vagal), “fight or flight” (sympathetic), and “shutdown” (dorsal vagal)
  • Normalize that reaching for food when stressed is a nervous system strategy, not a moral failure: “Your body is trying to regulate. It found something that works fast.”
  • Explain how high-carb and high-fat foods provide rapid nervous system soothing through dopamine
  • Introduce the idea that healing means expanding the menu of regulation options, not removing food from it

Clinical Notes:

  • Some clients may feel relief hearing that their eating patterns have a biological basis — watch for emotional softening
  • Others may resist the body-based framing if they are highly cognitive. Meet them where they are.
  • Avoid overwhelming with neuroscience. Use metaphor: “Your nervous system is like a smoke detector — sometimes it goes off when there’s no fire.”

In-Session Exercise: Three-State Body Map

25 minutes
  1. Provide three body outline drawings (or have the client draw three simple body shapes).
  2. Label them: “Safe & Connected,” “Fight or Flight,” and “Shutdown.”
  3. Using colors, lines, shapes, or words, invite the client to fill in each body with what they notice in each state. For example: “When you’re in fight or flight, where do you feel it? What color is it? Is it fast or slow?”
  4. After completing all three, ask: “Which state is most familiar to you?” and “Which state do you tend to be in when you reach for food to cope?”
  5. Gently explore what the body might be asking for in those moments beyond food.

Client Take-Home

Nervous System Eating Log

This week, before you eat (when you can remember), pause for 10 seconds and notice: “What state is my nervous system in right now?” You can use the three labels from today’s session. Write it down alongside what you ate. This is not a food diary for tracking calories — it’s a curiosity practice about the connection between your body’s state and your food choices.

Journaling Prompt: “When my body feels unsafe or overwhelmed, what does it reach for? What might it actually need?”

Group Adaptation

Conduct the Three-State Body Map exercise individually but follow with a group share: each member shares one insight from their map. Normalize the range of experiences. Facilitate a brief grounding exercise together at the end to co-regulate the group nervous system.

Session 3

Food Moralization & Scarcity Thinking

Unlearning the rules that keep you at war with food.

CBT Expressive Arts Somatic Experiencing

Session Goals

  • Identify the client’s internalized food rules and “good/bad” food beliefs
  • Use CBT thought records to examine and challenge food moralization
  • Explore how scarcity thinking activates the nervous system
  • Create an expressive arts piece externalizing food beliefs

Therapist Guide

Talking Points:

  • Explain food moralization: when we label food as “good” or “bad,” we absorb those labels into our identity — “I was good today” or “I was bad today”
  • Introduce scarcity energy: when the body senses restriction (real or anticipated), it activates a survival response that often leads to bingeing, obsession, or preoccupation with food
  • Use CBT framework: identify the automatic thought (“Carbs are bad”), examine the evidence, explore the emotional and behavioral consequences
  • Connect to nervous system: “When you tell yourself you can’t have something, notice what happens in your body. That tightening, that urgency — that’s your survival system activating.”

Clinical Notes:

  • Food rules are often deeply tied to family, culture, and identity — approach with compassion, not confrontation
  • Some clients may have orthorexic tendencies that feel “healthy” to them — gently explore the rigidity
  • The expressive arts exercise often reveals beliefs the client didn’t realize they held

In-Session Exercise: Food Beliefs Collage

30 minutes

Materials: Magazines, scissors, glue sticks, large paper, markers

  1. Invite the client to create a collage of all the food messages they’ve absorbed — from family, diet culture, social media, doctors, friends. They can cut out images, words, or draw their own.
  2. Encourage them not to censor: “Put it all on the page. The things you believe, the things you were told, the things you know aren’t true but still feel true.”
  3. Once complete, sit with the collage together. Ask: “What do you notice looking at this? What surprises you? What feels heavy?”
  4. Invite a somatic check-in: “As you look at these messages, what happens in your body?”
  5. Optional: On the back of the collage, write what you wish you had been told about food instead.

Client Take-Home

CBT Thought Record: Food Rules Edition

This week, when you notice yourself thinking of a food as “good” or “bad,” pause and complete this thought record:

  1. Situation: What was happening? What food were you eating or considering?
  2. Automatic thought: What popped into your mind? (e.g., “I shouldn’t eat this”)
  3. Emotion & body sensation: What did you feel emotionally and physically?
  4. Evidence for: What supports this belief?
  5. Evidence against: What challenges this belief?
  6. Balanced thought: What’s a more compassionate, realistic perspective?

Journaling Prompt: “If food had no moral value — if it was just food — how would I eat differently? How would I feel differently about myself?”

Group Adaptation

Create individual collages but display them on a shared wall or table. Facilitate a gallery walk where members observe each other’s work (with permission). Discuss: “What themes do you see across our collages? What does it tell us about the culture we live in?” This externalizes the problem and reduces individual shame.

Session 4

The Restriction-Binge Cycle

Understanding the biology and psychology behind the cycle that keeps you stuck.

CBT-E IFS Somatic Experiencing

Session Goals

  • Map the restriction-binge cycle using the CBT-E maintenance model
  • Help the client understand how restriction creates biological urgency to eat
  • Use IFS to meet the “restrictor” part with curiosity
  • Practice somatic tracking during moments of food-related anxiety

Therapist Guide

Talking Points:

  • Draw the CBT-E maintenance cycle together: restricting → biological deprivation → preoccupation with food → binge episode → shame/guilt → compensatory behavior or more restriction → cycle repeats
  • Emphasize: “Bingeing is not a failure of willpower. It is your body’s predictable, biological response to deprivation.”
  • Introduce the IFS lens: “There’s a part of you that restricts. That part has a job, a purpose, a reason for being there. Let’s get curious about it rather than fighting it.”
  • Somatic connection: “When the urge to restrict shows up, what happens in your body? Where does it live?”

Clinical Notes:

  • The restrictor part often carries beliefs about control, safety, worthiness, or punishment
  • Clients may feel protective of their restricting patterns — honor this as the part’s role
  • Be prepared for emotion: understanding the cycle intellectually and feeling it somatically can bring up grief or anger

In-Session Exercise: Meeting the Restrictor

25 minutes
  1. Guide the client into a grounded, calm state using a brief somatic settling exercise (feet on floor, slow breath).
  2. Ask: “Can you notice the part of you that wants to restrict or control food? Where do you sense it in your body?”
  3. Invite curiosity: “How old does this part feel? What does it look like? What is it trying to protect you from?”
  4. Ask: “What would this part want you to know about why it does what it does?”
  5. Thank the part for its efforts. Ask: “What does this part need from you right now?”
  6. Close with a somatic check-in: “After spending time with this part, what do you notice in your body now?”

Client Take-Home

Cycle Mapping

Draw your own version of the restriction-binge cycle. Personalize it with your specific thoughts, feelings, and body sensations at each stage. Use colors, images, or words — whatever helps you see the pattern clearly.

Journaling Prompt: “If the part of me that restricts could talk, what would it say? What is it afraid would happen if it let go?”

Group Adaptation

Draw the CBT-E cycle on a whiteboard as a group, inviting members to contribute their own experiences at each stage. Conduct the IFS exercise as a guided visualization (members work internally rather than sharing aloud). Follow with optional sharing in pairs.

Session 5

Emotional Eating vs. Hunger

Learning to hear what your body is actually asking for.

Somatic Experiencing CBT Expressive Arts

Session Goals

  • Distinguish between emotional hunger and physical hunger without judgment
  • Build body awareness of hunger and fullness cues through somatic tracking
  • Explore what emotions are being expressed through eating
  • Introduce curiosity-based (not restriction-based) awareness of eating patterns

Therapist Guide

Talking Points:

  • Clarify: “Emotional eating is not the enemy. Sometimes eating for comfort is a perfectly valid choice. The issue arises when it’s your only regulation tool or when it leaves you feeling worse.”
  • Teach the difference between physical and emotional hunger: physical hunger builds gradually, emotional hunger arrives suddenly; physical hunger can be satisfied, emotional hunger often can’t
  • Somatic focus: “Where do you feel hunger in your body? What does fullness feel like? Many of us have been so disconnected from these signals that we genuinely don’t know.”
  • CBT lens: identify the thought pattern — “I’m not hungry but I need to eat” — and explore what the “need” is really about

Clinical Notes:

  • Clients with a history of restriction may have genuinely disrupted hunger cues — rebuilding takes time
  • Avoid creating another set of “rules” — this is about curiosity, not control
  • Some clients may feel grief realizing how disconnected they are from their body’s signals

In-Session Exercise: The Hunger-Fullness Body Scan

20 minutes
  1. Guide the client through a slow body scan focused specifically on the stomach, throat, chest, and jaw areas.
  2. Ask: “Right now, what sensations do you notice in your belly? Is there emptiness, fullness, tightness, warmth, nothing at all?”
  3. Introduce a 1–10 hunger scale (1 = ravenous, 10 = uncomfortably full) and ask the client to place themselves on it.
  4. Explore: “Think of a recent time you ate when you weren’t physically hungry. What were you feeling emotionally? Where did that feeling live in your body?”
  5. Ask: “If that emotion could speak, what would it say it needs? What are three things besides food that might meet that need?”

Client Take-Home

Curiosity Eating Practice

Choose one meal this week to eat slowly and with full attention. Before you eat, notice your hunger level (1–10). Halfway through the meal, pause and check in again. After the meal, notice your fullness level. There’s no “right” answer — just practice noticing.

Journaling Prompt: “When I eat for emotional reasons, what am I usually feeling? What might I need instead of (or in addition to) food in those moments?”

Group Adaptation

Conduct the body scan as a guided group exercise. Follow with a group discussion about the difference between emotional and physical hunger — normalize the spectrum. If appropriate, bring a simple snack (e.g., a piece of fruit) and practice mindful eating together as a group, noticing sensations before, during, and after.

Session 6

Trauma & Eating: The Connection

Understanding how your past shaped your relationship with food.

IFS Somatic Experiencing CBT

Session Goals

  • Explore how childhood experiences and trauma shape the relationship with food
  • Use IFS parts mapping to identify which parts use food for protection or soothing
  • Build somatic awareness of stored body memories related to food and eating
  • Normalize the trauma-eating connection without shame

Therapist Guide

Talking Points:

  • Explain the trauma-eating connection: “When the world felt unpredictable or unsafe, food may have been the one thing you could control — or the one source of comfort that was always available.”
  • Explore common pathways: neglect leading to hoarding food, criticism leading to shrinking the body, chaos leading to rigid food control, emotional unavailability leading to food as a substitute for connection
  • IFS framework: “The part that binges, restricts, or purges is a protector. It learned this strategy for a reason. We’re not trying to eliminate it — we’re trying to understand it.”
  • Somatic lens: “Trauma isn’t just stored in your mind. Your body remembers, too. Sometimes the way you eat is your body trying to process what it hasn’t been able to.”

Clinical Notes:

  • This session may bring up significant emotion — pace to the client’s nervous system
  • Do not push for specific trauma disclosure — let the client lead
  • Use titration: small doses of awareness, returning to grounding between
  • Watch for dissociation or shutdown — have grounding tools ready

In-Session Exercise: Parts Mapping Around Food

30 minutes
  1. Provide a large piece of paper and colored markers/pencils.
  2. Ask the client to draw themselves (or a symbol for themselves) in the center.
  3. Around the center, invite them to draw or write the different “parts” that show up around food: the restrictor, the binger, the judge, the nurturer, the rebel, etc.
  4. For each part, explore: “What does this part want? What is it afraid of? How does it try to protect you?”
  5. Use lines or arrows to show relationships between parts (e.g., the restrictor triggers the binger).
  6. Ask: “Which part feels closest to the surface right now? Which part have you been fighting the most?”
  7. Close with a somatic check-in and grounding.

Client Take-Home

Letter to a Part

Choose one of the parts from your map — ideally the one you have the most conflicted relationship with. Write it a short letter. You might include: what you understand about why it exists, what you appreciate about its efforts, and what you wish it knew now that you’re an adult with more resources.

Journaling Prompt: “If my eating patterns could tell a story about my past, what story would they tell?”

Group Adaptation

Provide psychoeducation on the trauma-eating connection as a group. Conduct the parts mapping as an individual exercise within the group. Invite voluntary sharing of one insight (not trauma details). Emphasize: “You don’t have to share your story to heal alongside others.”

Session 7

Body Image & the Inner Critic

Softening the voice that keeps you at war with your body.

CBT IFS Expressive Arts

Session Goals

  • Identify cognitive distortions related to body image using CBT
  • Meet the inner critic part through IFS with compassion rather than resistance
  • Create an expressive arts body map exploring the relationship with the body
  • Introduce body neutrality as an alternative to forced body positivity

Therapist Guide

Talking Points:

  • Name common body image distortions: all-or-nothing thinking (“If I’m not thin, I’m disgusting”), mind reading (“Everyone is judging my body”), filtering (only seeing flaws)
  • IFS: “The inner critic has a job. Usually it’s trying to protect you from rejection, judgment, or pain. When it criticizes your body, it believes it’s keeping you safe.”
  • Body neutrality: “You don’t have to love your body today. You just have to stop being at war with it. Neutral is a perfectly good place to start.”
  • Explore whose voice the inner critic sounds like — a parent, peer, coach, media?

Clinical Notes:

  • Body image work often triggers deep shame — go slowly
  • The body mapping exercise can be highly activating for some clients — offer the option to use an abstract body rather than drawing their own
  • Watch for self-objectification language and gently redirect to somatic experience

In-Session Exercise: Body Map — What My Body Carries

30 minutes

Materials: Large paper, body outline template (or freehand), colored pencils, markers, pastels

  1. Invite the client to use the body outline as a canvas for exploring their relationship with their body.
  2. Prompt: “Using colors, shapes, words, or images, mark the places on this body where you carry criticism, pain, shame, numbness, strength, gratitude, or tenderness.”
  3. Let them work in silence for 10–15 minutes.
  4. Process together: “What do you notice? Where is there the most intensity? Is there anywhere that surprised you?”
  5. Somatic check-in: “As you look at this map, what happens in your actual body right now?”
  6. Invite one body-neutral statement: “Can you find one thing your body does for you that has nothing to do with appearance?”

Client Take-Home

Inner Critic Log & Body Neutrality Practice

This week, notice when the inner critic speaks about your body. Write down what it says, then ask: “Whose voice is this, really? What is it trying to protect me from?”

Also practice one body-neutral statement each day. Not forced positivity — just neutral acknowledgment. Examples: “My legs carried me today.” “My hands made something beautiful.” “My body is here, and that is enough.”

Journaling Prompt: “If my body could write me a letter, what would it say? What does it want me to know?”

Group Adaptation

Create individual body maps. Offer the option of working on the front of the paper (what the world sees) and the back (what lives underneath). If the group is ready, invite members to share one area of their map and one body-neutral statement. Close with a group somatic grounding exercise.

Session 8

ADHD & Binge Eating Connection

Understanding the dopamine-seeking patterns that link ADHD and eating.

CBT Somatic Experiencing Expressive Arts

Session Goals

  • Educate on the neurobiological link between ADHD and binge eating
  • Identify dopamine-seeking eating patterns without judgment
  • Build somatic tools for impulse awareness and nervous system regulation
  • Develop CBT strategies for pausing between urge and action

Therapist Guide

Talking Points:

  • Explain the dopamine connection: ADHD brains are chronically under-stimulated. Food — especially carbs, sugar, and highly palatable foods — provides a quick dopamine hit that temporarily satisfies the brain’s need for stimulation.
  • Normalize: “This isn’t about lacking willpower. Your brain is seeking regulation, and food is fast and available.”
  • Distinguish between: eating for stimulation (boredom eating), eating for emotional regulation, and eating due to forgotten meals (hyperfocus then crash)
  • Introduce the “urge surfing” concept: noticing the urge like a wave without immediately acting on it

Clinical Notes:

  • Note: this session is relevant even for clients without a formal ADHD diagnosis — many women with eating disorders have undiagnosed ADHD traits
  • Be careful not to pathologize — ADHD eating patterns are neurodevelopmental, not character flaws
  • Some clients may feel relief (“There’s a reason for this!”) while others may feel overwhelmed by the complexity

In-Session Exercise: Urge Surfing with Somatic Awareness

20 minutes
  1. Invite the client to recall a recent moment when they felt a strong urge to eat outside of hunger.
  2. Guide them to notice where in their body the urge lives: “Is it in your chest? Your stomach? Your hands? Your mouth?”
  3. Ask: “What quality does this sensation have? Is it buzzy? Heavy? Restless? Urgent?”
  4. Introduce the wave metaphor: “Imagine this urge is a wave. It rises, it peaks, and — if you don’t fight it or feed it — it naturally recedes. Can you stay with it for just 30 seconds?”
  5. Practice together: breathe through the sensation for 30–60 seconds.
  6. Debrief: “What did you notice? Did the intensity change?”

Client Take-Home

Dopamine Menu

Create a “Dopamine Menu” — a list of non-food activities that give your ADHD brain the stimulation it craves. Organize it into categories:

  • Appetizers (5 min or less): cold water on face, upbeat song, stepping outside
  • Entrées (15–30 min): creative project, walk, call a friend
  • Specials (longer/planned): art class, hiking, dance, cooking a new recipe

Journaling Prompt: “When I reach for food and I’m not hungry, what is my brain actually looking for? Stimulation? Comfort? Distraction? Connection?”

Group Adaptation

Create Dopamine Menus as a group activity — members can share ideas and build off each other’s lists. Practice urge surfing together as a guided exercise. Normalize the ADHD-eating connection through group psychoeducation and shared experience.

Session 9

Postpartum Body & Identity

Grieving the body you expected and embracing the one that carried life.

Expressive Arts Somatic Experiencing IFS

Session Goals

  • Create space for grief around body changes during and after pregnancy
  • Explore identity shifts through expressive arts
  • Challenge the “bounce back” narrative with somatic self-compassion
  • Address postpartum eating disorder relapse risk with compassion

Therapist Guide

Talking Points:

  • Validate: “Grieving the body you had before pregnancy is not vanity. It’s a real loss, and it deserves space.”
  • Name the cultural pressure: the “bounce back” myth, celebrity postpartum bodies, the message that your body should disappear after serving its purpose
  • Postpartum vulnerability: hormonal shifts, sleep deprivation, and identity upheaval create a perfect storm for eating disorder relapse
  • IFS: “The part of you that wants to control your postpartum body — what is it afraid of? What does it believe will happen if you don’t shrink?”

Clinical Notes:

  • This session applies to all postpartum timelines — the struggle can persist years after birth
  • Be aware of perinatal mood disorders that may be co-occurring
  • Clients without children may still benefit from exploring body changes and identity — adapt as needed
  • Skip this session entirely if not relevant to the client and proceed to Session 10

In-Session Exercise: Identity Collage — Then, Now, and Becoming

30 minutes

Materials: Three pieces of paper, magazines, glue, markers, personal photos (optional)

  1. Create three collage panels: “Who I Was Before,” “Who I Am Now,” and “Who I Am Becoming.”
  2. Encourage the client to include images, words, colors, and textures that represent each phase — not just body-related, but identity, roles, desires, losses.
  3. Process: “What do you notice about these three panels? What was lost? What was gained? What is emerging?”
  4. Somatic check-in with each panel: “When you look at ‘Who I Was Before,’ what happens in your body? And now?”
  5. Close with a self-compassion statement directed at the body: “My body did something extraordinary. It deserves tenderness, not punishment.”

Client Take-Home

Body Gratitude Practice

Each day this week, place your hands somewhere on your body and say (silently or aloud): “Thank you for [what this part of my body has done].” This is not about appearance. It’s about function, resilience, and the life your body has carried.

Journaling Prompt: “What would it mean to stop punishing my body for changing? What would I do differently? How would I feel?”

Group Adaptation

Adapt this session for mixed groups by broadening to “Body Changes & Identity” (not limited to postpartum). Create individual collages and share one panel each. Close with a group body gratitude circle: each member offers one statement of gratitude for their body.

Session 10

Nourishment Beyond Food

Expanding your sources of comfort, pleasure, and soul-deep sustenance.

Expressive Arts Somatic Experiencing CBT

Session Goals

  • Explore the concept of “soul nourishment” — what feeds you beyond food
  • Identify areas of life that are depleted and in need of tending
  • Use expressive arts to explore nourishment through creativity, movement, and connection
  • Reintroduce rest as a form of regulation and healing

Therapist Guide

Talking Points:

  • Introduce the soul nourishment concept: “When you’re only feeding the body but starving the soul — starving for rest, creative expression, connection, play, meaning — the body will seek nourishment wherever it can find it.”
  • Explore the domains of nourishment: emotional (connection, support), creative (expression, play), physical (rest, movement, sensory pleasure), spiritual (meaning, purpose, awe), relational (intimacy, belonging)
  • CBT: identify the thought pattern “I don’t deserve rest” or “Productivity equals worth”
  • Somatic: “What does a truly nourished body feel like? Can you remember a time you felt deeply fed — not just by food?”

Clinical Notes:

  • Many clients with eating disorders are chronically over-functioning and under-resting
  • Rest may feel threatening to parts that equate busyness with safety or worth
  • This session often marks a turning point from “what’s wrong with my eating” to “what’s missing in my life”

In-Session Exercise: Nourishment Painting

25 minutes

Materials: Watercolors or tempera paint, large paper, brushes, water cups

  1. Invite the client to close their eyes and recall a moment when they felt deeply nourished — not by food, but by life. It could be a walk in nature, a conversation, a creative moment, a rest.
  2. Ask: “What colors belong to that feeling? What shapes? What movement?”
  3. Without speaking, invite them to paint that feeling for 10–15 minutes. No representation needed — just color, shape, and sensation.
  4. When complete, process: “What is here? What does this painting tell you about what your soul is hungry for?”
  5. Bridge to daily life: “How could you bring even 5 minutes of this feeling into your week?”

Client Take-Home

Soul Nourishment Inventory

Rate each area of nourishment on a scale of 1–10 (1 = completely depleted, 10 = deeply nourished):

  • Emotional connection
  • Creative expression
  • Physical rest
  • Joyful movement
  • Spiritual/meaning
  • Relational intimacy
  • Play and pleasure

For the lowest-scoring area, identify one small action you could take this week.

Journaling Prompt: “Beyond food, what is my soul hungry for right now? What would it look like to feed that hunger?”

Group Adaptation

Paint together in silence (with optional soft music). After painting, do a gallery walk and invite members to share what they notice about each other’s work. Complete the Soul Nourishment Inventory individually, then discuss as a group: “What areas are most depleted for us? What could we offer each other?”

Session 11

Building a Sustainable Relationship with Food

Moving from rigidity to flexibility, from rules to rhythm.

CBT-E Somatic Experiencing

Session Goals

  • Introduce CBT-E strategies for regular, flexible eating patterns
  • Practice nervous system check-ins before, during, and after eating
  • Develop a personalized meal rhythm that supports regulation without rigidity
  • Explore what intuitive eating might look like for this client

Therapist Guide

Talking Points:

  • CBT-E regular eating: eating at regular intervals (every 3–4 hours) helps prevent the biological deprivation that fuels bingeing — this is structure, not a diet
  • Somatic approach to meals: “Before eating, check in: ‘Where is my nervous system right now? Am I eating from hunger, regulation need, or habit?’ During eating, pause: ‘What does this food feel like in my body?’ After eating, notice: ‘How does my body feel now?’”
  • Distinguish between structure and rigidity: “Structure supports you. Rigidity imprisons you. We’re building a rhythm, not a cage.”
  • Introduce intuitive eating as a long-term goal, not a starting point: “Intuitive eating works beautifully once your body trusts that food is available and safe. We’re building that trust.”

Clinical Notes:

  • Clients with restrictive patterns may resist regular eating — the restrictor part may activate
  • Clients with binge patterns may fear that structure will trigger restriction
  • Frame regular eating as nervous system medicine, not a meal plan

In-Session Exercise: Mealtime Nervous System Check-In

20 minutes
  1. Walk through a typical eating scenario with the client (or use a real snack if appropriate).
  2. Before: Pause. Feet on floor. “What is my body telling me right now? Am I hungry, stressed, bored, numb?” Rate hunger 1–10.
  3. During: Eat slowly. Notice textures, temperatures, flavors. Pause halfway. “Am I still hungry? Am I enjoying this? What do I notice in my body?”
  4. After: Pause again. “How does my body feel now? Satisfied? Overfull? Neutral? Guilty?” Notice without judgment.
  5. Debrief: “What was that like? What was easy? What was hard?”

Client Take-Home

My Eating Rhythm

Design a flexible eating rhythm for your week. Not a meal plan — a rhythm. Include approximate times for meals and snacks, and build in nervous system check-ins. Notice which meals feel easiest and which feel hardest. Bring your observations next session.

Journaling Prompt: “What would my relationship with food look like if it was guided by my body’s wisdom instead of rules?”

Group Adaptation

Practice the mealtime nervous system check-in together with a simple group snack. Discuss the difference between structure and rigidity as a group. Members can share their current eating rhythms and brainstorm flexible approaches together.

Session 12

Relapse Prevention & Nervous System Toolkit

Building your personalized toolkit for moments when old patterns call.

CBT Somatic Experiencing Expressive Arts

Session Goals

  • Create a CBT relapse prevention plan personalized to the client’s patterns
  • Build a nervous system regulation toolkit with somatic anchoring practices
  • Identify early warning signs and high-risk situations
  • Develop a creative, tangible toolkit the client can reference

Therapist Guide

Talking Points:

  • Reframe relapse: “A slip is not a failure. It’s information. It tells you that something in your system needs attention.”
  • Identify personal early warning signs: skipping meals, body checking, food rule rigidity returning, isolation, over-exercising
  • Identify high-risk situations: holidays, life transitions, stress, relationship conflict, hormonal shifts
  • Build the toolkit: “What works for your nervous system? What grounds you? What do you reach for when things feel hard?”

Clinical Notes:

  • This session often brings up anxiety about ending treatment — normalize and address
  • Ensure the plan is realistic and accessible, not aspirational
  • Include both immediate (in-the-moment) and longer-term strategies

In-Session Exercise: Create Your Regulation Toolkit

30 minutes

Materials: Card stock, markers, small box or envelope

  1. Create a set of regulation cards. Each card contains one strategy the client has found helpful during treatment:
  2. Somatic cards: grounding exercises, body scans, pendulation, breathwork
  3. CBT cards: thought records, balanced thoughts, evidence testing
  4. Expressive arts cards: “Paint what you feel,” “Write a letter to a part,” “Move for 5 minutes”
  5. Connection cards: “Call [name],” “Text your therapist,” “Go somewhere with people”
  6. Decorate and personalize the cards. Place them in a box or envelope the client can keep.
  7. Practice: “If you came home after a hard day and felt the urge to binge, which card would you reach for first?”

Client Take-Home

My Relapse Prevention Plan

Complete the following:

  1. My early warning signs: (list 3–5)
  2. My high-risk situations: (list 3–5)
  3. My go-to regulation strategies: (list 5+)
  4. People I can reach out to: (list 2–3 with contact info)
  5. What I want to remember about my healing: (a statement to your future self)

Journaling Prompt: “What do I know now about myself and my relationship with food that I didn’t know when we started?”

Group Adaptation

Create regulation toolkit cards as a group craft activity. Members can share strategies and add to each other’s toolkits. Practice a “what would you do?” scenario exercise where members coach each other through hypothetical difficult moments.

Session 13

Integration & Expressive Arts Culmination

Honoring the journey through creative expression.

Expressive Arts IFS Somatic Experiencing

Session Goals

  • Create an integrative expressive arts project that captures the healing journey
  • Conduct a parts check-in to honor how the internal system has shifted
  • Practice a somatic celebration of the body and its healing
  • Begin the process of reflecting on growth and change

Therapist Guide

Talking Points:

  • Reflect on the arc of treatment: “When you arrived, what did your relationship with food look like? What does it look like now? What has shifted inside?”
  • IFS check-in: “Let’s check in with the parts we met along the way. How is the restrictor? The inner critic? The protector? Have any parts softened, shifted, or found new roles?”
  • Frame the creative project as a gift to themselves: “This is not about making something beautiful. It’s about making something true.”

Clinical Notes:

  • This session is inherently emotional — allow space for tears, laughter, and everything between
  • Some clients may minimize their progress — gently reflect back what you’ve witnessed
  • The creative project can take any form the client chooses: painting, collage, poem, movement piece, song, letter

In-Session Exercise: Healing Journey Creative Project

35 minutes

Materials: All available art supplies (paint, markers, collage materials, paper, clay, fabric, etc.)

  1. Invite the client to create a piece that represents their healing journey. It can be abstract or representational, visual or written, still or moving.
  2. Suggested prompts (choose one or create their own):
    • “Create a before and after — not of your body, but of your inner landscape.”
    • “Make something that represents what you’ve reclaimed.”
    • “Create a love letter to the part of you that survived.”
  3. Allow 20 minutes for creation.
  4. Process: “Tell me about what you made. What does it mean to you? What feelings arose while creating it?”
  5. Close with a somatic celebration: a slow, intentional breath, a hand on the heart, a moment of acknowledgment for how far they’ve come.

Client Take-Home

Letter to Yourself

Write a letter to yourself — the you who walked into the first session. What do you want her to know? What would you tell her about what’s ahead? What has she already survived that proves she can do hard things?

Journaling Prompt: “What does healing look like for me now? Not the destination, but the ongoing practice?”

Group Adaptation

Create individual projects and host a group “gallery” or “performance” where each member shares their work. Create a supportive ritual: after each share, the group offers one word of reflection or affirmation. Close with a group somatic celebration and collective breath.

Session 14

Closing & Continued Care

Celebrating your growth and charting the path forward.

CBT Somatic Experiencing IFS

Session Goals

  • Reflect on growth, shifts, and insights across the full curriculum
  • Review the relapse prevention plan and regulation toolkit
  • Discuss continued care options: ongoing therapy, Soul Nourishment, support groups
  • Close with a somatic and IFS-based celebration of the healing that has occurred

Therapist Guide

Talking Points:

  • Reflect together: revisit the food story timeline from Session 1. What has changed? What has the client learned about the roots of their patterns?
  • Review the regulation toolkit and relapse prevention plan. Are there updates needed? Does it feel comprehensive and accessible?
  • Discuss next steps: ongoing individual therapy, Soul Nourishment coaching program, support group, periodic check-ins, or stepping down to maintenance
  • Honor the courage: “Healing takes enormous bravery. Every session you showed up — especially the hard ones — was an act of self-love.”

Clinical Notes:

  • Endings can be activating for clients with attachment wounds — be attentive to the relational dynamics
  • Some clients may need continued treatment; this curriculum is a foundation, not a ceiling
  • Leave the door open: “Healing is not linear. You are always welcome to come back.”

In-Session Exercise: Somatic Celebration & Parts Gratitude

20 minutes
  1. Guide the client into a grounded, settled state. Slow breath, feet on floor, hands in lap or on heart.
  2. Invite them to scan their internal system: “Check in with the parts we met during this journey. The restrictor, the inner critic, the binger, the nurturer. How are they now? What has shifted?”
  3. Offer gratitude to each part: “Thank you for protecting me. I understand why you did what you did. I’m learning new ways now, and you can rest.”
  4. Ask the client to place a hand on their body and offer a statement of appreciation: “Thank you, body, for carrying me through this. We’re on the same team now.”
  5. Close with a slow exhale and a moment of silence.

Client Take-Home

Continued Care Commitments

Write down three commitments to yourself for the next season of your healing:

  1. One thing you will continue doing that has supported your recovery
  2. One thing you will reach for when things get hard (from your toolkit)
  3. One way you will nourish your soul beyond food this month

Final Journaling Prompt: “If I could go back to the beginning and tell myself one thing about this journey, what would it be?”

Group Adaptation

Host a closing circle. Each member shares one insight, one gratitude, and one commitment. If the group created artwork throughout the curriculum, display it as a gallery. Offer a group ritual: each member receives a small token (stone, bead, card) as a reminder of their courage. Close with a collective breath and a moment of silence for the work done together.
