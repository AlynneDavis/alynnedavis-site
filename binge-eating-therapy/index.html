There’s More Going On Than You Think

Binge eating disorder is one of the most misunderstood and under-treated eating disorders. It’s often dismissed as a bad habit or lack of discipline — and that framing is not just unhelpful, it’s harmful. It adds shame to something that already carries so much of it.

The truth is: most binge eating is the nervous system managing what feels unmanageable. Stress, overwhelm, restriction, trauma, ADHD-driven impulsivity, the body fighting back against years of dieting — bingeing is rarely about food. It’s about everything the food is being asked to hold.

I’m Alynne Davis, LCMHC, a binge eating therapist serving women in Charlotte, NC and via telehealth across North Carolina and South Carolina. I work with women who are ready to stop fighting themselves — and start understanding what’s actually driving the cycle.

Who This Is For

You might be looking for binge eating help in Charlotte if any of this sounds familiar:

  • You eat past fullness and feel deeply ashamed afterward
  • The cycle feels compulsive — once you start, you can’t stop
  • You restrict during the day, then lose control at night — or some version of that pattern
  • You’ve tried every plan, app, and reset — and the bingeing always comes back
  • Stress, boredom, loneliness, or anxiety reliably trigger episodes
  • You hide your eating from others
  • You’ve wondered if ADHD might be connected to the way you eat
  • You’ve been told to “just stop” and you’re exhausted by how unhelpful that is

If you recognize yourself here, you’re not weak or broken. You’re someone whose nervous system learned a strategy that’s no longer working for you. And that can change.

What Binge Eating Therapy Actually Looks Like

We don’t start with food rules. We start with curiosity. What is actually happening before a binge? What does the body feel? What need isn’t getting met? What part of you is trying to cope?

Understanding the Cycle

Before we can change anything, we need to understand it. We slow down and look at what happens before a binge — not just in your behavior, but in your body, your thoughts, your emotional state. This isn’t about judgment. It’s about getting curious enough to interrupt the pattern at its root.

Nervous System Regulation

Most binge eating happens in a dysregulated state — when the nervous system is flooded with stress, anxiety, numbness, or overwhelm. Food becomes a regulation tool. The work here is building real regulation capacity — so that food isn’t the only thing available when the nervous system needs to come down. Read more about nervous system dysregulation and how it connects to eating patterns.

Addressing Restriction

Physical or emotional restriction is almost always part of the binge eating cycle. The body that has been denied — through dieting, food rules, or the internalized message that you shouldn’t need too much — eventually takes what it needs. We gently examine restriction and work toward a more trusting relationship with food and hunger.

Working With Shame

Shame doesn’t stop binge eating. It perpetuates it. We examine the cycle without judgment, building the capacity to be with difficult experiences in a different way — one that doesn’t require food to manage them.

The ADHD Connection

For many women, ADHD-driven impulsivity and dopamine-seeking are core drivers of binge eating — in ways that typical approaches completely miss. If you’ve tried everything and nothing has stuck, ADHD may be part of why. We address this directly. Read more about how ADHD impacts eating in women.

Trauma Work

For many women, binge eating is tied to unprocessed experiences — childhood wounds, relational trauma, the body’s way of coping with what it hasn’t been able to put down. When that’s part of the picture, we work with it directly. Explore the connection between trauma and eating disorders.

Therapy Approaches I Use

Expressive Arts Therapy

Binge eating often carries emotion that words can’t quite hold. Expressive arts therapy gives that emotion another way to move — through writing, visual art, imagery, and creative process. This approach can reach the parts of the experience that exist below the level of logical explanation, creating room for something to shift.

Somatic and Body-Based Approaches

The body knows things the mind hasn’t caught up with yet. Somatic approaches help you slow down, tune into body sensation, and work with the physical experience of dysregulation — so that the urge to binge has somewhere else to go. This builds a different kind of relationship with your own body: one based on curiosity and presence rather than management and control.

IFS — Internal Family Systems

IFS helps us understand the part that binges — not as the enemy, but as a protector doing its best. When we can meet that part with compassion instead of war, something different becomes possible. IFS gives us a map for understanding the whole system that binge eating is embedded in, and for healing the wounds underneath it.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

CBT helps identify the thoughts and beliefs that keep the binge-restrict cycle in motion — the all-or-nothing thinking, the catastrophizing after a slip, the inner critic that triggers shame. We work to interrupt those patterns and build more flexible, compassionate ways of relating to yourself and to food.

What Working Together Looks Like

Your First Session

Your first session is a space to share your story. I’ll ask questions and listen closely, and we’ll begin to get a sense of what’s been happening and what you’re hoping for. There’s no checklist to complete. Just a conversation about where you are and where you want to go.

Weekly Sessions

Sessions are 50 minutes, typically weekly. We move at a pace that feels honest and grounded — not so slow that nothing shifts, but not so fast that we skip the things that actually need attention. This work is real, and it takes time. The goal isn’t perfection. It’s a genuinely different relationship with yourself.

In Person & Telehealth

I practice in Charlotte, NC and offer telehealth to clients throughout North Carolina and South Carolina. Many women doing this work find that telehealth actually creates more comfort — you’re in your own space, and there’s no commute after a session that went somewhere real.

Free 15-Minute Consultation

A free consultation is available before you commit to anything. It’s a chance to ask questions, share a bit about where you are, and see if we feel like a good fit. No pressure, no forms. Just a conversation.
