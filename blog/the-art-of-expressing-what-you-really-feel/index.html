The Art of Expressing What You Really Feel

January 28, 2026

February and the Weight of Unspoken Emotions

February often brings conversations about love, romance, and connection. Social media fills with declarations of devotion, store shelves are lined with heart-shaped everything, and there is an unspoken cultural expectation that this should be a month of warmth and togetherness. But for many women, February stirs up something far more complicated than butterflies and affection. It stirs up unspoken emotions.

Maybe it surfaces as a quiet ache when you see couples holding hands. Maybe it shows up as irritation you cannot quite explain, or a heaviness that settles in your chest when someone asks, "How are you?" and you reflexively say, "I'm fine." Maybe you are in a relationship and still feel deeply alone, unable to articulate the distance between what you feel and what you show the world.

If any of this resonates, you are not broken. You are not "too much" or "too sensitive." You may simply be carrying emotions that have never been given a safe place to land.

Why So Many Women Learn to Suppress How They Feel

From a very young age, many women receive messages — subtle and not-so-subtle — about which emotions are acceptable and which are not. You learn to smile when you are hurting. You learn to shrink your anger into something more palatable. You learn that being "easy to be around" is more valued than being honest about your pain.

Over time, these messages become internalized. The suppression of emotion stops being a conscious choice and starts becoming automatic. You may not even realize you are doing it. You just know that something feels off, that there is a gap between the life you are living on the outside and what is actually happening within you.

This pattern is especially common in women who have experienced trauma, who grew up in households where emotions were dismissed or punished, or who have spent years in relationships where their needs were secondary to someone else's. The coping mechanism of emotional suppression once served a purpose — it kept you safe, functional, and accepted. But over time, the cost of holding it all in begins to show: in your body, your relationships, your sense of self, and your capacity for genuine connection.

The Body Keeps the Score

When emotions are chronically suppressed, they do not simply disappear. They settle into the body. They show up as tension in your shoulders, tightness in your jaw, a knot in your stomach, or an overall sense of heaviness and fatigue that you cannot shake no matter how much sleep you get. Some women experience chronic pain, digestive issues, or a persistent sense of being "on edge" without understanding why.

This is not a flaw in your system. It is your body trying to communicate what your words have not been allowed to say. And it is one of the reasons that healing through the body — through creative, embodied practices — can be so profoundly effective.

Expressive Arts Therapy: A Different Way to Process

Traditional talk therapy asks you to put your feelings into words. And for many people, this works beautifully. But for women who have been trained to intellectualize, minimize, or perform their emotions rather than truly feel them, verbal processing can sometimes become another form of managing rather than releasing.

Expressive arts therapy offers something different. It uses creative outlets — art, movement, writing, music, and other modalities — as alternatives to purely verbal processing. The invitation is not to explain what you feel, but to express it. To let it take shape through color, texture, rhythm, gesture, or the raw flow of words on a page.

Why Creativity Bypasses the Inner Censor

When you sit across from someone and try to articulate a feeling, your inner censor often kicks in. You edit yourself. You choose the "right" words. You worry about how you sound, whether you are making sense, whether you are being too dramatic or not dramatic enough. The result is a filtered version of your experience — not the raw, unedited truth.

Creative expression works differently. When you are painting, there is no grammar to correct. When you are moving your body in response to what you feel, there is no script to follow. When you are writing without stopping to edit, the words that emerge can surprise you — they often come from a deeper, more honest place than your carefully constructed narratives.

This is what makes expressive arts therapy so powerful for women who have spent years being "fine." The creative process bypasses the part of you that manages and controls, and instead invites the part of you that knows — the part that has been waiting for permission to be heard.

Try This: A 10-Minute Creative Check-In

You do not need to be in therapy to begin building a relationship with your own emotional truth. Here is a simple practice you can try on your own, right now, that reflects the spirit of what expressive arts therapy does in a therapeutic setting.

Step 1: Set a Timer for 10 Minutes

Give yourself this small container of time. Ten minutes is enough to drop beneath the surface without feeling overwhelmed. Turn off notifications. Close the door if you can. Let this be a pocket of uninterrupted space just for you.

Step 2: Choose Your Medium

Pick whatever feels most natural or available to you in this moment. You might grab a pen and a blank sheet of paper for writing. You might choose colored pencils, markers, or crayons for drawing. You might stand up and let your body move — stretching, swaying, curling inward, reaching outward. There is no wrong choice.

Step 3: Respond to This Prompt

"What am I carrying right now that I haven't said out loud?"

Do not think about it. Do not plan your response. Simply let your hand, your body, or your voice respond. If you are writing, keep the pen moving without stopping to edit or reread. If you are drawing, let shapes and colors emerge without worrying about what they look like. If you are moving, follow the impulse in your body without trying to make it look like anything.

Step 4: Reflect on What Emerged

When the timer ends, pause. Take a breath. Look at what you created — or notice how your body feels after moving. Ask yourself gently: What surprised me? What theme or feeling keeps showing up? Is there something here I have been avoiding?

You do not need to do anything with what you discover. Simply noticing it is enough. The act of giving your inner world a form — any form — is itself a step toward healing.

This Is What Expressive Arts Therapy Does at Its Core

The Creative Check-In you just tried is a small window into something much larger. In expressive arts therapy, we create a safe, held space where this kind of exploration happens regularly and with the support of a trained therapist who can help you make meaning of what emerges.

We are not trying to produce beautiful art. We are not grading your creative output. We are giving your inner world a safe way out — honoring the emotions, memories, and truths that have been living beneath the surface, waiting for a language that goes beyond words.

For many of the women I work with, this process is profoundly relieving. They describe it as finally being able to breathe. As putting down a weight they did not fully realize they were carrying. As coming home to parts of themselves they thought were lost.

Who This Is For

If you are a woman who has become very good at holding it together — at being productive, capable, and "fine" — but who quietly feels disconnected from yourself, overwhelmed by emotions you cannot name, or exhausted by the effort of performing wellness, this work may be for you.

You do not need to be an artist. You do not need to know what you are feeling before you walk in. You simply need to be willing to explore, with curiosity and without judgment, whatever is living inside you that has not yet been expressed.

You Deserve to Be Heard — By Yourself

This February, instead of focusing on what love looks like from the outside, I invite you to turn inward. What would it mean to truly listen to yourself? To honor the feelings you have been carrying in silence? To let them take shape — in color, in movement, in words that are messy and real and entirely yours?

That is the art of expressing what you really feel. And it is one of the bravest, most healing things you can do.
