When Binge Eating Is About Control, Not Food

By Alynne Davis, LCMHC · May 11, 2026

A client once shared something with me near the end of her work in therapy — something she said after more than a year in full recovery from binge eating disorder. She said:

“My approach to food was a reflection of how I approached life. I realized it was my point of control when I didn’t feel in control of anything else. Through recovery, I learned I don’t have to control outcomes — I can live in the moment and experience things in real time.”

I have thought about those words many times since she said them. Because they capture something that takes most people years to understand about themselves — and something that clinical language rarely says as clearly as she did.

Binge eating, for many people, is not really about food. It is about control.

When Life Feels Uncontrollable, the Body Becomes the Thing You Can Control

We live in a world that is largely unpredictable. Relationships are uncertain. Work is stressful. Family dynamics are complicated. The future is not guaranteed. For many people, especially those who grew up in environments where they had little agency — where things happened to them rather than with them — the nervous system learns to find pockets of control wherever it can.

Food is one of those pockets.

When everything else feels out of your hands, eating can become one of the few places where you get to decide. What you eat. How much. When. It is yours. No one can take it from you. In moments of overwhelm, anxiety, or helplessness, a binge can feel like the one thing you are fully in charge of — even if, afterward, it also becomes a source of shame.

This is not weakness. This is the nervous system doing what nervous systems do: finding a way to create safety and agency when neither feels available.

The Shame Loop Makes It Harder to See

One of the reasons the control piece takes so long to recognize is that binge eating almost always comes with a wave of shame. And shame is a powerful distraction from understanding.

After a binge, most people are not asking themselves “what was I trying to control?” They are telling themselves they are weak, broken, out of control, or hopeless. The shame moves in so quickly that it buries the original function of the behavior.

This is part of why working with a therapist who understands the psychological roots of disordered eating matters so much. Not because you need someone to manage your eating — but because you need a space where you can slow down long enough to ask: what was that doing for me? What was I trying to manage in that moment?

Those questions are not comfortable. But they are the ones that lead somewhere.

Control Is Not the Enemy

It is worth saying clearly: the need for control is not a character flaw. It is a completely understandable response to experiences that felt unsafe, unpredictable, or overwhelming. If you grew up not knowing what mood a parent would be in when they came home, or if you have been in relationships where you walked on eggshells, or if life has handed you losses you could not prepare for — your nervous system learned to manage uncertainty by controlling what it could.

The problem is not that you want control. The problem is that food — as a control strategy — comes at a cost. It affects your relationship with your body, your relationship with eating, and often your relationship with yourself.

Recovery is not about eliminating the need for control. It is about understanding it, developing more flexible ways to feel safe, and gradually learning that you can tolerate outcomes you did not choose — and survive.

What Recovery Actually Teaches You

The client whose words opened this post described something that I think is one of the most profound gifts of recovery: learning to live in the moment.

When control is driving the bus, life is lived in the future — anticipating what might go wrong, bracing for outcomes, managing variables, staying one step ahead of uncertainty. It is exhausting. And it keeps you out of your own life.

Recovery from binge eating disorder often involves learning, slowly and with real support, that you do not have to control outcomes to be okay. That uncertainty is survivable. That your nervous system can learn to settle even when things are not resolved. That presence — actual presence in your own life — is possible.

That is not a small thing. For many people, it is the first time they have felt genuinely at home in their own experience.

If This Resonates With You

If you read this and felt something shift — a recognition, a quiet exhale, a sense of “that is exactly it” — that matters. That recognition is often the first step.

Binge eating that is rooted in a need for control does not respond well to meal plans or willpower challenges. It responds to understanding, to compassion, and to the slow work of building a nervous system that feels safe enough to let go.
