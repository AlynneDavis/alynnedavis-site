When Love Feels Like an Invisible Web: Untangling Covert Narcissism, Untreated ADHD, and Substance Use

September 15, 2025

For years, I felt like I was living inside an invisible web — questioning myself, feeling unseen, and utterly alone. The web was woven with confusion, sticky threads of half-truths, and patterns I couldn't name at the time.

He also struggled with substance use, a way of numbing the guilt and shame of untreated ADHD. What looked like selfishness was often fueled by avoidance — but that didn't make the impact any less painful.

If you've been in a relationship that left you feeling confused, drained, and unsure of your own reality, you are not alone. These dynamics are more common than most people realize — and they are extraordinarily difficult to name, especially when love is involved.

What Covert Narcissism Looks Like

Unlike the loud, grandiose narcissism most people picture, covert narcissism operates in the shadows. It's quieter, more insidious, and often far harder to detect — even for trained clinicians.

Covert narcissism can show up as:

  • The victim stance: They consistently position themselves as the misunderstood one, the one who has it hardest, the one who is always being wronged. Conversations about your pain are redirected back to theirs.
  • Passive-aggressive jabs: Instead of direct conflict, they use subtle comments, backhanded compliments, or silent withdrawal to communicate displeasure. You're left wondering what you did wrong.
  • Fragile self-esteem: Beneath the surface, there is deep insecurity. Any perceived criticism — even gentle feedback — is met with defensiveness, shutdown, or punishment through emotional withdrawal.
  • Control through withdrawal: Rather than controlling through overt dominance, the covert narcissist controls through absence — pulling away affection, attention, or communication until you comply or give up trying.

The result? You begin to question your own feelings, your own perceptions, your own worth. You walk on eggshells without realizing the floor is made of glass.

What Untreated ADHD Looks Like in Men

ADHD in men — particularly when it goes undiagnosed and untreated — can create significant relational strain. But the root is neurological, not intentionally harmful.

Untreated ADHD often presents as:

  • Chronic forgetfulness: Missing important dates, forgetting promises, losing track of responsibilities. Not because they don't care, but because their executive functioning is impaired.
  • Disorganization: Difficulty following through on plans, managing household tasks, or maintaining consistent routines. This can leave partners feeling like they carry the entire mental load.
  • Emotional flooding: ADHD brains experience emotions intensely. When overwhelmed, they may shut down, lash out, or retreat — not out of cruelty, but because their nervous system is overloaded.
  • Deep shame: Years of being told they're lazy, careless, or not trying hard enough creates a well of shame. This shame often drives avoidance, defensiveness, and sometimes substance use as a coping mechanism.

Why They Get Confused

Here's where it gets complicated: covert narcissism and untreated ADHD can look remarkably similar on the surface. Both can result in:

  • Broken promises and inconsistency
  • Emotional unavailability
  • Difficulty taking accountability
  • Reactive or defensive behavior
  • Partners feeling invisible, exhausted, and alone

When you're living inside the experience, it can be nearly impossible to tell which patterns are rooted in neurodivergence and which are rooted in personality. And when substance use is layered in, the picture becomes even more blurred.

The Key Differences

While the behaviors may look similar, the underlying mechanisms are fundamentally different:

  • Intent: A person with untreated ADHD doesn't intend to hurt you. They are struggling with executive functioning, emotional regulation, and impulse control. A covert narcissist, however, may use subtle manipulation to maintain control and protect their ego — often unconsciously, but sometimes with awareness.
  • Empathy: People with ADHD typically have intact empathy — they may even feel too much. When they realize they've hurt you, genuine remorse follows. Covert narcissists may intellectually understand your pain but struggle to truly feel it. Their empathy is often performative or conditional.
  • Growth potential: With proper diagnosis, medication, and therapy, individuals with ADHD can develop strategies for better relational functioning. Narcissistic personality patterns are far more entrenched and resistant to change — particularly without long-term, specialized therapeutic intervention.

When Both Exist Together

Sometimes it isn't one or the other. Sometimes it's both — and with substance use layered in, the web becomes nearly impossible to navigate alone.

Substance use often serves as a numbing agent — a way to escape the shame of ADHD, the discomfort of emotional intimacy, or the internal emptiness that narcissistic wounding creates. Alcohol, cannabis, or other substances can temporarily soothe the nervous system, but they also erode accountability, deepen avoidance patterns, and make genuine connection nearly impossible.

For the partner, this triple intersection creates a uniquely disorienting experience. You may find yourself:

  • Making excuses for behavior that keeps crossing your boundaries
  • Feeling compassion for their struggles while simultaneously drowning in your own
  • Blaming yourself for not being patient enough, understanding enough, or loving enough
  • Losing track of who you are beneath the caretaking, the hypervigilance, and the grief

The Emotional Toll on Partners

Living in this kind of relationship doesn't just exhaust you — it reshapes you. Over time, partners in these dynamics often experience:

  • Chronic self-doubt and second-guessing
  • Anxiety that feels constant and free-floating
  • Depression that settles in like a fog
  • Loss of identity — you can't remember what you enjoy, what you want, or who you are outside of the relationship
  • Physical symptoms: insomnia, digestive issues, chronic tension, fatigue
  • A deep sense of loneliness, even when you're not alone

This is not weakness. This is what happens when your nervous system has been in survival mode for too long.

Where Healing Through Expressive Arts Therapy Comes In

Traditional talk therapy can help — but sometimes words aren't enough. When you've spent years silencing your voice to keep the peace, finding it again requires more than conversation.

Expressive arts therapy offers a different pathway to healing:

  • Externalize the web: Through art, movement, and creative expression, you can make the invisible visible. You can paint the web, sculpt the weight you've been carrying, or write a letter to the version of yourself who first got tangled.
  • See patterns clearly: Creative processes bypass the intellectual defenses that keep us stuck in justification, rationalization, and self-blame. When you create, the truth emerges — sometimes in colors, shapes, or metaphors that your conscious mind wasn't ready to articulate.
  • Reclaim your voice: Whether through journaling, songwriting, collage, or movement, expressive arts therapy helps you reconnect with the parts of yourself that were silenced. It helps you remember that your perceptions are valid, your feelings matter, and your story deserves to be told.

The Web Is Not You

If any of this resonates, I want you to know something: the web is not you. It was built around you, slowly and carefully, by dynamics that were never yours to manage alone.

You are not the confusion. You are not the self-doubt. You are not the exhaustion.

You are the weaver of what comes next.

Healing is possible. Clarity is possible. Coming home to yourself is possible. And you don't have to untangle the web alone.
