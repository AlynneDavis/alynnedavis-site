The Connection Between Trauma and Eating Disorders

April 21, 2026

If you’ve struggled with your relationship with food for a long time — and you’ve also experienced trauma — you may have wondered whether those two things are connected.

They usually are.

This isn’t about blame, or suggesting that every eating disorder has a trauma story behind it. It’s about understanding the very real ways that difficult experiences leave marks on the nervous system — and how those marks often show up in our relationship with food, our bodies, and our sense of safety in the world.

What Trauma Does to the Body

Trauma isn’t just a memory. It’s an experience that gets encoded in the body.

When something overwhelming happens — abuse, neglect, a difficult childhood, a loss, a relationship that made you feel unsafe, chronic stress you couldn’t escape — the nervous system stores it. The body learns to brace, to numb, to stay ready for threat. Long after the event has passed, the body is still responding as if it might happen again.

This shows up in anxiety, hypervigilance, dissociation, and trouble feeling safe in your own skin. And it shows up — powerfully, consistently — in eating.

How Trauma Shows Up in Eating Patterns

Control as Safety

For someone whose body or environment felt out of control, rigid rules around food can feel like the one place where control is possible. Restriction becomes a way to feel safe, contained, powerful. This is one of the core mechanisms behind restrictive eating and orthorexic patterns.

Disconnection from the Body

Trauma often teaches people to leave their bodies — to dissociate, to go numb, to stop feeling. Eating can become disconnected from hunger and fullness cues because you’ve learned not to trust or listen to what your body says. Bingeing without awareness, eating past fullness without noticing, feeling “checked out” during or after eating — these can all reflect this disconnection.

Food as the Only Safe Comfort

If other sources of comfort were unsafe, unreliable, or absent, food may have become the most consistent form of self-soothing available. For children especially, this is a survival strategy. In adulthood, it often becomes the root of emotional eating and binge cycles.

Using Food to Feel — or Not to Feel

Some people use restriction to feel in control and powerful. Others use food to numb difficult emotions or create a sense of fullness that has nothing to do with physical hunger. Both can be ways of managing the emotional aftermath of trauma.

Body Shame Rooted in Violation

When the body has been the site of violation or harm — through abuse, through growing up in an environment where bodies were criticized or sexualized — the relationship with one’s own body becomes fraught. Eating disorders can become a way of punishing, hiding, or reshaping a body that never felt like a safe home.

What the Research Shows

The connection between trauma and eating disorders is well-documented. Studies consistently show that women with binge eating disorder report higher rates of childhood trauma and adverse experiences. PTSD and eating disorders co-occur at high rates, particularly in women. The National Institute of Mental Health recognizes the significant overlap between PTSD and other mental health conditions, including eating disorders. Sexual trauma is one of the strongest predictors of eating disorder development. And emotional neglect — less visible than physical or sexual abuse but just as impactful — is also strongly linked to disordered eating.

This isn’t coincidence. It reflects the body’s deep logic: when survival requires disconnecting from your needs, numbing your feelings, or finding any available means of comfort or control, eating patterns absorb those functions.

What Trauma-Informed Eating Disorder Therapy Looks Like

Treating an eating disorder without addressing trauma is like trying to fix a leak while leaving the pipe broken.

Trauma-informed therapy doesn’t mean we dive into your worst memories right away. Good trauma work moves slowly and carefully — building safety first, developing resources, ensuring that the process of healing doesn’t re-traumatize.

In my work with women in Charlotte, trauma-informed eating disorder therapy includes:

  • Nervous system work — learning to recognize when you’re dysregulated and building tools to come back to yourself
  • Body-based exploration — gently rebuilding trust with the body’s signals, including hunger and fullness
  • Expressive arts — using creative tools to access what words can’t always reach; trauma lives in images, movement, and sensation as much as in narrative
  • Understanding the function of eating behaviors — meeting the part of you that learned to use food as protection with compassion rather than shame
  • Processing underlying experiences — when the time is right, and with appropriate pacing, exploring the original wounds

You’re Not Broken — You Adapted

One of the most important things I want women to hear is this: the eating patterns you’ve developed are not evidence that something is wrong with you. They’re evidence that you did what you needed to do to get through something hard.

The restriction, the bingeing, the numbing, the control — these weren’t failures. They were survival.

Healing doesn’t mean erasing those strategies. It means developing new ones, so that the old ones no longer have to carry so much weight.

If you’re looking for support that holds both your trauma history and your relationship with food with equal care, I’d love to talk with you.
