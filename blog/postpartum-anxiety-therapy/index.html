Postpartum Anxiety & Perinatal Mental Health: When You Don’t Feel Like Yourself

Everyone told you this was supposed to be the most joyful time of your life. And parts of it are. But underneath — or sometimes overwhelming everything else — there is a hum of dread you cannot turn off. Racing thoughts at 3am. A body that cannot settle. The constant sense that something terrible is about to happen.

By Alynne Davis, MA, LCMHC  •  June 18, 2026

This is not what you expected. Or maybe it is partly what you expected — the exhaustion, the love, the overwhelm — but there is something else underneath it that is harder to name. You do not feel like yourself. And you are not sure when you started feeling this way, or if it is going to pass on its own.

You are not alone, and you are not failing. What you are describing — whether it is postpartum anxiety, depression, birth trauma, or simply the weight of becoming a mother — is a real, recognized, treatable experience. And it deserves real support.

The Season Nobody Fully Prepares You For

Pregnancy and the postpartum period are two of the most psychologically significant transitions a person can move through. Your identity is shifting. Your body has changed in ways that may feel unfamiliar or even alienating. Your relationships are reorganizing. Your sense of who you are in the world is being renegotiated at a time when you are also operating on very little sleep and very little support.

Mental health struggles during this season are extraordinarily common — affecting an estimated one in five new mothers — and yet they are still deeply under-discussed, under-diagnosed, and under-treated. Many women spend months feeling like something is wrong with them before realizing what they are experiencing has a name and a pathway to healing.

Postpartum Anxiety: The Condition Nobody Talks About

When most people think about postpartum mental health, they think about postpartum depression. But postpartum anxiety is actually more prevalent — and because it does not fit the image of a sad, withdrawn new mother, it often goes unrecognized for much longer.

Postpartum anxiety can look like:

  • Racing, intrusive thoughts that you cannot slow down or stop
  • Constant worry about the baby’s safety — checking, monitoring, unable to hand them over
  • Physical symptoms: heart racing, chest tight, stomach in knots, unable to take a full breath
  • Difficulty sleeping even when the baby is asleep and someone else is watching
  • A persistent sense of dread that something bad is about to happen
  • Feeling like you are on high alert at all times, unable to relax
  • Irritability that surprises you — snapping at your partner, feeling unreasonably reactive
  • Intrusive thoughts about harm coming to your baby (not a desire to harm, but terrifying “what if” images)

Postpartum anxiety often looks like a very devoted, very vigilant, very together mother. Which is part of why it hides so well.

When Pregnancy and New Motherhood Activate Old Wounds

For many women, the perinatal period does not just bring new challenges — it reactivates old ones. Pregnancy and new motherhood are profound encounters with vulnerability, dependency, loss of control, and the body. If you carry unprocessed trauma — from childhood, from previous relationships, from a difficult medical history, or from earlier experiences with food and your body — this season has a way of bringing it forward.

A woman who has worked hard to manage her relationship with food may find that pregnancy’s inevitable body changes destabilize that work. A woman with an early history of abandonment or emotional neglect may find that becoming a mother activates grief she has carried for decades. A woman who experienced a previous pregnancy loss may carry it into a new pregnancy as anticipatory dread she cannot put down.

This is not weakness. It is the nature of significant life transitions: they tend to surface what has not yet been fully processed. And it is a powerful opportunity, with the right support, to do some of the deepest healing of your life.

What Perinatal Mental Health Therapy Addresses

Perinatal mental health therapy is not one-size-fits-all, because the perinatal experience is not one-size-fits-all. Support during this season may address:

  • Postpartum anxiety and depression — stabilizing the nervous system, reducing intrusive thoughts, building genuine capacity for rest
  • Birth trauma — processing a delivery that did not go as planned, an emergency, or an experience that left you feeling frightened or unheard
  • Identity shifts — the profound disorientation of motherhood and who you are becoming
  • Relationship strain — the way a new baby reorganizes every relationship in your life, including the one with yourself
  • Body image in the postpartum body — navigating changes in how your body looks and feels when the cultural message is to “bounce back”
  • Grief — for previous pregnancy losses, for the version of yourself before becoming a mother, for the birth experience you did not get
  • The reactivation of earlier trauma — what pregnancy and motherhood have surfaced from your own history

Why Telehealth Is Often the Best Option for New Mothers

One of the most significant barriers to postpartum mental health support is logistics. Getting out of the house with a newborn is a production. Finding childcare for an appointment is its own stressor. Sitting in a waiting room when you are already running on empty can feel impossible.

Telehealth removes most of those barriers. You can meet with me from your couch, your car, your bedroom — wherever you have a few minutes of relative quiet. No commute. No parking. No transition time. Just the support, delivered where you actually are.

If you are in NC or SC and struggling in the perinatal period, you are within reach of support.

You Do Not Have to Feel This Way Alone

The pressure on new mothers to feel grateful, joyful, and okay is enormous. Admitting that you are struggling can feel like a betrayal of the experience you were supposed to be having, or proof of something being wrong with you as a mother.

It is neither of those things. It is honesty. And it is the first step toward feeling better.

Whether you are pregnant and already noticing anxiety rising, newly postpartum and not recognizing yourself, or months into this season and still not feeling like you have come up for air — support is available and it works. You do not have to white-knuckle your way through this.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is postpartum anxiety?

Postpartum anxiety is a perinatal mood and anxiety disorder characterized by excessive worry, racing thoughts, difficulty sleeping, physical symptoms like a racing heart or tight chest, and a persistent sense that something bad is about to happen. It is not a character flaw or a sign of bad parenting — it is a clinical condition with identifiable causes and effective treatment, and it affects far more new mothers than most people realize.

What is the difference between postpartum depression and postpartum anxiety?

Postpartum depression is characterized by persistent sadness, withdrawal, and difficulty bonding. Postpartum anxiety is characterized by excessive worry, hypervigilance, and a nervous system that feels constantly activated. Many women with postpartum anxiety do not feel sad — they feel wired, scared, and unable to turn their minds off. Both are real, both are treatable, and they can occur at the same time.

What does perinatal mental health therapy look like?

Perinatal mental health therapy is tailored to the emotional and psychological experiences of pregnancy and the postpartum period. It may address anxiety, depression, birth trauma, identity shifts, relationship changes, body image concerns, grief, or the reactivation of earlier trauma. Therapy during this season focuses on regulation, self-compassion, and processing difficult experiences at a pace that works for where you are.
