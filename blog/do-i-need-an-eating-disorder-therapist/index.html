How to Know if You Need an Eating Disorder Therapist

April 7, 2026

There’s a question I hear often — sometimes out loud, sometimes in the way someone trails off mid-sentence:

“But is it really bad enough to get help?”

It’s one of the most painful questions a person can ask themselves. Because underneath it is usually this: Am I allowed to be struggling?

Here’s what I want you to know before we go any further: you don’t have to have a clinical diagnosis. You don’t have to be at your lowest weight or your highest. You don’t have to have been hospitalized, or have someone else express concern, or check any particular box to deserve support.

If your relationship with food is causing you suffering — that’s enough.

But I also know that “enough” can feel abstract when you’re in the middle of it. So let me be more concrete.

Signs You Might Benefit from Working with an Eating Disorder Therapist

1. Food Takes Up a Disproportionate Amount of Your Mental Space

You wake up thinking about what you’ll eat — or won’t eat. You spend meals either meticulously tracking or feeling like you’ve already failed. You replay what you ate yesterday and negotiate with yourself about tomorrow. If food is occupying mental real estate that leaves little room for anything else, that’s worth paying attention to.

2. You Eat in Ways That Feel Out of Your Control

This might look like eating well all day and then feeling like something “snaps” in the evening. Or buying foods you swear you won’t touch and eating them all at once. Or eating past fullness not because you’re enjoying it, but because you can’t seem to stop — and you don’t know why.

3. You Use Food to Manage Emotions

Eating when you’re anxious, lonely, bored, overwhelmed, or numb isn’t a moral failing — it’s a coping strategy. A very human one. But when it becomes the primary strategy, and when it always ends in shame, something more is going on.

4. You Restrict — and Then Swing the Other Direction

The restrict-binge cycle is exhausting and demoralizing. Each “new start” feels like hope, and each slip feels like proof of something broken. If you’ve been in this loop for months or years, you deserve support that actually interrupts it — not just another food plan.

5. Your Body Image Causes You Significant Distress

If you spend meaningful amounts of time criticizing what you see in the mirror, avoiding situations because of how you feel in your body, or measuring your worth in your size or shape — this isn’t just “normal insecurity.” It’s worth exploring with someone who understands how deep this can go.

6. You Don’t Fit the “Typical” Picture — But Something Still Feels Wrong

Many women dismiss their own struggle because they don’t look the way they think an eating disorder “should” look. But disordered eating exists on a wide spectrum. You can be in a larger body and have restrictive behaviors. You can binge eat without ever restricting. You can have a deeply troubled relationship with food that doesn’t meet diagnostic criteria and still be suffering. The National Eating Disorders Association recognizes this full spectrum and provides guidance for people at every stage.

If you’ve ever thought I’m not sick enough for therapy, I’d gently offer: that thought itself is often part of the disorder.

What Eating Disorder Therapy Actually Addresses

It’s not just about food.

When women come to me for eating disorder therapy in Charlotte, we almost always end up talking about much more than what they’re eating. We talk about the experiences that taught them their body wasn’t safe or acceptable. We explore the anxiety that has no other outlet. We look at the ADHD symptoms no one ever recognized, the trauma that lives in the nervous system, the perfectionism that turned eating into a performance.

Food becomes a way in — a doorway to understanding yourself at a deeper level.

Therapy helps you:

  • Understand why you eat the way you do, without judgment
  • Interrupt cycles of shame that keep the behavior going
  • Build a relationship with your body based on something other than control
  • Find other ways to regulate your nervous system when emotions feel overwhelming
  • Trust yourself again — with food, and in general

You Don’t Have to Figure Out If You “Qualify”

If you’re reading this, something brought you here. That something matters.

You don’t have to have the worst story in the room to deserve a seat in it.
