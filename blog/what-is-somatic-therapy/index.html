What Is Somatic Therapy? A Plain-Language Guide

You have probably heard the phrase “the body keeps the score.” You may have nodded along because something in you already knows it is true — that the tension in your shoulders, the tightness in your chest, the way your stomach drops in certain conversations is not random. Your body is holding something.

By Alynne Davis, MA, LCMHC  •  June 18, 2026

Somatic therapy is the name for approaches to healing that work with the body as a partner in that process — not just as a container for the mind, but as an active source of information, memory, and change.

If you have ever felt like you understand your patterns intellectually but cannot seem to actually shift them — like you know exactly why you do what you do and yet keep doing it anyway — that gap is often where somatic therapy lives. Because insight alone does not reach the places where the body has learned to brace, numb, or react. Getting there requires a different kind of work.

What “Somatic” Actually Means

The word somatic comes from the Greek soma, meaning body. Somatic therapy, at its core, means therapy that takes the body seriously — that treats your physical sensations, your nervous system responses, your breath and posture and tension patterns as important data about your inner life and history.

This is a departure from more traditional approaches that focus primarily on thought patterns and narrative. Cognitive approaches ask: What are you thinking? Somatic approaches also ask: What do you notice in your body right now?

Neither is better than the other. In my practice, I find that the most effective healing draws from both — using somatic awareness to access what the body is holding, and working with thoughts, beliefs, and story to make meaning of it. The body and mind are not separate systems. Treating them as if they are is where a lot of traditional therapy leaves people feeling like something is still missing.

Why the Body Holds Trauma

When something overwhelming happens — a frightening event, a chronic stressful environment, a relationship that required you to suppress yourself — the nervous system responds. Heart rate spikes. Muscles brace. The body mobilizes energy to fight or flee, or shuts down into freeze.

If that response is allowed to complete — if the danger passes, the body settles, and there is time and support to process what happened — the nervous system returns to baseline. But when the response is interrupted, suppressed, or has nowhere to go (which is common in chronic stress, childhood trauma, and experiences where no one around you acknowledged what was happening), that activation can remain stored in the body.

Stored activation looks different for different people. It might be chronic muscle tension that never fully releases. It might be a hair-trigger startle response. It might be emotional numbness, disconnection from physical sensation, or the sense of being constantly on alert even in safe situations. It might be patterns of bracing in the body before difficult conversations, or the way your chest closes when you try to set a limit.

These are not character flaws or psychological weaknesses. They are the body’s intelligence, doing exactly what it was designed to do: protect you from something that once felt dangerous. Somatic therapy works by gently engaging with those stored responses and helping the nervous system complete what it never got to finish.

What a Somatic Therapy Session Actually Looks Like

One of the most common things I hear from people who are curious about somatic therapy is: I don’t know what that means in practice. What are we actually doing?

It is a fair question, because somatic therapy can sound abstract until you experience it. Here is what it actually looks like in a session:

Most of the time, we are talking. But woven into the conversation is an ongoing, gentle attention to the body. I might slow the pace when something significant comes up and ask: What are you noticing in your body right now? or As you say that, where do you feel it?

You might notice tightness in your throat. A heaviness in your chest. Your shoulders lifting. Your breath becoming shallow. Warmth spreading. A kind of bracing or shrinking. These sensations are not interruptions to the work — they are the work. They are the body’s way of contributing to the conversation.

From there, we might:

  • Simply stay with the sensation for a moment and let it move or shift
  • Bring curiosity to it: what is this sensation saying? what does it need?
  • Work with breath — not in a forced or performative way, but by noticing what happens when you let the exhale lengthen
  • Make a small movement or adjustment that feels right to your body in the moment
  • Use imagery or expressive arts to give form to what the body is holding

There is no script. It is deeply collaborative, and you are always in the driver’s seat. The goal is never to push you into anything your nervous system is not ready for. Healing at the body level happens gradually, through titrated doses of contact with difficult material — not through forcing or flooding.

How Somatic Therapy Differs From Talk Therapy

Talk therapy and somatic therapy are not opposites, and many therapists integrate somatic awareness into their broader approach. The key difference is where the primary focus lives.

In pure talk therapy, the main material is what you say — your thoughts, your history, your interpretations of events. In somatic therapy, your physical experience is given equal weight. What your body does as you speak is treated as just as relevant as the words themselves.

This distinction matters most for people whose issues are held primarily at the body level — particularly trauma survivors, people with eating disorders, and those with chronic anxiety or nervous system dysregulation. For these experiences, talking about what happened often is not enough. The body needs to be part of the healing.

Who Benefits Most From Somatic Therapy

Somatic approaches tend to be especially valuable for:

  • Trauma survivors whose symptoms live in the body — hypervigilance, startle responses, chronic tension, numbness or dissociation
  • Women with eating disorders who have a complicated or disconnected relationship with their own body
  • People with anxiety who experience it primarily as physical — racing heart, tight chest, shallow breath, stomachaches
  • Anyone who feels stuck despite having done significant talk therapy — who understands themselves but cannot seem to actually change
  • People who feel disconnected from their bodies, their emotions, or their sense of self
  • Women navigating life transitions where the body is carrying the weight of what is shifting

Somatic Therapy in My Practice

I weave somatic awareness through all of my work with clients. It is not a separate module or a technique I apply at designated moments — it is part of how I understand healing.

In sessions focused on trauma, we pay close attention to the nervous system’s responses as we approach difficult material, moving at a pace the body can tolerate. In work with eating disorders, somatic awareness is central to rebuilding a relationship with the body that is grounded in curiosity rather than control or avoidance. In expressive arts therapy, the body often leads — through the movement of a paintbrush, the choice of color, the way a piece of paper gets torn rather than cut.

The RRR Method at the heart of my practice — Remember, Return, Reclaim — is inherently somatic. Returning to yourself means returning to your body. It means learning to trust what you feel, to stay present with sensation rather than fleeing it, and to find in your own body the wisdom and steadiness that has always been there.

Frequently Asked Questions About Somatic Therapy

What is somatic therapy?

Somatic therapy is an approach to healing that works with the body as well as the mind. Rather than treating thoughts and emotions as separate from physical experience, somatic therapy recognizes that the body holds the effects of stress, trauma, and difficult emotional experiences — and that lasting healing requires addressing what lives in the body, not just what lives in the mind.

How does somatic therapy help with trauma?

Trauma is not just a memory — it is a body experience. When survival responses don’t get to complete, their energy can remain stored as chronic tension, numbness, hypervigilance, or disconnection. Somatic therapy works directly with these stored responses, supporting the nervous system in completing what was interrupted and building a felt sense of safety in the body over time.

What does a somatic therapy session look like?

Most somatic work happens through conversation, with careful ongoing attention to what is happening in your body as you talk. A therapist might ask what you notice physically in a given moment, invite you to slow down and breathe, or simply stay with a sensation that arises. You are always in control of how much you engage with your body, and the pace is always collaborative and gentle.

Is somatic therapy the same as talk therapy?

Somatic therapy and talk therapy are not opposites — many therapists integrate somatic awareness into traditional approaches. The key difference is that somatic therapy actively includes the body in the healing process. While talk therapy focuses on thoughts and narrative, somatic therapy also tracks physical sensations and nervous system states that hold important information about a person’s experience and history.

Who can benefit from somatic therapy?

Somatic therapy can benefit anyone who feels stuck in patterns that don’t shift through thinking alone — people with trauma histories, anxiety, eating disorders, chronic stress, body image struggles, or a general sense of disconnection from themselves. It is especially helpful for those who understand their issues intellectually but don’t feel different in daily life, or who experience physical symptoms alongside emotional challenges.
