Holistic Approaches to Managing Holiday Stress and Disordered Eating

November 4, 2025

The Holidays Are Complicated — And That Is Okay

The holiday season is often painted as a time of warmth, gratitude, and togetherness. And for many people, it does hold genuine moments of joy. But for women navigating disordered eating, body image struggles, trauma, or complicated family dynamics, the holidays can also bring a heightened level of stress, anxiety, and emotional pain that rarely gets acknowledged.

If you are dreading the holiday season rather than looking forward to it — if the thought of family dinners, food-centered gatherings, and the relentless cultural focus on indulgence and then restriction fills you with anxiety — I want you to know that you are not alone. And I want to offer you something more useful than the typical advice to "just enjoy the season."

This post integrates expressive arts therapy, mindful eating practices, boundary-setting, and body-positive approaches to help you navigate the holidays with more compassion, more tools, and less white-knuckling.

Why the Holidays Trigger Disordered Eating Patterns

To navigate the holidays more effectively, it helps to understand why they are so activating in the first place. The triggers are not random — they are deeply embedded in the structure and culture of how we celebrate.

Food Is Everywhere — And It Is Loaded with Meaning

Holiday gatherings are organized around food. Meals are elaborate. Treats are abundant. The message is to eat, enjoy, indulge. For someone with a healthy relationship with food, this can be genuinely pleasurable. But for someone navigating an eating disorder or disordered eating, each plate, each serving, each second helping comes with a cascade of internal calculations, judgments, and anxieties.

Food at the holidays is also loaded with emotional meaning. Grandmother's recipe is love. Refusing seconds is rejection. Eating too much is failure. Not eating enough is suspicious. The simple act of feeding yourself becomes a performance observed and commented on by others, and the pressure to eat "normally" — while your relationship with food is anything but — can be excruciating.

Family Dynamics and the Return to Old Roles

Returning to your family of origin during the holidays often means returning to old relational dynamics. You may find yourself reverting to childhood roles — the peacekeeper, the people-pleaser, the invisible one, the one who holds everything together. Family members may make comments about your body, your eating habits, your weight, or your appearance, either directly or through the thin veil of "concern."

These interactions can be deeply destabilizing, especially if your family of origin is where your disordered eating patterns first developed. Being back in that environment can reactivate old wounds, old coping mechanisms, and old patterns of self-harm disguised as self-control.

Comparison and the Highlight Reel

The holidays amplify comparison. Social media is flooded with images of perfect families, beautiful tables, effortless celebrations. In-person gatherings bring their own version: the cousin who seems to have it all together, the sister-in-law whose body looks a certain way, the aunt who cannot stop talking about her new diet. The constant comparison — whether digital or in person — feeds the voice that says you are not enough, not thin enough, not happy enough, not trying hard enough.

Mindful Eating Practices for the Holiday Season

Mindful eating is not about eating perfectly. It is about eating with awareness, without judgment, and with permission to honor what your body is telling you. Here are some practices that can help you navigate food-centered gatherings with more ease.

Eat Before the Event

One of the most common and harmful patterns during the holidays is restricting food before a big meal, either to "save room" or to "earn" the indulgence. This backfires almost every time. Arriving at a gathering ravenous disconnects you from your hunger and fullness cues and sets the stage for bingeing, which then triggers shame, which then triggers restriction — the familiar, painful cycle.

Instead, eat normally throughout the day. Have a balanced breakfast. Have lunch. Arrive at the gathering with your blood sugar stable and your body already nourished. This gives you the best chance of being able to tune into what you actually want to eat, rather than eating reactively from a place of deprivation.

Check In With Yourself Before, During, and After

Before you eat, pause. Place a hand on your belly. Ask: What am I feeling right now? Am I hungry? Am I anxious? Am I eating because I want to, or because I feel I should? This is not about controlling your eating — it is about bringing awareness to it. When you eat from awareness rather than autopilot, you are more likely to make choices that feel good in the moment and afterward.

During the meal, check in periodically. How does the food taste? How does your body feel? Are you still enjoying what you are eating, or are you continuing out of habit, pressure, or numbness?

After the meal, resist the urge to evaluate or judge. You ate. That is neutral. It is neither a success nor a failure. If difficult feelings arise — guilt, shame, the urge to compensate — notice them without acting on them. They are feelings, not facts. They will pass.

Give Yourself Unconditional Permission to Eat

This may sound counterintuitive if you struggle with disordered eating, but one of the most powerful tools in recovery is unconditional permission to eat. When you remove the rules — "I can have one serving but not two," "I can eat pie but not cookies," "I need to eat clean on Monday to make up for Sunday" — you take away the power that food has over you. Food becomes food, not a moral test.

This does not happen overnight. It is a process, often best supported by a therapist and a dietitian who specialize in eating disorders. But the holidays are a good time to practice, even imperfectly, the radical act of trusting your body.

Setting Boundaries with Family

Boundaries are one of the most important — and most difficult — tools for surviving the holidays with your mental health intact. Many women struggle with boundaries because they have been taught that having needs is selfish, that saying "no" is unkind, and that keeping the peace is their responsibility. But boundaries are not about controlling others. They are about protecting yourself.

Prepare for Common Triggers

Before the gathering, take stock of the comments or situations that are most likely to be triggering for you. Is it your mother commenting on your weight? Is it your uncle's diet talk at the table? Is it the expectation that you will eat a specific food or a specific amount? Identifying your triggers in advance gives you the power to prepare responses rather than being caught off guard.

Scripts for Difficult Moments

Having prepared responses does not make you rigid or inauthentic. It makes you protected. Here are some options:

  • If someone comments on your body or weight: "I'd prefer not to talk about bodies. How has your fall been?"
  • If someone comments on what or how much you are eating: "I'm good, thanks" — and redirect the conversation. You do not owe anyone an explanation of your food choices.
  • If diet talk dominates the conversation: "I'm taking a break from diet talk — it's been really freeing. Hey, did anyone see that movie everyone's talking about?"
  • If you need to leave: Give yourself permission to step outside, take a walk, sit in your car for five minutes, or leave early. You are allowed to protect your well-being.

The Power of the Planned Exit

One of the most empowering boundaries you can set is a planned exit strategy. Decide before the gathering how long you will stay and what your exit plan is. Drive separately if possible. Have a trusted friend you can text if you need support. Know that leaving early is not failure — it is self-preservation. You can love your family and also recognize that their environment is not always safe for your recovery.

Creative Outlets for Emotional Processing

The holidays stir up big emotions — grief, anger, loneliness, longing, joy, confusion, all of it, sometimes simultaneously. When emotions are this complex and this intense, verbal processing alone may not be enough. This is where expressive arts therapy offers a powerful supplement.

Journaling as a Release Valve

Keep a small journal with you during the holiday season. Before and after gatherings, take five minutes to write. Do not worry about structure, grammar, or making sense. Just let the words flow. You might write about what you are dreading, what you are feeling, what happened that triggered you, or what you need in this moment. Journaling externalizes the internal chaos and gives it somewhere to go besides your body.

Art-Making as Containment

When emotions feel overwhelming, sometimes the most helpful thing is to give them a container — a physical boundary. Try this: draw a circle on a blank page. Inside the circle, use colors, shapes, or words to express what you are feeling. The circle acts as a holding space — a reminder that your feelings are real and valid, but they are also contained. They do not have to consume you.

Movement as Regulation

After a difficult family interaction, your nervous system may be activated — heart racing, jaw clenching, shoulders tense. Instead of reaching for food or retreating into numbness, try moving your body. Step outside and walk briskly for five minutes. Shake your hands vigorously. Roll your shoulders. Stamp your feet. These simple movements help discharge the stress energy that has built up in your body and bring your nervous system back toward regulation.

Body-Positive Practices for the Season

The holidays are a minefield for body image. Between the cultural narrative of "holiday weight gain," the diet ads that start appearing in December, and the casual body commentary that seems to flow freely at family gatherings, your relationship with your body is under constant assault.

Dress for Comfort, Not Performance

Wear clothes that feel good on your body, not clothes that you think you "should" fit into. If your holiday outfit requires you to suck in your stomach, adjust constantly, or avoid sitting comfortably, it is not serving you. Choose softness. Choose ease. Your body deserves to be comfortable, especially during stressful events.

Curate Your Media Environment

In the weeks surrounding the holidays, be intentional about what you consume digitally. Unfollow or mute accounts that promote diet culture, body comparison, or the "earn your holiday meal" mentality. Seek out voices that affirm body diversity, intuitive eating, and the radical idea that your worth is not determined by your size.

Affirmations That Actually Help

Affirmations work best when they feel believable. If "I love my body" feels like a stretch, try something closer to where you actually are:

  • "My body carried me through today, and that is enough."
  • "I do not have to earn my food."
  • "Other people's opinions about my body are not my responsibility."
  • "I am allowed to take up space."
  • "My worth is not determined by what I eat or how I look."

Working with a Treatment Team

If you are in recovery from an eating disorder, the holiday season is not the time to go it alone. This is when your treatment team — your therapist, your dietitian, your psychiatrist, your support group — becomes most essential. Consider scheduling extra sessions in the weeks leading up to and following the holidays. Talk to your dietitian about a meal plan that accounts for holiday meals without restriction or rigidity. Reach out to your support system when you are struggling, even if the voice in your head says you should be able to handle it on your own.

Recovery is not about being strong enough to do it alone. It is about being wise enough to ask for help when you need it.

You Do Not Have to White-Knuckle Through the Holidays

If your current strategy for surviving the holidays is to grit your teeth, push through, and collapse in exhaustion on January second, I want you to know that there is another way. You do not have to endure this season. You can learn to navigate it — with more awareness, more compassion, more tools, and more support.

The holidays will never be perfect. Family will always be complicated. Food will always be present. But you can change your relationship to all of it. You can learn to set boundaries without guilt. You can eat with awareness instead of anxiety. You can process your emotions through creative expression instead of self-punishment. And you can arrive at the new year not depleted and ashamed, but grounded and whole.

That is what holistic healing looks like. And it is available to you — not just during the holidays, but every day.
