Why Summer Feels So Hard for Your Body — A Trauma-Informed, Somatic Perspective

June 7, 2026

Maybe you dread summer in a way you can’t quite explain to people who seem to love it.

Maybe it’s the clothes that feel too exposing, the pool parties you find excuses to skip, the way your body suddenly feels like something to manage rather than something to live in. Maybe you spend the season bracing — for comments, for comparison, for the exhausting inner noise that gets louder when there’s less to hide behind.

If summer feels heavy in a way that has nothing to do with the heat, you’re not alone. And you’re not vain, shallow, or broken for feeling this way.

What you’re experiencing often has roots that go much deeper than body dissatisfaction. It lives in your nervous system. And it makes complete sense.

It’s Not About Vanity. It’s About Safety.

When summer arrives and the cultural pressure to have the “right” body intensifies, something happens in the body that goes beyond discomfort with your reflection.

For many women — especially those with histories of trauma, disordered eating, or chronic shame around their bodies — summer triggers a real threat response. The nervous system reads exposure, scrutiny, and comparison as danger. Not metaphorical danger. Actual, physiological danger.

This is especially true if you grew up receiving harmful messages about your body. A parent’s comment about your weight. A peer’s cruelty at the pool. A culture that relentlessly told you your body was a problem to be solved. These experiences don’t disappear with time — they get stored in the body, in the nervous system, as implicit memory.

So when summer comes and bodies become more visible, more evaluated, more commented on — your nervous system doesn’t experience it as an aesthetic inconvenience. It experiences it as a familiar threat. And it responds accordingly: with anxiety, avoidance, hypervigilance, shame spirals, or a desperate urge to control food and your body.

This is a trauma response. Not a character flaw.

What’s Happening in Your Body

From a somatic perspective, body image struggles aren’t just thoughts — they’re physical experiences.

You might notice it as a tightening in your chest when you catch your reflection. A drop in your stomach when someone suggests going to the beach. A sudden constriction in your throat when someone comments on what you’re eating. A dissociated, floaty feeling when you’re in a swimsuit and feel watched.

These are your body’s signals. Your nervous system moving into protection mode — bracing, collapsing, or fleeing to keep you safe from something it learned, long ago, was threatening.

The work isn’t to override these signals with positive thinking or force yourself to “love your body.” That approach skips over something important. The work is to slowly, gently build a different relationship with those signals — one rooted in curiosity rather than war.

Somatic therapy offers exactly this. Rather than working top-down — changing your thoughts to change how you feel — somatic work moves bottom-up. Starting with what’s happening in the body, and gently creating new experiences of safety from the inside out.

The Expressive Arts Dimension

There’s another layer here that I find especially meaningful in my work with women navigating body image and trauma.

Often, the experiences that shaped our relationship with our bodies happened before we had words for them. Before we could articulate what a comment did to us, or what it felt like to be in a body that was criticized or controlled. Those experiences live in the body as sensation, image, and feeling — not as neat, narratable memory.

This is where expressive arts therapy becomes a powerful companion to somatic work. Through drawing, writing, movement, or image-making, we can access and process what lives below the level of language. Not to perform healing. Not to create beautiful art. But to give the wordless parts of your experience somewhere to go.

When a woman I work with creates an image of what her body shame feels like — without needing to explain it — something often shifts. The experience becomes visible, external, workable. Less like a prison and more like something she can look at, and eventually, something she can begin to put down.

Some Gentle Things to Try This Summer

These aren’t fixes. They’re invitations to get curious.

Notice without narrating. When you feel triggered by something body-related this summer, pause before the story starts. What do you actually feel in your body? Tightness? Heat? Numbness? Just notice. You don’t have to change it yet.

Name the nervous system state. Are you revved up and anxious (fight/flight)? Or flat and checked out (freeze/fawn)? Naming it can create just enough distance to breathe.

Give it an image. If you journal or draw, try this: what does your body shame look like right now? Not what does your body look like — what does the feeling look like? Color, shape, texture. Let it be abstract. Let it be honest.

Find one moment of neutral. Not positive — neutral. One moment today where your body was just doing its job: breathing, walking, holding a warm cup. That counts as data too.

Come back to the present. Shame tends to pull us into the past (old wounds) or the future (imagined scrutiny). Grounding in the present moment — through breath, sensation, or the five senses — is a gentle way to interrupt the spiral.

This Is Deeper Than Summer

The discomfort that peaks in summer doesn’t start or end with the season. It’s part of a longer story — about what you learned to believe about your body, what experiences shaped those beliefs, and what it would mean to slowly, carefully, begin to live differently.

Trauma-informed therapy for body image isn’t about achieving body acceptance on a timeline. It’s about building a relationship with your body that isn’t organized around threat. It’s slow, real work — and it’s some of the most meaningful work I do.
