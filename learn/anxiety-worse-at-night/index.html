Why Is My Anxiety Worse at Night?

February 28, 2026

The day was manageable. You stayed busy, you got things done, you held it together. But now the lights are off, the house is quiet, and your mind is anything but. Your thoughts begin to spin. Your chest tightens. Your heart picks up speed. Everything you managed to push aside during the daylight hours comes flooding in all at once — the worries, the replays, the what-ifs, the things you said and should not have, the things you did not say and wish you had. Sleep feels impossibly far away, and the more you try to force it, the more alert your body becomes.

If your anxiety gets worse at night, you are not imagining it, and you are not alone. There are real, biological reasons why your nervous system ramps up when the world gets quiet. Understanding those reasons is the first step toward creating the kind of safety your body needs to let go into sleep.

Your Nervous System Does Not Know the Difference Between Then and Now

To understand why nighttime is so hard, it helps to understand what your nervous system is doing all day. During your waking hours, your body has access to an enormous number of coping strategies, most of which you are not even conscious of. You are moving, problem-solving, interacting with people, consuming information, checking things off your list. All of this activity serves a dual purpose: it helps you function in the world, and it keeps your nervous system occupied. Movement, stimulation, and task completion all provide a kind of regulation, even if it is not the deep, restorative regulation your body actually needs.

When you lie down at night, all of those buffers disappear. The doing stops. The distractions fall away. And your nervous system, which has been running on low-level activation all day, is suddenly left with nothing between it and whatever it has been holding. The anxiety you feel at night is not new. It has been there the whole time. You are just finally still enough to feel it.

This is why telling yourself to just relax or just stop thinking does not work. Your nervous system is not racing because you are choosing to think anxious thoughts. It is racing because it has been in a state of chronic activation, and night is when the mask comes off.

Sleep Requires Vulnerability — and Vulnerability May Not Feel Safe

Sleep is one of the most vulnerable things a human body can do. It requires you to surrender control, to close your eyes, to let go of awareness, to trust that you will be safe while you are unconscious. For a regulated nervous system, this transition happens naturally. The body recognizes cues of safety — warmth, darkness, quiet, comfort — and begins to wind down.

But for a nervous system that has learned that vulnerability is dangerous, sleep can feel like a threat rather than a refuge. If you grew up in an environment where nighttime was associated with conflict, unpredictability, fear, or loneliness, your body may carry an implicit memory that the dark and the quiet are not safe. You may not have conscious memories of this. Your body does not need conscious memories to hold the pattern. It learned, at a preverbal, pre-cognitive level, that letting your guard down at night was risky. And it is still running that program.

This is why some people feel a specific, hard-to-name dread that settles in as bedtime approaches. It is not about tomorrow’s to-do list. It is not about any particular worry. It is a felt sense in the body that says, It is not safe to let go right now. And no amount of sleep hygiene tips can override that message if the nervous system is sending it.

The Biology of Nighttime Anxiety

Beyond the psychological and relational roots, there are biological factors that contribute to nighttime anxiety, and understanding them can help you feel less like something is wrong with you and more like your body is doing something predictable.

Cortisol and the stress response cycle. Cortisol, your body’s primary stress hormone, follows a natural daily rhythm. It is highest in the morning (helping you wake up and feel alert) and lowest at night (helping you wind down). But when your nervous system is chronically stressed, this rhythm can become disrupted. You may experience cortisol spikes in the evening — a second wind of stress activation just when your body should be winding down. This is not a character flaw. It is a hormonal pattern that reflects the level of stress your nervous system is carrying.

The absence of external regulation. During the day, you are surrounded by potential sources of co-regulation — other people, movement, sensory input, the structure of your routine. At night, especially if you sleep alone or feel emotionally disconnected from a partner, that external regulation disappears. Your nervous system is left to self-regulate, which may be a skill that was never fully developed if you grew up without consistent, attuned caregiving. The aloneness of nighttime can activate attachment wounds that feel formless and confusing but are deeply real in the body.

The brain’s default mode network. When you stop doing and start being still, your brain shifts into what neuroscientists call the default mode network — the neural pathways that activate during rest, daydreaming, and self-reflection. For many people, this network is where rumination lives. Without an external task to focus on, the brain turns inward and begins cycling through unresolved problems, past experiences, and future fears. This is especially pronounced for women with high-functioning anxiety, who may spend all day in a productive, forward-focused mode and then find themselves ambushed by their own thoughts the moment they stop.

Sensory deprivation and hypervigilance. Darkness and silence reduce the amount of sensory information available to your nervous system. For a hypervigilant system — one that is always scanning for threats — this reduction in input does not feel calming. It feels alarming. With less information coming in from the outside world, your nervous system fills the gap with internal signals: racing thoughts, body sensations, emotional flooding. It is scanning harder because it has less data, not because there is more danger.

When Nighttime Anxiety Has Deeper Roots

For some people, nighttime anxiety is not just about stress management or sleep hygiene. It is connected to specific experiences that made the night a time of danger rather than rest.

If you experienced abuse, neglect, or family conflict that escalated at night, your body may have learned to associate darkness and bedtime with threat. If a caregiver was unpredictable or unavailable at night — leaving you alone with fear, pain, or confusion — your nervous system may have encoded the lesson that night is when bad things happen. If you experienced disrupted sleep in childhood, whether from nightmares, a chaotic household, or the hypervigilance of monitoring a parent’s mood, your body may never have learned what safe, restful sleep feels like.

These are not just psychological memories. They are somatic memories — stored in the tissues, the muscles, the breath patterns of your body. Your body does not need to remember the events consciously to respond to them. It just needs the cues: darkness, quiet, stillness, aloneness. And then the old alarm sounds.

If this resonates with you, know that you are not overreacting and you are not being dramatic. Your body is responding to something real, even if that something happened a long time ago. Trauma-informed therapy can help you work with these somatic memories in a safe, paced way that does not require you to relive what happened but does help your nervous system update the old survival program.

Creating Safety for Sleep: A Nervous System Approach

The conventional advice for better sleep — reduce screen time, keep a consistent bedtime, avoid caffeine — is not wrong. But for people whose nighttime anxiety has nervous system roots, those strategies alone are not enough. You need to create the conditions for your body to feel safe enough to let go. This is a fundamentally different project than optimizing your sleep environment.

Build a transition ritual, not just a routine. The shift from daytime activation to nighttime rest is a significant nervous system transition, and your body may need more support for it than you are currently giving. Rather than going from full speed to lying in bed, build in a transition period of 30 to 60 minutes where you are gradually signaling safety to your nervous system. Dim the lights. Move slowly. Do something that involves gentle, repetitive sensory input — a warm shower, gentle stretching, brushing your hair, applying lotion. The repetitive, soothing quality of these actions sends calming signals to the vagus nerve.

Give your thoughts somewhere to land. One reason nighttime thoughts spiral is that they have nowhere else to go. Try a brief writing practice before bed — not journaling in the traditional sense, but a brain dump. Write down everything that is on your mind, every worry, every incomplete task, every unprocessed emotion. You are not solving these problems. You are externalizing them. You are telling your brain, I have captured these. They are written down. They will be here tomorrow. You do not have to hold them all night.

Use body-based calming techniques. The somatic tools outlined in How to Calm Your Nervous System After an Emotional Trigger are particularly useful at bedtime. Extended exhale breathing, where the exhale is longer than the inhale, directly activates the parasympathetic nervous system. Placing a hand on your heart or wrapping your arms around yourself provides warmth and pressure that your body reads as safety. A weighted blanket, if it appeals to you, provides deep pressure stimulation that can help a hyperactivated nervous system settle.

Create a sensory environment of safety. Your nervous system responds to sensory cues. Consider what signals safety to your specific body. This is personal — what feels soothing to one person may feel neutral or even activating to another. Some possibilities: a soft light left on (especially if darkness itself triggers anxiety), a sound machine that provides gentle, consistent noise, a familiar scent (lavender, vanilla, or whatever your body associates with calm), the texture of a specific blanket or pillow, or a warm drink held in both hands before bed.

Address the aloneness. If part of your nighttime anxiety is connected to feeling alone — whether you live alone or feel emotionally alone even with someone beside you — finding ways to bring a sense of connection into your nighttime routine can make a meaningful difference. This might mean listening to a familiar, comforting voice (a podcast, an audiobook, a guided meditation by someone whose voice feels safe), texting a friend before bed, or even writing a brief letter to yourself from the compassionate, caring part of you. Co-regulation does not always require another physical body. It requires the felt experience of not being alone with your distress.

What to Do When You Wake Up at 3 AM

Waking in the middle of the night and being unable to fall back asleep is one of the most common and most distressing features of nighttime anxiety. The 3 AM wake-up is particularly cruel because the world is at its quietest and your nervous system is at its most vulnerable. Here is what to try.

Do not fight it. The more you try to force yourself back to sleep, the more activated your nervous system becomes. Lying in bed wrestling with wakefulness tells your body, This is a problem. We need to fix this. And that message of urgency is the opposite of what your nervous system needs to hear. Instead, try this radical approach: accept that you are awake. Not resignedly, not angrily, but gently. I am awake. This is where my body is right now. I can be with this.

Shift your focus from your mind to your body. At 3 AM, your thinking brain is not your friend. It will spin stories, catastrophize, and convince you that everything is terrible. Instead of engaging with the thoughts, bring your attention to your body. Start at your feet and slowly scan upward, noticing what you feel in each area. You are not trying to change anything. You are just noticing. This kind of interoceptive awareness gently activates the ventral vagal system and can help ease you back toward rest.

Use the physiological sigh. Two short inhales through the nose, followed by one long exhale through the mouth. This breathing pattern, described in more detail in our article on calming the nervous system after a trigger, has been shown to be one of the fastest ways to reduce sympathetic activation. Do it a few times, softly, without forcing.

If you need to get up, keep the environment soft. If you have been awake for more than 20 minutes and your body feels wired, it is okay to get up. But keep the lights dim, avoid screens if possible, and do something unstimulating — sit in a chair with a blanket, sip warm water, read something undemanding. You are not abandoning sleep. You are giving your nervous system space to settle without the pressure of lying in bed feeling like a failure.

Nighttime Anxiety and the Bigger Picture

The anxiety that shows up at night is rarely just about nighttime. It is a window into the overall state of your nervous system. If your body cannot settle at night, it is telling you something about how much activation it is carrying during the day — activation that may be masked by busyness, productivity, caretaking, or sheer determination.

Addressing nighttime anxiety at its roots means addressing the broader patterns of nervous system dysregulation that underlie it. It means learning to create moments of genuine regulation during the day — not just powering through and collapsing at night. It means exploring the relational and developmental roots of why your body does not trust rest. And it often means working with a therapist who understands that sleep is not just a behavioral issue but a nervous system issue.

If you are a woman who pushes through all day and falls apart at night, if you have been called anxious and told to try melatonin, if you have been managing insomnia for years without anyone asking what your body might be trying to tell you, you deserve a different kind of support. Trauma-informed therapy can help you build the felt sense of safety that your nervous system needs to surrender into sleep — not through willpower, but through the patient, compassionate work of teaching your body that it is okay to let go.

You Deserve Rest That Actually Restores

You have been running for a long time. Your body has been vigilant, alert, scanning for danger, bracing for impact — even in the dark, even in your own bed, even in the place that should feel safest. That vigilance was not your choice. It was something your nervous system learned to do because, at some point, it needed to. But you are not in that situation anymore. And your body deserves to know that.

Rest is not a reward you earn by exhausting yourself enough to collapse. Rest is a fundamental need, and you are allowed to have it without guilt, without justification, and without having to prove that you deserve it. The work of healing nighttime anxiety is the work of creating a body that can receive rest — a body that knows, in its bones, that it is safe to close its eyes and let the night hold it.

If you are ready to start building that kind of safety, schedule a free consultation. You do not have to keep lying in the dark alone with your thoughts. There is another way, and it begins with the simple, brave act of asking for support.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why does my mind race as soon as I lie down?

When you lie down to sleep, you remove the distractions and tasks that have been keeping your conscious mind occupied all day. Without those buffers, your nervous system’s underlying state of activation becomes fully apparent. The racing thoughts are not new — they have been running in the background. You are simply now still enough to hear them. Additionally, the transition from active to still requires your nervous system to shift from a sympathetic state to a parasympathetic state, and if your nervous system is dysregulated, it may struggle to make that shift.

Is nighttime anxiety a sign of trauma?

Nighttime anxiety can be connected to trauma, but it is not always. For people with trauma histories, night can be particularly activating because sleep requires vulnerability, and vulnerability may not have been safe in their past. If nighttime was associated with danger or unpredictability during childhood, the body may carry an implicit memory that darkness and stillness are not safe. However, nighttime anxiety can also result from chronic stress, general nervous system dysregulation, or lifestyle patterns. If your nighttime anxiety feels especially intense, exploring it with a trauma-informed therapist can help clarify what your body is responding to.

Should I take sleep medication for nighttime anxiety?

Sleep medication can be a helpful short-term support for some people, and that is a conversation to have with your prescribing provider. However, medication alone does not address the underlying nervous system patterns driving the nighttime anxiety. If your body does not feel safe enough to let go into sleep, addressing that felt sense of safety through somatic work and trauma-informed therapy can create more lasting change. A combined approach that includes both professional support for sleep and therapeutic work on the underlying patterns tends to be most effective.

How can I stop checking my phone when I cannot sleep?

The urge to reach for your phone when you cannot sleep is often a nervous system strategy rather than a habit problem. Your phone provides stimulation that temporarily distracts your mind from anxious thoughts. Rather than trying to willpower your way out of it, replace it with something that addresses the same need but supports regulation: keep a journal by your bed for writing out racing thoughts, use a guided body scan or breathing exercise, or try an audiobook that gives your mind something to follow without visual stimulation. The goal is to address the need underneath the behavior rather than just removing the behavior itself.
