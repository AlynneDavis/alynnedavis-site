Healing Modalities: Therapy Types and How They Work

Not all therapy looks the same. Learn about the different modalities that support healing from trauma, eating disorders, ADHD, and emotional overwhelm — and how to find the approach that fits you.

Why the Type of Therapy Matters

When most people think of therapy, they imagine sitting across from someone and talking about their feelings. And while talk therapy can be incredibly valuable, it is only one approach among many. The truth is, healing from trauma, eating disorders, ADHD, and deep emotional pain often requires more than conversation. It requires approaches that reach the parts of you that words alone cannot touch — your body, your nervous system, your creative spirit, and the protective parts of you that formed long before you had language for what you were experiencing.

Choosing the right therapeutic modality is not about finding the "best" approach. It is about finding the approach that meets you where you are. What works for one woman may not resonate with another, and the most effective therapy is often an integration of multiple modalities tailored to your unique story, your nervous system, and what you need in this season of your life.

Expressive Arts Therapy

Expressive arts therapy uses creative modalities — painting, drawing, collage, movement, music, writing, and journaling — to help you access emotions and experiences that talk therapy may not reach. It is grounded in the understanding that creativity is a fundamental part of being human, and that the act of creating can unlock healing in ways that words alone cannot.

This approach is especially powerful for women who feel disconnected from their emotions, who struggle to put their experiences into words, or who have spent years living in their heads rather than their bodies. You do not need any artistic talent or experience. The value is not in making something beautiful. It is in what emerges as you create — the colors you choose, the way your hand moves across the page, the words that surface when you stop editing yourself. All of it becomes material for therapeutic exploration.

Expressive arts therapy can be integrated into individual therapy sessions or offered as a standalone experience, depending on what feels right for you.

Internal Family Systems (IFS)

Internal Family Systems is a gentle, compassionate approach that understands your inner world as made up of different "parts," each with its own role, feelings, and protective strategies. You may recognize some of your own parts: the inner critic that drives perfectionism, the anxious part that catastrophizes, the numb part that shuts down when things feel overwhelming, or the people-pleasing part that says yes when you desperately want to say no.

IFS does not try to silence or override these parts. Instead, it helps you develop a curious, compassionate relationship with each one, understanding why it exists, what it is protecting you from, and what it needs in order to relax. At the center of this work is your Self — the wise, calm, connected core of who you are beneath all the protective layers.

IFS is particularly effective for women navigating trauma, eating disorders, perfectionism, and chronic self-criticism. It offers a way to heal internal conflicts without judgment, and to build a more integrated, compassionate relationship with all of who you are.

Somatic and Body-Based Approaches

Trauma, anxiety, and emotional pain do not live only in your mind. They live in your body — in tight shoulders, a clenched jaw, a racing heart, a stomach that cannot settle. Somatic therapy works with the body directly, helping you notice physical sensations, release stored tension, and rebuild a sense of safety in your own skin.

Somatic approaches recognize that your nervous system holds the imprint of everything you have been through. When your body stays stuck in survival mode — hypervigilant, bracing, or shut down — no amount of cognitive understanding will fully resolve what you are carrying. You have to help your body complete what it started. Somatic therapy provides the tools and the safety to do exactly that.

Body-based work can include breathwork, gentle movement, body scanning, grounding exercises, and guided awareness of physical sensation. It is never about pushing past your limits. It is about slowly, gently teaching your nervous system that it is safe to come back online.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy is one of the most widely researched and practiced therapy modalities. It focuses on the relationship between your thoughts, feelings, and behaviors, helping you identify unhelpful thinking patterns and develop more balanced, compassionate ways of understanding your experiences.

CBT can be incredibly useful for managing anxiety, depression, disordered eating patterns, and the negative self-talk that so many women carry. It offers practical tools — thought records, behavioral experiments, exposure techniques — that you can use between sessions to build new habits of mind.

That said, CBT works best when it is part of a larger, more integrative approach. For women who have experienced trauma, relational wounding, or deep emotional disconnection, cognitive tools alone may not be enough. When combined with somatic awareness, creative expression, and parts work, CBT becomes significantly more powerful — helping you think differently and feel differently in your body.

Mindfulness and Nervous System Regulation

Mindfulness is not about emptying your mind or achieving some perfect state of calm. In a therapeutic context, mindfulness is about building your capacity to be with what is happening inside you without being swept away by it. It is about noticing your thoughts, your emotions, and your bodily sensations with curiosity rather than judgment.

For women living with anxiety, trauma responses, or ADHD, mindfulness-based practices can be transformative. Learning to notice when your nervous system is activated — and having tools to gently bring yourself back to a place of regulation — changes your relationship with stress, emotional reactivity, and the overwhelm that has been running the show.

Nervous system regulation is woven throughout all of the work I do. It is not a separate modality but a foundational thread that supports everything else: the ability to feel safe enough to create, to explore your inner world, and to make meaningful changes in your life.

The RRR Method™: Remember. Return. Reclaim.

In my practice, I do not follow a single modality or a fixed formula. Every person who walks through the door is different — different history, different nervous system, different protective strategies, different creative language. What remains constant is the destination: helping you remember who you are beneath the stories, return to the wisdom of your body and authentic self, and reclaim the voice, relationships, boundaries, and life that have always belonged to you.

This is the heart of Embodied Reclamation — the philosophy that guides everything I do. It is built on one foundational belief: you were born with an innate, wise, creative, compassionate Self. Life experiences, attachment wounds, trauma, shame, and survival adaptations did not destroy that Self. They buried it. The work of therapy is not fixing something broken. It is remembering what was always there.

Remember

Before the people-pleasing. Before the perfectionism. Before the shrinking and the performing and the protecting. There was a version of you that existed before you learned you had to earn your place. This first step is about gently uncovering who that person is — the stories that shaped you, the parts of you that went quiet, and the adaptive strategies that once helped you survive but may now be keeping you stuck.

Return

Healing is not only intellectual. It is lived. Felt. Integrated. The body holds what the mind has forgotten, and the nervous system carries stories that words alone cannot reach. Return is the process of coming back into your body, your emotions, your creativity, and your inner knowing — building the kind of safety that allows your most protected parts to finally soften.

Reclaim

Reclamation is the ongoing, active process of stepping back into what has always been yours — your voice, your needs, your boundaries, your creativity, your agency, your authentic expression. Not a new self. Not a fixed self. The original self, the one that existed before shame convinced you that you were too much or not enough.

The modalities I draw from — expressive arts, IFS, somatic awareness, CBT, mindfulness — are not the framework. They are the instruments. What shifts from person to person and session to session is which combination of those instruments creates the right conditions for your healing to move. Like a symphony, the goal is not to use every instrument at once, but to know which ones this moment, this person, this nervous system is asking for.

How to Choose the Right Modality for You

If you are not sure which approach is right for you, that is completely okay. In fact, you do not need to choose. The best therapy is responsive, not prescriptive. A skilled therapist will draw from different modalities based on what you need in any given session, not force you into a single framework.

What matters most is that you feel safe, seen, and genuinely supported. The therapeutic relationship itself — the trust, the attunement, the consistency — is the most powerful predictor of healing, regardless of which modality is used. If you are curious about what integrative therapy looks like in practice, a free consultation is a wonderful place to start.

Frequently Asked Questions About Therapy Modalities

What is a healing modality?

A healing modality is a specific therapeutic approach or method used to support emotional, psychological, or physical healing. In mental health therapy, modalities include approaches like Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), Internal Family Systems (IFS), somatic therapy, expressive arts therapy, and mindfulness-based practices. Each modality has its own framework, techniques, and goals — and many therapists use an integrative approach that blends several modalities based on the client's needs.

What are the most common types of therapy modalities?

The most common therapy modalities include Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), which focuses on thought patterns and behaviors; Internal Family Systems (IFS), which works with inner "parts"; somatic therapy, which addresses how trauma and emotions are held in the body; expressive arts therapy, which uses creative processes for healing; EMDR, which processes traumatic memories; and mindfulness-based approaches that build present-moment awareness. Many therapists use an integrative approach that draws from multiple modalities.

What is body-centered therapy?

Body-centered therapy (also called somatic therapy or body-based therapy) is an approach that recognizes trauma, anxiety, and emotional pain are stored not just in the mind, but in the body itself. Rather than focusing only on thoughts and words, body-centered therapy attends to physical sensations, nervous system states, posture, and breathing. It helps people release what is held in the body and rebuild a sense of safety and groundedness.

How do I know which therapy modality is right for me?

You do not need to know which modality is right for you before starting therapy. A skilled therapist will assess your history, goals, and nervous system needs and draw from different approaches based on what you need. The most important factor is the therapeutic relationship itself — feeling safe and understood with your therapist. A free consultation is a great place to explore which approach might fit you.

What is expressive arts therapy and how is it different from regular art therapy?

Expressive arts therapy is a multimodal approach that uses multiple creative forms — visual art, movement, music, writing, and poetry — for therapeutic exploration and healing. It differs from art therapy in that it is not limited to visual art, and the goal is not to produce artwork but to use the creative process itself as a vehicle for accessing emotions, processing experiences, and building self-understanding. No artistic skill or experience is required.
