Somatic Therapy: What to Expect in Body-Based Healing

February 28, 2026

You have done the work. You have talked about what happened. You understand your patterns, your triggers, your family dynamics. You can explain your trauma with remarkable clarity. And yet — something in your body has not gotten the message. Your shoulders still climb toward your ears at the slightest tension. Your stomach still churns when a conversation gets difficult. You still freeze when you need to speak up, still hold your breath without realizing it, still carry a weight in your chest that no amount of insight seems to lift.

If this sounds familiar, it is not because your therapy has failed or because you have not tried hard enough. It is because trauma does not live only in your thoughts. It lives in your body. And until you address it there, some part of the healing puzzle will always feel incomplete. This is where somatic therapy comes in — not to replace what you have already done, but to reach the places that talk alone cannot go.

What Is Somatic Therapy?

Somatic therapy is a body-centered approach to healing that recognizes the inseparable connection between your physical experience and your emotional and psychological wellbeing. The word somatic comes from the Greek word soma, meaning body. At its foundation, somatic therapy is built on the understanding that traumatic experiences are not only recorded in your memory but are also stored in your muscles, your posture, your breathing patterns, your nervous system responses, and the subtle sensations that move through your body moment to moment.

When something overwhelming happens — whether it is a single traumatic event or years of chronic stress, neglect, or emotional harm — your nervous system responds by mobilizing survival energy. Your heart races, your muscles tense, your breath quickens or stops. In an ideal situation, once the danger passes, your body completes the survival response and returns to a state of regulation. But when trauma is too much, too fast, or too prolonged, or when you are too young or too unsupported to process what happened, that survival energy does not get discharged. It stays locked in your body, creating patterns of chronic tension, hypervigilance, numbness, pain, digestive issues, and a nervous system that remains stuck in a state of fight, flight, or freeze long after the original threat is gone.

Somatic therapy works by gently helping your body complete what it could not finish at the time of the traumatic experience. It is not about reliving trauma or analyzing your past from a cognitive perspective. It is about creating the conditions for your nervous system to release what it has been holding, at a pace that feels safe rather than overwhelming. For a deeper understanding of how trauma affects the nervous system, you can explore the anxiety and nervous system resource page.

How Trauma Gets Stored in the Body

To understand why somatic therapy works, it helps to understand how trauma gets lodged in the body in the first place. Your nervous system has two primary modes: the sympathetic nervous system, which activates your fight-or-flight response, and the parasympathetic nervous system, which governs rest, digestion, and recovery. In a healthy, regulated system, these two modes work together in a fluid dance, activating and deactivating as needed.

When you experience something traumatic, your sympathetic nervous system floods your body with stress hormones. Your muscles brace for impact. Your breath becomes shallow or held. Your digestive system shuts down. Your entire body organizes itself around one goal: survive this moment. If you are able to fight or flee — if you can physically move through the survival response — the energy is discharged and your body returns to baseline.

But so often, especially for women and children, fighting or fleeing is not possible. You could not fight back because the threat was someone you depended on. You could not run because you had nowhere to go. You could not even scream because you learned that making noise would make things worse. So your body did the next best thing: it froze. It collapsed. It held all of that survival energy inside and waited for a moment of safety that may not have come for years, if it came at all.

That undischarged energy is what creates the physical symptoms that so many trauma survivors live with daily. The chronic tension that no massage can fully release. The startle response that fires at the slightest unexpected sound. The digestive issues that no medical test can explain. The feeling of being permanently braced for impact. The exhaustion that comes from a nervous system that never fully powers down. These are not separate problems from your emotional pain. They are the body’s way of holding the story your mind may or may not remember.

What a Somatic Therapy Session Actually Looks Like

One of the biggest barriers to trying somatic therapy is not knowing what to expect. If you have only experienced traditional talk therapy, the idea of a body-focused session can feel mysterious or even intimidating. So let me walk you through what a session might actually look like.

You will likely begin sitting in a chair or on a couch, much like a traditional therapy session. Your therapist will check in with you about how you are feeling, what has been on your mind, and what is showing up for you today. This part may feel familiar. The difference comes in where the conversation goes from there.

Rather than focusing primarily on the content of your thoughts or the details of your story, your therapist will begin to draw your attention to your body. They might ask questions like: Where do you notice that feeling in your body? What does the tension in your chest feel like? If that knot in your stomach had a shape or a color, what would it be? Can you stay with that sensation for a moment and see what happens?

This is the practice of body awareness, and it is the foundation of somatic work. You are learning to track the subtle sensations that move through your body — the tightness, the warmth, the tingling, the heaviness, the hollowness — and to stay present with them instead of immediately jumping into analysis, avoidance, or action.

Your therapist will also pay close attention to your nervous system state. They are tracking whether your system is activated (sympathetic arousal — anxiety, agitation, racing thoughts), shut down (dorsal vagal collapse — numbness, fatigue, disconnection), or in the window of tolerance where processing can happen. Their job is to help you stay within that window, guiding you to titrate the intensity of what you are feeling so that it can be processed without overwhelming you.

A session might include:

  • Breathwork — not the intense, forced breathing you might associate with certain wellness trends, but gentle, regulated breathing that signals safety to your nervous system
  • Grounding exercises — feeling your feet on the floor, the support of the chair beneath you, the weight of your body, the contact between your hands and your legs
  • Tracking sensations — noticing and naming physical experiences as they arise without trying to change or interpret them
  • Pendulation — moving your attention between areas of discomfort and areas of the body that feel neutral or safe, teaching your nervous system that distress is not the whole story
  • Mindful movement — small, slow, intentional movements that allow your body to complete defensive responses it could not finish during the traumatic experience, such as pushing away, turning, or reaching out
  • Resource building — identifying and strengthening the internal and external resources that help you feel safe, grounded, and connected

There is no script. There is no formula. Each session is guided by what your body is showing in that moment, which means the work is always relevant, always alive, and always meeting you exactly where you are.

The Nervous System and the Window of Tolerance

A concept that comes up frequently in somatic therapy is the window of tolerance — the zone in which your nervous system can experience activation (stress, emotion, sensation) without becoming overwhelmed or shutting down. When you are within your window of tolerance, you can feel difficult things and still maintain a sense of groundedness, presence, and choice. When you are pushed outside of it, you tip into either hyperarousal (panic, anxiety, reactivity) or hypoarousal (numbness, dissociation, collapse).

For many trauma survivors, the window of tolerance has become very narrow. It does not take much to push you from okay to overwhelmed, or from functioning to shut down. This is not a weakness. It is the direct result of a nervous system that spent too long in survival mode and never had the opportunity to recalibrate. Your system learned that the world is dangerous and that staying on high alert or staying numb is the safest strategy.

Somatic therapy works by gradually expanding your window of tolerance. Each session offers your nervous system a small, manageable dose of activation followed by a return to regulation. Over time, this teaches your body something it may not have learned before: that you can feel something intense and survive it. That activation can rise and then fall. That your body is capable of moving through distress and coming back to calm. This is what it means to build resilience at the nervous system level, and it changes everything about how you experience daily life.

How Somatic Therapy Differs from Talk Therapy

If you have spent time in traditional talk therapy, you may have experienced the frustration of understanding your trauma perfectly without feeling any less affected by it. This is not a failure of talk therapy. It is a reflection of the fact that trauma is stored in a part of the brain that does not respond to language and logic the way your thinking mind does.

The parts of the brain most involved in trauma processing — the amygdala, which processes threat, and the brainstem, which manages autonomic responses like heart rate and breathing — operate below the level of conscious thought and language. They do not respond to reasoning, insight, or reframing. They respond to experience. To sensation. To the felt sense of safety or danger in your body.

This is why you can know intellectually that you are safe and still feel terrified. Your thinking brain has the information. But your survival brain has not been updated. Somatic therapy speaks the language of the survival brain. It communicates through sensation, breath, movement, and presence rather than through words and analysis. This is not to say that talk is irrelevant in somatic therapy — you will still speak, share, and make meaning of your experiences. But the primary vehicle for change is your body, not your thoughts. Understanding how cognitive approaches differ from trauma therapy can help clarify when each is most useful.

Somatic Therapy and Other Healing Modalities

Somatic therapy is powerful on its own, and it also combines beautifully with other trauma-informed approaches. When paired with Internal Family Systems (IFS) therapy, for example, somatic awareness becomes a way to access and communicate with different parts of your internal system. A protector part might show up as tension in your jaw. An exile part might live as a hollow ache in your chest. The body becomes a map to your inner world, and somatic skills help you navigate it.

Expressive arts therapy is another natural partner. Movement, art-making, and creative expression all engage the body and bypass the thinking mind, making them ideal vehicles for accessing and releasing what words cannot reach. When you paint what your anxiety looks like or move the way your body wants to move when it feels safe, you are doing somatic work through a creative lens. The combination of somatic awareness and creative expression often reaches places that neither approach could access alone.

Many therapists, particularly those who practice trauma-informed therapy, weave somatic elements into their work alongside other modalities. You do not necessarily need to choose between approaches. The most effective healing often happens when multiple modalities work together, addressing different layers of the trauma simultaneously. If you are exploring your options, the guide to choosing a therapy modality can help you think through what might be the best fit.

Who Benefits from Somatic Therapy

Somatic therapy can be helpful for anyone, but it is particularly powerful for people who:

  • Have experienced trauma that has not fully resolved through talk therapy alone
  • Live with chronic physical symptoms — tension, pain, digestive issues, headaches — that do not have a clear medical explanation
  • Experience anxiety as a body experience rather than just a thinking experience — racing heart, shallow breathing, tightness in the chest, a constant sense of being on edge
  • Feel disconnected from their bodies or struggle with numbness, dissociation, or feeling like they are living from the neck up
  • Struggle with the fawn response, people-pleasing, or other nervous system-driven patterns that feel automatic and hard to change
  • Have a history of developmental or relational trauma, where the harm was chronic and relational rather than a single event
  • Want to build more capacity for emotional regulation, stress tolerance, and presence in their daily lives

Somatic therapy is also particularly well-suited for women, whose bodies so often become the site where cultural pressure, relational trauma, and emotional pain are stored. If you have spent your life being told to be smaller, quieter, more accommodating — if your body has become something to manage, ignore, or override rather than inhabit — somatic therapy offers an invitation to come home to yourself in the most literal sense.

What Healing Through the Body Feels Like

Healing through somatic therapy is often described as a process of thawing. Where once there was rigidity — in your muscles, in your breathing, in the way you moved through the world — there begins to be fluidity. Where once there was numbness, there begins to be sensation. Where once there was a constant hum of anxiety, there begins to be moments of genuine calm, not the forced calm of trying to relax, but the organic calm that comes from a nervous system that has finally learned it is safe to let go.

You might notice that you begin to breathe more deeply without trying. That your shoulders drop away from your ears. That you can feel your feet on the ground when you walk. That you can tolerate a difficult conversation without your whole body clenching. That emotions come and go more fluidly instead of either overwhelming you or being completely absent.

Some people experience moments of release that are physical — shaking, trembling, tears, heat, or a wave of energy moving through the body. These are normal and healthy responses. They are signs that your body is discharging the survival energy it has been holding, sometimes for years or decades. Your therapist will help you understand and welcome these experiences as evidence that your body is doing exactly what it was designed to do — heal.

Over time, the changes that begin in your body ripple outward into every area of your life. You may find that your relationships feel different when you are no longer operating from a state of chronic tension. You may notice that you can make decisions more clearly when your body is not flooded with anxiety. You may discover parts of yourself — creativity, playfulness, sensuality, spontaneity — that have been dormant under layers of protective armoring. As one writer on healing put it, rest becomes a form of restoration rather than simply the absence of productivity.

Your body is not the enemy. It is not broken. It is not something to be overridden or ignored. Your body is the record of everything you have survived, and it is also the pathway to everything you can become. Somatic therapy offers you the tools to read that record with compassion and to write the next chapter from a place of wholeness rather than survival.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I have to be touched during somatic therapy?

No, touch is not a required part of somatic therapy. Many somatic therapists work entirely without physical contact, guiding you through body awareness exercises, breathwork, and mindful movement using only verbal cues. Some practitioners may offer light, supportive touch with your explicit consent, but this is always optional and you are always in control of whether touch is part of your sessions. If touch feels uncomfortable or unsafe for any reason, your therapist will respect that boundary completely.

What if I feel disconnected from my body or cannot feel sensations?

Feeling disconnected from your body is actually one of the most common reasons people seek somatic therapy, and it is a completely valid starting point. Disconnection from the body is often a protective response that developed to help you cope with overwhelming experiences. Your therapist will not push you to feel more than you are ready for. Instead, the work begins wherever you are, even if that means starting with noticing something as simple as the feeling of your feet on the floor. Over time, your capacity to sense and inhabit your body grows naturally.

How is somatic therapy different from yoga or meditation?

While somatic therapy shares some elements with yoga and meditation, such as body awareness and breathwork, it is a clinical therapeutic modality practiced by a trained mental health professional. Unlike yoga or meditation classes, somatic therapy is specifically designed to process and resolve trauma that is stored in the nervous system. Your therapist tracks your nervous system responses in real time and guides the process based on what your body needs in that moment. Yoga and meditation can be wonderful complements to somatic therapy, but they are not substitutes for the relational, trauma-informed clinical work that happens in session.
