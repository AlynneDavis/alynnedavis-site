Somatic Therapy for Trauma: What to Expect

February 28, 2026

You have probably tried to think your way out of what you are feeling. You have analyzed, journaled, talked about your past, and tried to understand why you react the way you do. Maybe some of that helped. But there is a part of your experience that understanding alone cannot seem to reach — a tightness in your chest that will not let go, a restlessness in your body that no amount of reasoning can settle, a sense of being stuck that no amount of insight seems to release.

That is because trauma does not live only in your thoughts. It lives in your body. And healing it requires meeting it where it actually is.

Somatic therapy is a body-based approach to healing that works with the physical sensations, movement patterns, and nervous system responses that trauma leaves behind. If you have been wondering what somatic therapy is, how it works, and what it actually feels like in a session, this guide will walk you through everything you need to know.

Why Trauma Lives in the Body

When you experience something overwhelming, your nervous system activates a cascade of survival responses — the impulse to fight, flee, freeze, or fawn. These are not conscious decisions. They are automatic, biological programs designed to protect you from danger. Your heart rate spikes, your muscles tense, your breathing quickens, and your body prepares to take action.

In an ideal scenario, the threat passes, and your body completes the survival response. The energy that mobilized for fight or flight discharges naturally — through shaking, crying, deep breathing, or physical movement — and your nervous system returns to a state of equilibrium. But trauma happens when this cycle is interrupted. When you cannot fight, when you cannot run, when the threat is ongoing, when there is no safe person to co-regulate with afterward, the survival energy gets trapped in your body. Your nervous system stays locked in a state of alarm, or it shuts down entirely to protect you from overwhelm.

This is why you can understand intellectually that you are safe now and still feel terrified. This is why you can know that the past is behind you and still feel your body bracing for impact. The thinking brain has processed the timeline, but the body has not received the update. It is still responding as if the threat is happening right now.

Somatic therapy addresses this gap. It works directly with the body’s stored responses, helping your nervous system complete what it could not complete at the time of the trauma and gradually return to a state of regulation and ease.

What “Somatic” Actually Means

The word somatic comes from the Greek word soma, meaning “body.” In the context of therapy, somatic refers to any approach that works with the body’s lived experience — sensations, impulses, posture, breath, movement, tension patterns, and the autonomic nervous system responses that underlie your emotional and psychological experience.

Somatic therapy is not a single technique. It is a category that includes several related approaches, all of which share the fundamental premise that the body is not separate from the mind, and that healing trauma requires engaging the body directly. Some of the most widely practiced somatic approaches include:

  • Somatic Experiencing (SE), developed by Peter Levine, which focuses on tracking body sensations and supporting the nervous system in discharging trapped survival energy
  • Sensorimotor Psychotherapy, which integrates body awareness with cognitive and emotional processing
  • Somatic-informed expressive arts therapy, which uses creative processes like movement, art, and music to access and express what the body holds
  • Breathwork, which uses specific breathing patterns to shift nervous system states and release tension
  • Body-based mindfulness practices, which build your capacity to notice and be present with physical sensations without becoming overwhelmed

What all of these approaches share is a deep respect for the body’s intelligence. Your body is not malfunctioning when it carries tension, numbness, or reactivity. It is communicating. Somatic therapy teaches you to listen.

What a Somatic Therapy Session Feels Like

If you have never experienced somatic therapy, you might be wondering what actually happens in a session. The answer is that it can look quite different depending on your therapist’s specific training and what your nervous system needs on a given day. But here is a general sense of what you might experience.

Your therapist will likely begin by checking in with you — not just about your thoughts and emotions, but about what is happening in your body right now. They might ask questions like:

  • “What do you notice in your body as you settle into this space?”
  • “Where do you feel the most tension or holding right now?”
  • “What does your breathing feel like today?”
  • “Is there a place in your body that feels calm or neutral?”

These questions are invitations to turn your attention inward, to begin the practice of noticing. You do not need to have precise or poetic answers. You might say, “My shoulders feel tight,” or, “I notice a heaviness in my stomach,” or even, “I do not really feel anything.” All of those are valid starting points.

As the session unfolds, your therapist might guide you to stay with a particular sensation — not to analyze it or try to change it, but simply to observe it with curiosity. What happens when you bring your attention to that tightness in your chest? Does it shift, expand, move, intensify, or soften? This process of tracking sensations is one of the cornerstones of somatic work, and it can reveal things that talking alone might never reach.

You might notice that as you stay with a sensation, emotions arise. A feeling of sadness washes through. A memory surfaces. A wave of anger or grief moves through your body. Your therapist will support you in staying present with whatever arises, using grounding techniques and pacing to make sure you stay within your window of tolerance — the zone where you can feel without becoming overwhelmed.

The Nervous System and Regulation

At the center of somatic therapy is the nervous system, specifically the autonomic nervous system (ANS), which governs your body’s involuntary responses to safety and threat. Understanding the basics of how your nervous system works can make the somatic therapy experience feel much less mysterious.

Your ANS has two primary branches:

The sympathetic nervous system activates your fight-or-flight response. When you perceive danger, this system speeds up your heart rate, tenses your muscles, sharpens your alertness, and prepares you to take action. In a trauma response, this system can get stuck in the “on” position, leaving you in a chronic state of anxiety, hypervigilance, irritability, or restlessness.

The parasympathetic nervous system has two branches of its own. The ventral vagal system supports feelings of safety, connection, and calm. The dorsal vagal system activates the freeze or shutdown response when the threat becomes too overwhelming for fight or flight. In a trauma response, the dorsal vagal system can get stuck, leaving you feeling numb, dissociated, exhausted, or disconnected from your body and from others.

Somatic therapy helps you learn to recognize which state your nervous system is in at any given moment and gradually expand your capacity to move between states with more flexibility. Instead of being trapped in hyperarousal or shutdown, you learn to find your way back to the ventral vagal state — the place where you feel present, grounded, and capable of connection.

This is not about willpower. It is about building new neural pathways through repeated, gentle, body-based experiences of safety. Each time your nervous system completes a stress cycle, each time you practice dropping into calm from a place of activation, each time you notice sensation without being hijacked by it, you are literally rewiring your brain and body’s default responses.

What Somatic Discharge Looks Like

One of the concepts that often comes up in somatic therapy is the idea of discharge — the process by which your body releases the survival energy that got stuck during a traumatic experience. Discharge is what your body would have done naturally if the trauma cycle had been allowed to complete.

Discharge can look different for different people and at different times. You might experience:

  • Trembling, shaking, or vibrating — especially in the legs, arms, or core
  • A deep, involuntary sigh or yawn
  • A sensation of warmth or tingling spreading through your body
  • Spontaneous tears that may or may not feel connected to a specific emotion
  • A rumbling or gurgling in your stomach or digestive system
  • A sudden wave of emotion — grief, anger, relief — that moves through and passes
  • An urge to move — to push, reach, run, or curl up
  • A feeling of lightness, openness, or spaciousness after a period of intensity

These responses are not signs that something is going wrong. They are signs that your body is doing exactly what it needs to do. Your nervous system is completing an interrupted survival cycle, releasing the energy that has been held in your tissues, and returning to a state of balance.

If this sounds intense, please know that a skilled somatic therapist will pace this work carefully. Discharge happens gradually, in doses your nervous system can handle. You are never forced to go faster than your body is ready for. The goal is not catharsis — it is regulation.

Somatic Therapy and Other Modalities

Somatic approaches do not have to stand alone. They are beautifully complementary to other forms of trauma therapy, including Internal Family Systems (IFS), EMDR, and expressive arts therapy.

When somatic awareness is combined with IFS, it allows you to not only communicate with the different parts of your inner system but to feel where those parts live in your body. You might discover that your people-pleasing part shows up as a tightness in your throat, or that your inner critic creates a heaviness in your chest. Working with parts somatically can deepen the healing process and help those protective parts feel truly heard.

When combined with expressive arts therapy, somatic work becomes even more expansive. Movement, sound, art-making, and other creative processes give your body additional channels for expression and release. The arts can access preverbal trauma — experiences from early childhood that were encoded in the body before language was available — in ways that words alone cannot.

Many trauma therapists integrate somatic principles throughout their work, even when they are not practicing a strictly somatic modality. Paying attention to the body, tracking the nervous system, and supporting regulation are hallmarks of good trauma-informed care across all approaches.

What Somatic Therapy Can Help With

While somatic therapy is particularly powerful for trauma, its applications extend to many areas of struggle:

  • Chronic anxiety — learning to downregulate your nervous system instead of just managing anxious thoughts
  • Dissociation and numbness — gently rebuilding your connection to your body and to the present moment
  • Chronic pain and tension — understanding the emotional stories your body is holding and creating space for release
  • Disordered eating — reconnecting with hunger, fullness, and body cues that trauma may have disrupted. The relationship between childhood trauma and eating disorders is deeply rooted in the body.
  • Relationship difficulties — noticing how your body responds in relational dynamics and building the capacity for safe intimacy
  • Sleep problems — teaching your nervous system that it is safe enough to rest
  • Burnout and exhaustion — understanding why your body is depleted and learning to restore through rest rather than just pushing through

Coming Home to Your Body

For many women who have experienced trauma, the body has been a battlefield, a source of pain, or a place you learned to leave. Coming back into your body can feel vulnerable, unfamiliar, and even frightening at first. That is a perfectly reasonable response. Your disconnection from your body was a form of protection, and somatic therapy honors that.

But your body is also a source of wisdom, pleasure, intuition, and aliveness. It holds not only your pain but also your capacity for joy, for connection, for knowing what is true for you. When you learn to listen to it again — gently, at your own pace, in the company of someone who makes it safe to feel — something fundamental shifts. You stop living from the neck up. You stop running from your own physical experience. You start to inhabit your life more fully.

This is not a quick fix. Somatic healing unfolds over time, layer by layer, at the pace your nervous system can tolerate. If you are curious about how long trauma therapy takes, the answer is deeply personal. But every moment you spend learning to be present in your own body is a moment of reclamation. It is a moment of saying, This body is mine, and I am allowed to be here.

You do not have to be an expert in body awareness to start somatic therapy. You do not have to be flexible, athletic, or good at meditation. You just have to be willing to show up, to notice what you notice, and to trust that your body already knows the way. Your therapist will meet you wherever you are.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does somatic therapy involve being touched?

Not necessarily. Many forms of somatic therapy are entirely talk-based, with the therapist guiding your awareness to body sensations through verbal cues and questions. Some somatic practitioners do use gentle, consensual touch as part of the work, but this is never required and is always discussed and agreed upon beforehand. You are always in control of what feels comfortable.

What if I cannot feel anything in my body?

This is very common, especially for people who have experienced trauma. Numbness, disconnection, and difficulty sensing the body are all normal protective responses. Somatic therapy does not require you to have an immediate or dramatic body awareness. The work begins wherever you are, even if that means starting with the simple observation that you cannot feel much at all. Over time, as your nervous system begins to feel safe, sensation gradually returns.

Can somatic therapy help with anxiety and not just trauma?

Absolutely. Anxiety is a nervous system state, and somatic therapy is fundamentally about working with the nervous system. Many people who come in with anxiety find that somatic approaches help them understand and regulate their stress responses in a way that cognitive strategies alone could not. You can explore more about this connection on the anxiety and nervous system resource page.
