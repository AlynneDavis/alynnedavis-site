You Don’t Have to Keep Fighting This Alone

If you’ve ever noticed that stress and your relationship with food seem to be in constant conversation with each other — you’re not imagining it. Food is one of the ways our bodies and minds try to manage what’s too much to hold.

I’m Alynne Davis, a licensed therapist in Charlotte, North Carolina, and I specialize in helping women untangle their relationship with food, their bodies, and themselves. The women I work with are often deeply self-aware, capable, and quietly exhausted by the war they’ve been waging against themselves at every meal — sometimes for decades.

As an eating disorder therapist in Charlotte, NC, my work goes beyond behavior change. We look at why food took on the role it did in your life — and we build something more sustainable in its place.

Who This Is For

You might be looking for an eating disorder therapist near me or binge eating help in Charlotte if any of this sounds familiar:

  • You feel out of control around food, even when you desperately want to stop
  • You eat to manage emotions — stress, loneliness, boredom, anxiety — and then feel shame
  • You’ve tried every diet, food plan, or reset. The cycle keeps starting over.
  • Your relationship with food feels all-or-nothing: perfectly controlled or completely off the rails
  • You think about food and your body constantly, and it’s exhausting
  • You’ve been told you don’t look like you have an eating disorder — but something still feels deeply wrong
  • Food is tied to anxiety, old wounds, perfectionism, or trauma you haven’t fully dealt with
  • You suspect ADHD may be playing a role in how you eat — and no one has ever addressed that

Eating disorders and disordered eating don’t always look the way they do in textbooks. They often look like a high-functioning woman who holds everything together — and quietly falls apart alone.

How I Help

I work with women experiencing:

Binge Eating and Emotional Eating

Binge eating often develops as a way to manage stress, numb difficult emotions, or cope with restriction. We work to understand the cycle, address what’s driving it, and develop a more attuned, compassionate relationship with food and hunger — without more rules.

Body Image Distress

If you spend meaningful time criticizing what you see in the mirror, avoiding situations because of how you feel in your body, or measuring your worth in weight and shape — this isn’t just “normal insecurity.” It’s worth exploring with someone who understands how deep this goes.

Restriction, Orthorexia, and Food Rules

When healthy eating becomes rigid, all-consuming, and frightening to deviate from, it stops being about health. I help women loosen the grip of food rules and reconnect with a more flexible, trusting way of nourishing themselves.

Eating Concerns Connected to Trauma or ADHD

Many women are surprised to learn how much trauma and ADHD shape their relationship with food. Both can create patterns of emotional eating, impulsive eating, restriction, or disconnection from hunger cues — and both deserve to be addressed directly in treatment. Read more about how ADHD impacts eating in women and the connection between trauma and eating disorders.

Disordered Eating That Doesn’t Have a Name

You don’t need a diagnosis to deserve support. If your relationship with food is causing real suffering — that’s enough.

What Makes This Work Different

A lot of eating disorder treatment focuses on behavior: what you’re eating, how much, when. That matters — but it’s rarely where the healing lives.

The women I work with have usually already tried the behavioral approaches. They know what to do. What they haven’t had space to explore is the why — the emotional history, the nervous system patterns, the unmet needs that food has been quietly trying to fill.

My work goes there.

Expressive Arts Therapy

Words alone aren’t always enough — especially when the roots of eating patterns live in the body and in emotion, not just in thought. I use creative tools including writing, visual art, imagery, and movement to help you access what talk therapy can’t always reach. This isn’t about making art. It’s about giving your inner world a different way to speak.

Nervous System Regulation

Eating disorders are often nervous system stories — patterns that developed when the body needed to regulate, soothe, or protect itself. I help you understand your nervous system’s rhythms, recognize when you’re dysregulated, and build real tools to come back to yourself before food becomes the only option.

Self-Trust Work

Most women who struggle with food have stopped trusting themselves — their hunger, their fullness, their desires, their instincts. Rebuilding that trust is at the center of this work. Not rules. Not willpower. Trust.

Trauma-Informed Care

I understand that eating disorders and disordered eating are almost always connected to larger experiences — childhood dynamics, relational wounds, trauma, or a nervous system that learned early that it wasn’t safe to have needs. My work honors that complexity.

What Eating Disorder Therapy Looks Like With Me

Your First Session

Your first session is a space to tell your story. I’ll ask questions, listen closely, and we’ll begin to get a sense of what’s been happening and what you’re hoping for. There’s no assessment checklist. It’s a conversation.

Weekly Sessions

Sessions are 50 minutes, typically weekly. We move at a pace that feels safe — not so slow that nothing shifts, but not so fast that you feel flooded. This work asks something real of you, and I want the container to feel steady enough to hold it.

In Person & Telehealth

I practice in Charlotte, NC and offer telehealth to clients throughout North Carolina and South Carolina. Many women find telehealth actually creates more safety for this kind of work — you’re in your own space, and there’s no commute after a hard session.

Free 15-Minute Consultation

A free consultation is available before you commit. It’s a chance to ask questions, share a bit about where you are, and see if we feel like a good fit. No pressure, no forms to fill out. Just a conversation.
