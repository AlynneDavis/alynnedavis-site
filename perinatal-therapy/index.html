Perinatal Therapy in Charlotte, NC — Prenatal & Postpartum Support

The perinatal period — from pregnancy through the first year and beyond — is one of the most transformative, vulnerable, and underserved seasons of a woman’s life. Whether you are carrying anxiety before birth, struggling after your baby arrives, or somewhere in between, you do not have to navigate this alone. Support is available before, during, and after delivery.

Perinatal Therapy Covers the Full Arc — Before and After Birth

The word perinatal refers to the period surrounding birth: conception, pregnancy, birth itself, and the postpartum months that follow. Perinatal therapy addresses mental health across this entire span, not just after delivery.

Many people think of maternal mental health support as something you seek if things go wrong after the baby arrives. But the research is clear: beginning support before birth is often one of the most protective things you can do. Prenatal anxiety and depression are common, frequently unrecognized, and respond well to therapy. And building a therapeutic relationship during pregnancy means that if the postpartum period becomes difficult, you already have support in place — not scrambling to find it while sleep-deprived and overwhelmed.

Whether you are pregnant and struggling, newly postpartum, months into the postpartum period and still not feeling like yourself, or simply wanting to get a support structure in place before your baby comes — this work is available to you.

This Is Far More Common Than You May Have Been Told

Postpartum depression affects approximately 1 in 5 new mothers — and that is likely an undercount, given how many women never disclose what they are experiencing. Postpartum anxiety may be even more prevalent, affecting up to 15–20% of new mothers, and often goes unrecognized because it looks less like sadness and more like hypervigilance, racing thoughts, or the inability to stop worrying even when everything appears fine.

Prenatal depression and anxiety are equally underacknowledged — affecting an estimated 1 in 5 pregnant women — and are among the strongest predictors of postpartum mood disorders. What happens during pregnancy matters for what happens after birth.

Together, perinatal mood and anxiety disorders are among the most common complications of pregnancy and childbirth. They are not a reflection of how much you love your baby. They are not a character flaw. They are a physiological, psychological, and relational response to one of the most destabilizing transitions a human being can go through.

If you are in the thick of this right now — or trying to get ahead of it — you are not alone. And you do not have to keep holding it by yourself.

What Postpartum Depression and Anxiety Actually Feel Like

The way postpartum mood disorders are portrayed — a mother who cannot bond with her baby, visibly breaking down — is only one version of this experience. Many women are moving through it silently, still functioning, still caring for their child, while carrying something heavy no one around them can see.

Postpartum Depression May Look Like

  • Persistent sadness, emptiness, or feeling nothing at all
  • Crying more than you expected — or not being able to cry even when you want to
  • Feeling disconnected from your baby, or from yourself
  • Exhaustion that goes far beyond sleep deprivation
  • Losing interest in things that used to matter to you
  • Feeling like a failure, or like your baby deserves a better mother
  • Withdrawing from your partner, friends, or family
  • Intrusive or frightening thoughts that feel foreign and distressing
  • Difficulty making decisions or concentrating

Postpartum Anxiety May Look Like

  • Racing, relentless thoughts you cannot turn off
  • Constant worry about your baby’s safety — even when nothing is wrong
  • Inability to sleep even when the baby is sleeping
  • Heart racing, chest tightness, or physical tension
  • Feeling like something terrible is about to happen
  • Needing to check on the baby repeatedly through the night
  • Difficulty letting anyone else care for your child
  • Irritability, snapping, or rage that surprises you

Many new mothers experience both simultaneously. And many experience symptoms for months before reaching out — because they are ashamed, because they were told it would pass, or because no one asked.

Postpartum Depression Does Not Always Arrive Right Away — or Leave Quickly

One of the most important things to understand about postpartum depression is that it does not follow a predictable schedule. While it often develops in the first few months after birth, it can emerge or intensify at 6, 9, or even 12 months postpartum — sometimes coinciding with a return to work, weaning, or the ongoing accumulation of sleep deprivation and relational stress.

Clinically, postpartum depression is increasingly recognized within a 12–18 month window after birth. The sustained stressors of new parenthood do not end at six weeks. Neither does the vulnerability. Research supports what many mothers have long known: the postpartum period is long, and so is the window in which support is needed and effective.

Without treatment, postpartum depression can persist for a year or more. With support, most people experience meaningful improvement. The earlier support begins, the better — but it is never too late to seek help.

What Makes Some Women More Vulnerable

Postpartum depression and anxiety can develop in anyone, but certain experiences significantly increase the risk. Understanding these factors is not about blame — it is about recognizing where support is most needed, and building it proactively when possible.

Pre-Existing Mental Health History

A personal or family history of depression, anxiety, PTSD, OCD, or other mental health conditions is one of the strongest predictors of postpartum mood disorders. If you have navigated mental health challenges before, the neurobiological and hormonal upheaval of the postpartum period can reactivate or intensify those patterns. This does not mean postpartum depression is inevitable — but it does mean having a therapist and a support plan in place before or immediately after birth is genuinely protective.

Traumatic or Difficult Birth Experiences

A birth that felt frightening, out of control, medically complicated, or different from what you hoped for can leave a real psychological impact — one that is often minimized or dismissed with “but the baby is healthy.” Emergency interventions, prolonged labor, unexpected C-sections, NICU stays, significant physical recovery, or birth experiences that felt violating or dehumanizing can contribute to postpartum anxiety and PTSD alongside or instead of postpartum depression. Your birth experience matters. What happened to you matters.

Sleep Deprivation

Sleep deprivation is not just exhausting — it is neurologically destabilizing. Chronic sleep loss directly impairs emotional regulation, increases reactivity, lowers resilience, and is a known contributor to depression and anxiety. New parenthood routinely involves a level of sleep deprivation that would be considered extreme in any other context. This is a physiological stressor, not a weakness, and it deserves to be taken seriously.

The Transition to Parenthood With a Partner

Research consistently shows that relationship satisfaction often declines significantly in the first year after having a baby. The division of labor, changing roles, differences in parenting approaches, shifts in intimacy and connection, and the grief of losing a previous version of your relationship all create real strain. Feeling alone in a partnership — especially while already sleep-deprived and hormonally dysregulated — is a meaningful risk factor for postpartum depression.

Lack of Support and Social Isolation

Humans are not meant to raise children in isolation. The loss of extended community, geographic distance from family, limited parental leave, financial stress, and the cultural expectation that mothers simply “figure it out” creates conditions that make postpartum mood disorders far more likely. Feeling alone with a newborn — even when surrounded by people — is one of the most common experiences new mothers describe.

Previous Pregnancy Loss or Infertility

Grief from pregnancy loss, miscarriage, or a long fertility journey does not disappear when a baby arrives. In some cases it intensifies — complicated by relief, guilt, hypervigilance about the baby’s health, and the difficulty of holding both joy and grief at the same time.

You Don’t Have to Wait Until After Birth

Perinatal mental health care does not begin after delivery — it can begin the moment you discover you are pregnant, or even before. Prenatal therapy is one of the most effective investments you can make in your postpartum mental health, because it puts support in place before the stress peaks.

Prenatal Anxiety and Depression Are Real

Pregnancy is often portrayed as a time of joy and anticipation. For many women, it is also a time of significant anxiety, ambivalence, fear, grief, or depression — especially for those with a history of mental health struggles, pregnancy loss, trauma, or complicated feelings about becoming a parent. Prenatal depression and anxiety affect an estimated 1 in 5 pregnant women and are among the strongest predictors of postpartum mood disorders. You deserve support during pregnancy, not only after.

Building a Foundation Before Birth

Starting therapy during pregnancy means that when the postpartum period arrives — with its sleep deprivation, identity upheaval, and relational strain — you already have a therapeutic relationship, coping tools, and a plan. You are not starting from scratch in your most depleted state. For women who know they carry risk factors for postpartum mood disorders, prenatal therapy is not just helpful. It is often protective in a meaningful clinical sense.

Processing What Pregnancy Brings Up

Pregnancy can bring up things you did not expect: unresolved feelings about your own childhood, complicated dynamics with your mother or family of origin, fears about repeating patterns, anxiety about your body, grief, or a shift in how you see your relationship and your sense of self. Prenatal therapy makes space for all of it, without judgment and without rushing past it.

Birth Preparation Beyond the Physical

Childbirth education addresses the physical process of labor and delivery. Prenatal therapy addresses the emotional and psychological preparation: processing fears about birth, developing self-advocacy skills, preparing for the identity shift of becoming a parent, and building the relational foundation that will carry you through the postpartum months. These are not extras. They are part of genuinely preparing for what is ahead.

Partners Experience This Too

Postpartum depression is not only a mother’s experience. Research shows that approximately 1 in 10 new fathers and non-birthing partners experience postpartum depression, with some studies reporting rates as high as 25%. Partners face many of the same stressors — sleep deprivation, identity shift, financial pressure, relationship strain — without the same cultural acknowledgment or access to support.

Paternal and partner postpartum depression often looks different than what we typically picture. It may present as:

  • Increased irritability, anger, or emotional withdrawal
  • Throwing themselves into work or other distractions
  • Increased alcohol or substance use
  • Feeling like they are failing or not enough
  • Disconnection from the baby or their partner
  • Anxiety or physical symptoms without an obvious cause

If you are a partner who is struggling, or if you are a mother whose partner seems to be — this is real, it is common, and support is available for everyone in the family.

How I Support Perinatal Mental Health

My approach to perinatal therapy is trauma-informed, relational, and grounded in the reality of what pregnancy, birth, and new parenthood actually demand. I do not believe in pushing through or performing wellness. I believe in creating a space where you can be honest about how hard this is, and where real healing becomes possible — before birth and after.

Trauma-Informed Care

If your birth experience was frightening, traumatic, or not what you expected, we make space for that. Birth trauma is real and underacknowledged, and it often sits underneath postpartum depression and anxiety in ways that standard treatment misses. We work with what actually happened — not just the symptoms it left behind.

Nervous System Regulation

Perinatal mood disorders are not just emotional — they are physiological. Chronic sleep deprivation, hormonal shifts, and the demands of caregiving create a nervous system that is running on empty or in a state of constant alert. We build practical tools for regulation: ways to move out of the alarm response and back into a window where you can actually function, rest, and connect.

Expressive Arts Therapy

Sometimes what is happening in the perinatal period is too big, too complex, or too close to reach with words. Expressive arts therapy offers other ways in — through movement, image-making, writing, or creative process. This is especially useful for processing birth trauma, identity shifts, and the grief that often accompanies the transition to parenthood.

Identity and Role Transition

Becoming a mother changes everything — your sense of self, your relationship with your body, your role in your partnership, your relationship with your own parents. This transition, called matrescence, is as significant as adolescence and far less acknowledged. We make room for all of it: the love and the grief, the identity you are stepping into and the one you are leaving behind.

Relationship and Partnership Support

The relational strain of the perinatal period is real and rarely discussed honestly. We can work through feelings about your partnership — the loneliness, the resentment, the grief over what changed — and build language for what you actually need, even when it feels impossible to ask.

Compassionate Processing, Not Toxic Positivity

You do not have to find the silver lining. You do not have to be grateful when you are exhausted. You are allowed to love your child completely and also find this profoundly hard. My job is not to convince you that everything is fine. It is to sit with you in what is actually true, and help you find your way through it.

What Working Together Looks Like

Your First Session

Your first session is a space to tell your story — as much or as little as you want to share. I will ask questions, listen closely, and we will begin to understand what has been happening and what you are hoping for. There is no checklist to get through. There is no performance required. You can come exactly as you are.

Weekly Sessions

Sessions are 50 minutes, typically weekly. Perinatal work often involves both the immediate day-to-day of survival and the deeper processing of birth, identity, and relational shifts. We hold space for both. The pace is always yours.

In Person & Telehealth

I offer perinatal therapy in person in Charlotte, NC and via telehealth throughout North Carolina and South Carolina. For many new mothers, telehealth is the most accessible option — no arranging childcare, no commute, no getting yourself out the door. You can attend a session from your couch, your car, or wherever you have 50 minutes.

Free 15-Minute Consultation

Before committing to anything, you are welcome to schedule a free 15-minute consultation. It is a low-pressure conversation where you can share a little about where you are, ask any questions, and see if working together feels like the right fit. No forms, no pressure — just a conversation.
