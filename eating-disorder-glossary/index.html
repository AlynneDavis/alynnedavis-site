Eating Disorder Glossary

Clinical terms, recovery language, and the words patients often use — explained clearly and without judgment.

Whether you are navigating your own recovery, supporting a loved one, or simply trying to understand what you have heard in a clinical setting, this glossary is here to help. Language matters in eating disorder recovery — and so does understanding it.

This page covers clinical diagnoses and terms used by professionals, as well as the informal language and slang that people with eating disorders commonly use among themselves. If you encounter a term in a session, in a support group, or online and you are not sure what it means, this is a place to start.

ABCD EFHI LMNO PRST W

Clinical Terms & Definitions

A

Anorexia Nervosa
An eating disorder characterized by severely restricted food intake, an intense fear of gaining weight, and a distorted body image. People with anorexia often see themselves as larger than they are, regardless of their actual weight. Anorexia has one of the highest mortality rates of any mental health condition. See also: Atypical Anorexia, Restriction.
ARFID (Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder)
An eating disorder involving severely limited food intake due to sensory sensitivity (texture, smell, color), fear of choking or vomiting, or low interest in food — but not driven by body image concerns or fear of weight gain. ARFID is common in children but also affects adolescents and adults. Learn about ARFID therapy.
Atypical Anorexia
A diagnosis under the OSFED umbrella in which all criteria for anorexia nervosa are met — including significant restriction, distorted body image, and psychological harm — but the person's weight falls within or above the "normal" range. Atypical anorexia is just as medically and psychologically serious as anorexia nervosa and is frequently missed because the person does not appear underweight.

B

Binge Eating Disorder (BED)
The most common eating disorder in the United States. BED involves recurrent episodes of eating large amounts of food in a short period, often rapidly and past the point of fullness, accompanied by a sense of loss of control and significant distress. Unlike bulimia, BED does not involve compensatory behaviors such as purging or restriction. Learn about binge eating therapy.
Body Checking
Repetitive behaviors used to monitor or assess the size, shape, or weight of one's body — such as pinching skin, measuring body parts, looking in mirrors repeatedly, or feeling bones. Body checking typically reinforces body image distortion and anxiety rather than providing reassurance.
Body Dysmorphic Disorder (BDD)
A condition involving obsessive preoccupation with perceived flaws in appearance — flaws that are often minor or invisible to others. BDD is distinct from eating disorders but frequently co-occurs with them. People with BDD may spend hours daily checking, hiding, or trying to fix the perceived flaw.
Body Image
The internal picture a person holds of their own body — including how they see it, feel about it, and relate to it. Body image is shaped by lived experiences, family messages, culture, trauma, and media. A negative body image is a central feature of many eating disorders, and healing the relationship with one's body is a key part of recovery.
Body Neutrality
A framework that focuses on what the body can do rather than what it looks like. Body neutrality does not require feeling positively about one's appearance — it simply asks for a truce: treating the body with basic care and respect regardless of how you feel about it on any given day. Often more accessible than body positivity for people in eating disorder recovery.
Body Positivity
A movement and philosophy centered on the belief that all bodies deserve acceptance and respect regardless of size, shape, ability, or appearance. Body positivity has roots in fat liberation activism. In a clinical context, it is sometimes a long-term goal of recovery — though for many clients, body neutrality is a more realistic early milestone.
Bulimia Nervosa
An eating disorder characterized by cycles of binge eating followed by compensatory behaviors — most commonly purging (self-induced vomiting), but also excessive exercise, fasting, or laxative misuse — intended to prevent weight gain. Bulimia is often driven by shame, perfectionism, and a desperate need for control. Learn about bulimia treatment.

C

CBT-E (Enhanced Cognitive Behavioral Therapy)
The leading evidence-based treatment for eating disorders, developed by Christopher Fairburn. CBT-E directly addresses the thoughts, beliefs, and behaviors that maintain disordered eating — including perfectionism, low self-esteem, mood intolerance, and interpersonal difficulties. It is transdiagnostic, meaning it is effective across eating disorder diagnoses. Learn how I use CBT-E in therapy.
Compensatory Behaviors
Actions taken after eating in an attempt to "undo" or offset the perceived consequences of food consumed. These include purging, excessive exercise, fasting, laxative use, and diuretic misuse. Compensatory behaviors are a defining feature of bulimia nervosa but also appear in other eating disorders.

D

Diet Culture
A system of beliefs that equates thinness with health, morality, and social value — while stigmatizing larger bodies and promoting chronic dietary restriction as normal or virtuous. Diet culture creates fertile ground for disordered eating by normalizing food restriction, fear of weight gain, and the idea that bodies need to be "fixed." Read more about how diet culture affects the nervous system.
Disordered Eating
A broad term describing a range of irregular or harmful eating behaviors that may not meet the full criteria for a clinical eating disorder diagnosis but still cause distress and impact quality of life. This includes chronic dieting, skipping meals, emotional eating, rigid food rules, and fear-based eating patterns. Learn the difference between disordered eating and eating disorders.

E

Emotional Eating
Using food to manage or soothe emotions rather than in response to physical hunger. Emotional eating is not inherently pathological — food does provide comfort — but it becomes problematic when it is the primary or only coping strategy for difficult feelings. Learn to distinguish emotional eating from hunger.
Expressive Arts Therapy
A therapeutic approach that uses creative modalities — visual art, movement, music, writing, drama — as pathways into emotional experience. In eating disorder recovery, expressive arts therapy can help clients access body-based experiences, process shame, and develop a sense of self beyond the eating disorder, particularly when words feel insufficient. Learn about expressive arts therapy.

F

Fear Foods
Foods that cause significant anxiety, avoidance, or distress for someone with an eating disorder — often due to caloric content, perceived "unhealthiness," or associated memories or rules. Fear food work is a common component of eating disorder treatment, involving gradual, supported exposure to avoided foods in a therapeutic context.
Food Rules
Internal guidelines — often rigid and shame-driven — that govern what, when, how much, or how a person allows themselves to eat. Food rules are a hallmark of disordered eating and can include rules about "clean" foods, meal timing, calorie limits, or what must be "earned" before eating. Challenging food rules is central to eating disorder recovery.
Full Recovery
A contested but important concept in the eating disorder field. Full recovery generally refers to a state in which a person is no longer engaging in disordered eating behaviors, is not preoccupied with food or body to a degree that impairs their life, and has developed the self-awareness and coping skills to sustain wellbeing over time. Recovery is not linear and looks different for everyone.

H

Hypermetabolism
A state of elevated metabolic rate that can occur during the refeeding phase of eating disorder recovery — particularly in anorexia nervosa. The body, recalibrating after a period of restriction, may require more calories than typical to restore weight and physiological functioning. Hypermetabolism is a medical reality that dietitians and treatment teams account for in recovery nutrition plans.

I

Intuitive Eating
A framework developed by dietitians Evelyn Tribole and Elyse Resch that emphasizes eating in response to internal hunger and fullness cues rather than external rules, diets, or moral judgments about food. Intuitive eating has ten principles including rejecting the diet mentality, honoring hunger, making peace with food, and respecting the body. It is often a long-term goal of eating disorder recovery — though for some clients, structured support is needed first.
Intensive Outpatient Program (IOP)
A structured level of eating disorder treatment that provides therapeutic support several hours per day, multiple days per week, while allowing clients to live at home. IOP typically includes group therapy, individual therapy, and nutritional support. It sits between standard weekly outpatient therapy and Partial Hospitalization (PHP) on the continuum of care.

L

Levels of Care
Eating disorder treatment is delivered across a spectrum of intensity based on medical and psychological need. From least to most intensive: outpatient (weekly therapy), IOP (intensive outpatient, several hours per week), PHP (partial hospitalization, full days), residential (24-hour non-hospital care), and inpatient (hospital-based medical stabilization). Matching someone to the right level of care is a critical component of safe treatment.

M

Masking (in ED context)
The process of concealing eating disorder behaviors or their severity — from family members, clinicians, or oneself. Masking can include eating normally in social settings while restricting privately, hiding food, underreporting symptoms, or presenting as "fine" to avoid concern or intervention. Masking is extremely common and is one reason eating disorders are often underestimated in severity.
Meal Support
A therapeutic intervention — typically offered in higher levels of care or by trained supports at home — in which a clinician, dietitian, or trained family member is present during meals to provide emotional support, reduce anxiety, and help the client complete their meal. Meal support is most common in the treatment of anorexia and ARFID.
Mindful Eating
A practice of bringing full, non-judgmental attention to the experience of eating — noticing hunger, fullness, taste, texture, and emotions without distraction or self-criticism. Mindful eating can be a useful recovery tool but is typically introduced after foundational work, as heightened body awareness can initially increase distress for some clients.

N

Night Eating Syndrome (NES)
A pattern characterized by minimal appetite in the morning, significant caloric intake in the evening and nighttime hours, and frequent waking during the night to eat. NES is recognized as an eating disorder and is associated with sleep disturbance, depression, and anxiety. It differs from binge eating in that the nighttime eating is typically not experienced as a binge episode.

O

Orthorexia
An obsessive focus on eating "correctly" or "purely" — characterized by rigid, escalating food rules, significant distress when "rules" are broken, and increasing restriction of what is considered acceptable to eat. Orthorexia is not yet a formal DSM diagnosis but is widely recognized clinically. It often masquerades as health-consciousness, which can delay recognition and treatment. Learn about orthorexia treatment.
OSFED (Other Specified Feeding or Eating Disorder)
A diagnostic category for eating disorders that cause significant distress or impairment but do not meet the full criteria for anorexia, bulimia, or BED. OSFED includes atypical anorexia, purging disorder, subthreshold bulimia, subthreshold BED, and night eating syndrome. OSFED is not a lesser diagnosis — presentations in this category can be just as medically and psychologically serious as the named disorders.

P

PHP (Partial Hospitalization Program)
A structured, full-day treatment program (typically 6–8 hours per day, 5 days per week) for individuals who need more support than IOP but do not require 24-hour residential care. PHP typically includes group therapy, individual therapy, dietitian sessions, and supported meals. It is often a step down from residential treatment or a step up from IOP when someone's condition is worsening.
Pica
An eating disorder involving the persistent ingestion of non-food substances — such as dirt, clay, chalk, hair, paper, or ice — that have no nutritional value. Pica is most common in children but also occurs in adults, particularly during pregnancy or in individuals with intellectual disabilities. It can cause serious medical complications depending on the substance consumed.
Purging
Any behavior used to rid the body of food already consumed, most commonly self-induced vomiting. Purging also includes misuse of laxatives, diuretics, or enemas for weight control purposes. Purging is a medical emergency risk — it can cause severe electrolyte imbalances, esophageal damage, tooth erosion, and cardiac complications. See also: Compensatory Behaviors.
Purging Disorder
An OSFED presentation in which a person purges (via vomiting, laxatives, or other means) after eating normal or small amounts of food — without the binge eating episodes that characterize bulimia nervosa. Purging disorder is often under-recognized because the person may not appear to be "struggling with food" in other visible ways.

R

Refeeding Syndrome
A potentially life-threatening metabolic complication that can occur when nutrition is reintroduced too quickly after a period of significant restriction or starvation. Refeeding syndrome involves dangerous shifts in phosphate, potassium, and magnesium levels. Medical monitoring is essential during the refeeding phase of eating disorder recovery, particularly in anorexia nervosa.
Relapse
A return to disordered eating behaviors after a period of recovery. Relapse is common in eating disorder recovery and does not indicate failure — it is a signal that additional support or a change in approach may be needed. Understanding the triggers that precede relapse is an important part of long-term recovery planning.
Restriction
The deliberate limitation of food intake — whether by reducing quantity, eliminating food groups, or imposing rigid eating schedules. Restriction may begin as dieting but can become a deeply entrenched behavioral pattern driven by anxiety, the need for control, or distorted beliefs about food and the body. Restriction activates the nervous system's scarcity response, often triggering cycles of bingeing or increasing obsession with food.
Rumination Disorder
A condition involving the repeated, involuntary regurgitation of recently swallowed food — which is then re-chewed, re-swallowed, or spat out. Rumination is not accompanied by nausea or retching and is often not within the person's conscious control. It can occur in infants, children, adolescents, and adults and is often mistaken for reflux.

S

Safe Foods
Foods that someone with an eating disorder feels comfortable eating — typically low in calories, familiar, or in some way perceived as "acceptable" within the eating disorder's rules. While safe foods can provide a starting point in treatment, rigid reliance on them often maintains the eating disorder. See also: Fear Foods.
Set Point Theory
The theory that the body has a genetically influenced weight range it is predisposed to maintain, and will actively regulate metabolism, hunger, and fullness signals to return to that range when disrupted by restriction or overeating. Set point theory is relevant in eating disorder recovery because it helps explain why the body may resist both significant weight loss and weight gain, and why chronic dieting is often physiologically counterproductive.
Somatic Therapy
A body-based therapeutic approach that focuses on the connection between physical sensations and emotional experience. In eating disorder recovery, somatic approaches help clients develop a more trusting relationship with their body's signals — including hunger, fullness, and emotion. Learn more about somatic therapy.

T

Trauma-Informed Care
A clinical framework that recognizes the widespread impact of trauma on mental and physical health, integrates knowledge of trauma into all aspects of care, and prioritizes safety, trustworthiness, and client empowerment. Trauma-informed eating disorder therapy recognizes that for many people, disordered eating is directly connected to traumatic experiences — and that recovery requires addressing what lies beneath the behavior, not just the behavior itself. Learn about trauma-informed therapy.

W

Weight Restoration
The process of returning to a physiologically healthy weight as part of eating disorder recovery — most commonly in the treatment of anorexia nervosa. Weight restoration is a medical necessity in cases of medical compromise, and research consistently shows that cognitive function, emotional regulation, and the ability to engage in therapy improve significantly as weight is restored. Weight restoration is not the same as full recovery — it is an important foundation that makes deeper psychological work possible.

Slang & Informal Language Patients Often Use

People living with eating disorders — particularly those who have spent time in online communities or inpatient settings — often use a distinct vocabulary. Understanding this language helps clinicians, family members, and loved ones recognize what is being communicated. It is worth noting that some of this language, particularly terms like "Ana" and "Mia," originated in pro-eating disorder communities and can reinforce the eating disorder as an identity. In therapy, we gently explore the meaning and function of the language a client uses.

"Ana" / "Mia"
Personifications of anorexia ("Ana") and bulimia ("Mia") — used in online communities and sometimes in personal journals or conversations to refer to the eating disorder as if it were a separate entity or inner voice. While personification can help externalize the disorder in therapy, "Ana" and "Mia" as identities are associated with pro-eating disorder communities that actively promote restriction and purging behaviors.
"Body check" / "Body checking"
See: Body Checking in the clinical terms section. Used colloquially to describe compulsive self-monitoring behaviors such as mirror checking, scale weighing, or measuring body parts.
"Chew and spit"
The act of chewing food for its taste or sensory experience and then spitting it out before swallowing. Often used as a way to eat "without consequences" — though it is associated with significant physical and psychological harm and is considered a disordered eating behavior.
"Clean eating"
A term popularized in wellness culture to describe eating only whole, minimally processed foods. When used in an eating disorder context, "clean eating" often describes rigid rules about food purity that function as restriction. The concept of "clean" eating implies that other foods are "dirty" — a moralization of food that can fuel disordered patterns. Read more about how food morality affects the nervous system.
"Detox"
Used in the eating disorder context to justify restriction under the guise of health — e.g., "I just need to detox for a few days." The concept of dietary detoxing is not supported by medical evidence; the liver and kidneys handle detoxification continuously. When used repeatedly, "detox" language often signals restriction cycles.
"Earning it" / "Merit eating"
The belief that food must be earned through exercise, productivity, restriction, or good behavior. Merit eating reflects the moralization of food — the idea that eating is a reward or privilege rather than a basic human need. This pattern is extremely common in eating disorder presentations and is directly addressed in recovery work.
"ED brain" / "Eating disorder voice"
Informal terms for the internalized thoughts, rules, and urges that feel like they come from the eating disorder rather than the person's authentic self. Externalizing the eating disorder — recognizing it as something separate from one's identity — is a therapeutic technique used in many approaches to eating disorder treatment.
"Fitspiration" / "Fitspo"
Social media content that frames extreme exercise, low body fat, and restrictive eating as aspirational or motivational. While framed as positive, fitspo content frequently promotes unrealistic body standards and can trigger or reinforce disordered eating behaviors, particularly in people who are already vulnerable.
"Head hunger"
A colloquial term for the experience of wanting to eat in the absence of physical hunger — often driven by emotions, habit, or cravings. Used to distinguish psychological desire to eat from physiological hunger cues. The concept is useful in recovery but can also be weaponized by the eating disorder to dismiss legitimate hunger as "not real."
"Numbers"
A euphemism for specific weights, calorie counts, clothing sizes, or measurements that carry significant emotional weight for someone with an eating disorder. Clinicians and recovery communities often choose not to share "numbers" to avoid triggering comparison or competition. Discussing goals in terms of behaviors and feelings rather than numbers is a common recovery practice.
"Pro-Ana" / "Pro-Mia"
Online communities and content that actively promote anorexia and bulimia as lifestyle choices rather than mental health conditions. Pro-Ana and Pro-Mia spaces are medically dangerous and psychologically harmful, providing tips for restricting, purging, and hiding behaviors from clinicians and family members. If a client or loved one is spending time in these communities, it is important to raise with a treatment provider.
"Restrict"
Used as both a noun and a verb — "I've been restricting" or "going into a restrict." In patient language, restricting often refers to intentional caloric limitation but can also encompass more subtle patterns of avoidance, skipping meals, or reducing variety. See: Restriction.
"Safe foods" / "Unsafe foods"
See: Safe Foods and Fear Foods in the clinical terms section. Used colloquially to describe foods that feel permissible versus those that provoke fear or avoidance within the eating disorder's rule system.
"Thinspiration" / "Thinspo"
Social media images and content featuring extremely thin bodies, shared with the explicit or implicit goal of inspiring restriction. Thinspo content is associated with pro-eating disorder communities and has been linked to increased eating disorder symptoms in research. Many platforms have policies against this content, though it continues to circulate.
"Void eating"
Eating in a dissociated or numbed state — consuming food without awareness, connection, or sensory experience, often to avoid the emotional experience of eating. Void eating is distinct from binge eating in that it may not involve large quantities; it is about the absence of presence rather than the amount consumed.

A Note on Language in Recovery

Language in eating disorder recovery is not neutral. The words we use — about food, bodies, hunger, and worth — carry meaning that can either reinforce disordered thinking or gently challenge it. Part of the work in therapy is examining the language you use and what it reveals about the beliefs driving your eating disorder.

If you have encountered a term here that resonates — or one that you use yourself and are wondering about — that can be a meaningful place to begin a conversation. Therapy is a space to explore not just behaviors, but the stories and beliefs beneath them.
