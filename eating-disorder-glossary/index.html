Eating Disorder Glossary

Clinical terms, medical complications, treatment approaches, recovery language, and the words patients use — all in one place, explained without judgment.

Whether you are in your own recovery, supporting someone you love, or trying to understand what you heard in a session or support group — this glossary is here to help. Language in the eating disorder world is layered: there is clinical language, recovery community language, and the words that people use quietly among themselves when they are still in the thick of it.

This page covers all three. You will find formal DSM diagnoses, medical complications, treatment approaches, therapeutic frameworks, and the informal slang that often signals someone is struggling in ways that may not yet be visible. If a term resonates — that is worth paying attention to.

Clinical Terms, Diagnoses & Definitions

A

Alexithymia
Difficulty identifying, describing, or distinguishing between emotions and physical sensations. Alexithymia is highly prevalent among people with eating disorders and is thought to be both a contributing factor and a consequence of disordered eating. When someone cannot name what they feel, food and body behaviors often become the language for those unnamed emotional states. Expressive arts therapy can be particularly helpful here, as it offers non-verbal pathways into emotional experience.
All-or-Nothing Thinking
A cognitive distortion in which things are evaluated in extreme, black-and-white terms — food is "good" or "bad," a day is "perfect" or "ruined," a person is "in control" or "out of control." All-or-nothing thinking is one of the most common cognitive patterns underlying eating disorders and is directly targeted in CBT-E and other evidence-based treatments. See also: Cognitive Distortions.
Ambivalence in Recovery
A normal and expected experience in eating disorder recovery — holding simultaneous feelings of wanting to get better and wanting to hold on to the eating disorder. The eating disorder often serves important psychological functions (control, identity, coping, numbing), and recovery asks someone to give those up before they have found alternatives. Ambivalence is not resistance; it is a sign that recovery is real and challenging work.
Amenorrhea
The loss of the menstrual period, often caused by malnutrition, low body weight, or physiological stress from an eating disorder. Amenorrhea is a serious medical complication, historically included as a diagnostic criterion for anorexia nervosa (since removed from DSM-5). It indicates that the body has deprioritized reproductive function in response to insufficient energy — and is associated with bone density loss and long-term hormonal consequences.
Anorexia Nervosa
An eating disorder characterized by severely restricted food intake, intense fear of weight gain, and significant disturbance in how one's weight or body shape is perceived. Anorexia has one of the highest mortality rates of any mental health condition — from both medical complications and suicide. There are two subtypes: the restricting type and the binge-purge type. See also: Atypical Anorexia, Restriction. Learn about treatment.
ARFID (Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder)
An eating disorder involving severely limited food intake due to sensory sensitivity (texture, smell, appearance, temperature), fear of choking or vomiting, or low interest in food — but not driven by body image concerns or fear of weight gain. ARFID is common in children but also affects adolescents and adults, including many who were previously undiagnosed. Learn about ARFID therapy.
Atypical Anorexia
An OSFED diagnosis in which all criteria for anorexia nervosa are present — severe restriction, intense fear of weight gain, distorted body image — but the person's weight is in the "normal" or above-normal range. Atypical anorexia is frequently missed, minimized, and under-treated because the person doesn't "look sick enough." It is just as medically and psychologically serious as anorexia nervosa. This diagnosis reflects a critical flaw in how eating disorders are often perceived and treated.

B

Binge Eating Disorder (BED)
The most common eating disorder in the United States. BED involves recurrent episodes of eating unusually large amounts of food in a discrete period of time, accompanied by a sense of loss of control and significant emotional distress — but without the compensatory behaviors (purging, restriction, excessive exercise) that characterize bulimia. BED is strongly associated with shame, trauma, emotional dysregulation, and a long history of dieting. Learn about binge eating therapy.
Binge-Purge Subtype (Anorexia)
One of two subtypes of anorexia nervosa, in which the person engages in binge eating and/or purging behaviors (vomiting, laxative use, excessive exercise) in addition to severe restriction. This subtype is often associated with higher impulsivity, more intense emotional dysregulation, and greater medical instability than the restricting subtype. See also: Anorexia Nervosa, Restricting Subtype.
BMI (Body Mass Index)
A numerical measure calculated from height and weight that has historically been used to classify bodies as underweight, normal, overweight, or obese. BMI is widely used in medical and research settings but is a deeply flawed tool — it was designed using data from white European men, does not account for muscle mass, bone density, age, sex, or ethnicity, and has been used to gatekeep eating disorder diagnoses. A person with a "normal" BMI can be medically compromised by an eating disorder. See also: Atypical Anorexia, Weight Stigma.
Body Avoidance
The deliberate avoidance of looking at, touching, or attending to one's own body — wearing loose clothing, avoiding mirrors, never weighing, not looking down in the shower. Body avoidance typically coexists with negative body image and maintains it, because it prevents the person from gathering accurate information about their body or developing a neutral relationship with it. See also: Body Checking.
Body Checking
Repetitive behaviors used to monitor or assess the size, shape, or weight of the body — pinching skin, measuring waist or thighs, checking in mirrors, counting ribs or hipbones, or weighing multiple times per day. Body checking typically reinforces body image distortion and anxiety rather than providing reassurance, creating a cycle that intensifies preoccupation.
Body Dysmorphic Disorder (BDD)
A mental health condition involving obsessive preoccupation with a perceived flaw in appearance that others cannot see or see only slightly. BDD is distinct from eating disorders but co-occurs with them frequently. People with BDD may spend hours each day checking, hiding, or attempting to fix the perceived flaw, and the distress can be severe and disabling.
Body Image
The internal, multidimensional experience a person has of their own body — including how they see it, feel about it, sense it from inside, and relate to it over time. Body image is shaped by early experiences, trauma, family messages, culture, media, and the nervous system. A disturbed body image is a central feature of many eating disorders, and healing body image is a significant — often long — part of recovery.
Body Neutrality
A framework that shifts focus away from how the body looks toward what the body can do and feel. Body neutrality does not ask someone to love their body — it simply asks for a functional truce: caring for the body, not punishing it, without requiring positive feelings about appearance. For many people in eating disorder recovery, neutrality is a more accessible and honest goal than body positivity.
Body Positivity
A movement grounded in fat liberation activism that asserts all bodies are worthy of respect, dignity, and care regardless of size, shape, ability, or appearance. While body positivity is often a long-term recovery aspiration, the pressure to feel positively about one's body can feel unattainable early in recovery. See also: Body Neutrality.
Body Shame
The deeply painful belief that one's body is fundamentally wrong, unacceptable, or disgusting. Body shame is distinct from dissatisfaction — it is not just not liking one's appearance but feeling that the body itself is evidence of moral or personal failure. Body shame is a core driver of many eating disorders and one of the most important targets in trauma-informed eating disorder therapy.
Bulimia Nervosa
An eating disorder characterized by repeated cycles of binge eating followed by compensatory behaviors — most commonly purging (self-induced vomiting), but also excessive exercise, fasting, or laxative misuse — intended to prevent weight gain or "undo" the binge. Bulimia is often driven by shame, perfectionism, emotional dysregulation, and a profound fear of losing control. Many people with bulimia are within a "normal" weight range, which means the disorder is often invisible to others. Learn about bulimia treatment.

C

CBT-E (Enhanced Cognitive Behavioral Therapy)
The leading evidence-based treatment for eating disorders, developed by Christopher Fairburn. CBT-E is "transdiagnostic" — effective across eating disorder diagnoses — and addresses the specific thoughts, behaviors, and maintaining mechanisms of disordered eating, including perfectionism, low self-esteem, mood intolerance, and interpersonal difficulties. It is highly structured and typically conducted over 20 weeks for outpatient presentations. Learn how I work with clients.
Cognitive Distortions
Patterns of thinking that are inaccurate, extreme, or unhelpful — and that maintain and reinforce eating disorder behaviors. Common cognitive distortions in eating disorders include all-or-nothing thinking, catastrophizing, mind-reading, emotional reasoning ("I feel fat, therefore I am fat"), magical thinking around food, and selective attention to body-related information. See also: All-or-Nothing Thinking.
Compensatory Behaviors
Actions taken after eating in an attempt to "undo" or offset food already consumed. Includes purging (vomiting), laxative and diuretic misuse, excessive or compulsive exercise, and fasting. Compensatory behaviors are a defining feature of bulimia nervosa but appear across eating disorder presentations and are associated with serious medical risks. See also: Purging, Compulsive Exercise.
Compulsive Exercise / Exercise Addiction
A pattern of exercise that is rigid, excessive, distress-driven, and continued despite injury, illness, or social consequence. Compulsive exercise differs from healthy exercise in its function: it is used to "burn off" food, punish the body, manage anxiety or guilt, or maintain a sense of control. Also called hypergymnasia. It is a compensatory behavior in many eating disorders and can be medically dangerous. See also: Hypergymnasia.
Co-Occurring Disorders
Mental health conditions that exist alongside an eating disorder. Common co-occurring disorders include depression, anxiety disorders, PTSD and complex trauma, OCD, ADHD, borderline personality disorder, and substance use disorders. Effective eating disorder treatment attends to the whole person — not just the food and body behaviors — and addresses underlying conditions that maintain or were caused by the eating disorder. Learn about trauma-informed care.
Control (as an ED Driver)
The experience of controlling food and body as a response to feeling out of control in other areas of life — relationships, emotions, trauma, unpredictability. Food restriction in particular is often described by clients as the "one thing I can control." Understanding what the eating disorder is providing a sense of control over is a key part of the therapeutic process.

D

DBT (Dialectical Behavior Therapy)
An evidence-based treatment developed by Marsha Linehan that combines cognitive-behavioral techniques with mindfulness and acceptance strategies. DBT is commonly used in eating disorder treatment, particularly for clients with significant emotional dysregulation, impulsivity, or self-harm behaviors alongside their eating disorder. Core skills include distress tolerance, emotion regulation, interpersonal effectiveness, and mindfulness.
Dental Complications
Physical damage to teeth and gums caused by repeated purging. Stomach acid exposure erodes tooth enamel, causes tooth sensitivity and discoloration, and can lead to significant dental damage. Dentists are often the first clinicians to notice signs of purging. Dental complications are a common medical consequence of bulimia nervosa and purging disorder. See also: Purging, Russell's Sign.
Diet Culture
A pervasive system of beliefs that equates thinness with health, morality, and social value — while stigmatizing larger bodies and normalizing chronic restriction as virtuous. Diet culture is not neutral background noise; it is the water in which disordered eating grows. It creates and sustains eating disorders by pathologizing normal, natural bodies and teaching people that their worth depends on their size. Read more about diet culture and the nervous system.
Disordered Eating
A broad umbrella term for harmful eating patterns that cause distress or impairment but may not meet full clinical criteria for an eating disorder diagnosis. Includes chronic dieting, skipping meals, compensatory behaviors, fear-based eating, rigid food rules, and using food to cope with emotions. Disordered eating exists on a continuum with clinical eating disorders, and many people who struggle never receive a formal diagnosis. Learn the difference.
Dissociation (in eating disorder context)
A disconnection from body sensations, emotions, or present experience — common during binge episodes, purging, or periods of extreme restriction. Dissociation in an eating disorder context often develops as a coping response to trauma or overwhelming emotion: the eating disorder behavior becomes a way to "leave" an unbearable internal experience. Trauma-informed treatment attends carefully to dissociative patterns. Learn about trauma-informed eating disorder therapy.
Diuretic Misuse
The misuse of diuretics (water pills) to reduce water weight as a compensatory behavior. Diuretics do not cause fat loss — they cause water and electrolyte loss — but may create a temporary reduction in scale weight that reinforces the behavior. Diuretic misuse causes dangerous electrolyte imbalances, dehydration, kidney stress, and can lead to cardiac complications. See also: Compensatory Behaviors, Electrolyte Imbalance.

E

EDNOS (Eating Disorder Not Otherwise Specified)
The former DSM-IV catch-all diagnosis for eating disorders that did not meet the full criteria for anorexia or bulimia — now replaced by OSFED and UFED in the DSM-5. EDNOS was widely used clinically for many years and is still referenced in older records and community discussions. Like OSFED, EDNOS presentations were often just as serious as the named disorders.
Ego-Syntonic vs. Ego-Dystonic
Ego-syntonic means the eating disorder feels consistent with, or even central to, the person's sense of self — it feels like "me," not a problem. Ego-dystonic means the eating disorder feels alien, unwanted, or separate from who the person knows themselves to be. Most eating disorders begin as ego-syntonic and shift toward ego-dystonic as recovery progresses. Understanding where a client is on this spectrum informs pacing and approach in treatment.
Electrolyte Imbalance
A potentially life-threatening disruption in the body's levels of key minerals — potassium, sodium, phosphate, magnesium, and calcium — caused by purging, laxative misuse, restriction, or diuretic use. Electrolyte imbalances can cause cardiac arrhythmias, muscle weakness, kidney failure, and death. They are one of the primary reasons eating disorders are medically dangerous even when a person does not appear underweight.
Emotional Dysregulation
Difficulty managing emotional experiences — being flooded by emotions, struggling to return to baseline after distress, or feeling emotionally numb. Emotional dysregulation is both a cause and a consequence of eating disorders. Food and body behaviors often function as regulation strategies: restriction as control, bingeing as numbing, purging as relief. Recovery requires building alternative emotional regulation skills. Learn about emotion-focused therapy.
Emotional Eating
Eating in response to emotions rather than physical hunger — using food to soothe, numb, distract, celebrate, or cope. Emotional eating is not inherently disordered, but it becomes problematic when it is the primary coping mechanism and causes distress. Learn to distinguish emotional eating from hunger.
Expressive Arts Therapy
A therapeutic modality using creative expression — visual art, movement, music, poetry, drama — as pathways into emotional experience that words alone cannot always reach. In eating disorder recovery, expressive arts therapy helps clients access the body, process shame, build an identity beyond the eating disorder, and develop a relationship with themselves that is not mediated through appearance or food. Learn more.

F

FBT / Maudsley Approach (Family-Based Treatment)
An evidence-based eating disorder treatment developed at the Maudsley Hospital in London, designed primarily for adolescents with anorexia nervosa. FBT involves parents and caregivers taking temporary control of the child's eating to support weight restoration (Phase 1), then gradually returning control to the adolescent (Phase 2), and finally addressing broader developmental and identity issues (Phase 3). FBT is one of the most researched treatments for adolescent eating disorders.
Fear Foods
Foods that cause significant anxiety, avoidance, or distress — often because of perceived caloric content, food rules, or associations with "losing control." Fear food work is a common, gradual component of eating disorder recovery: supported, repeated exposure to feared foods in a therapeutic context that builds tolerance and challenges the belief that eating those foods is catastrophic.
Food Morality
The assignment of moral value to food and eating — calling foods "good" or "bad," "clean" or "dirty," and by extension labeling oneself as good or bad based on what one has eaten. Food morality is one of the most common cultural underpinnings of disordered eating and is directly addressed in recovery. Food does not have moral value. Eating a piece of cake does not make someone "bad." Read more.
Food Noise
The near-constant mental chatter about food — what to eat, what not to eat, calorie calculations, meal planning, food regret, what was eaten, what will be eaten next. Food noise is a consequence of restriction and scarcity: a brain that is not getting enough food becomes biologically preoccupied with obtaining it. When people describe "not being able to stop thinking about food," food noise is what they are experiencing. It typically resolves with adequate, consistent nourishment.
Food Rules
Rigid, internally imposed guidelines governing what, when, how much, or how one may eat. Food rules are a hallmark of eating disorders and can include rules about calories, food groups, timing ("no eating after 7pm"), combinations ("no carbs with fat"), or earning food through exercise. The more elaborate and rigid the food rules, the more they typically maintain the disorder and generate shame when "broken."
Full Recovery
The concept that complete recovery from an eating disorder is possible — not just managing symptoms, but genuinely no longer engaging in disordered behaviors and no longer being significantly preoccupied with food or body. Full recovery is debated in the clinical community but strongly supported by research and lived experience. Recovery is not linear; it involves setbacks, and it looks different for everyone. The goal is a life no longer organized around food and body.

G

Grazing
A pattern of eating continuously throughout the day in small amounts — nibbling, snacking repeatedly, eating without clear start or stop — rather than eating structured meals. Grazing can be a form of restriction (avoiding "real" meals), a response to anxiety about eating larger amounts at once, or a way of eating that has lost structure. It is recognized clinically and may be addressed in treatment through meal planning and structure.

H

Health at Every Size (HAES)
A framework and social justice movement that challenges the idea that weight is a reliable indicator of health and that weight loss should be a primary health goal. HAES emphasizes size-inclusive medical care, joyful movement, intuitive eating, and addressing weight stigma in healthcare. HAES-aligned clinicians do not pursue weight loss as a treatment goal and do not use weight or BMI as measures of recovery success.
Hunger Amnesia
The loss of reliable hunger and fullness cues after a prolonged period of restriction or chaotic eating. When someone has spent a long time ignoring, overriding, or not experiencing hunger, the body's internal signaling system can become suppressed or distorted. Re-learning to recognize and respond to hunger is a significant part of early recovery — and often requires structured meal support rather than relying solely on internal cues.
Hypergymnasia
The clinical term for compulsive, excessive exercise used as an eating disorder behavior — exercising beyond what is healthy, often driven by guilt, anxiety, the need to compensate for food eaten, or to "earn" the right to eat. Hypergymnasia is associated with anxiety, rigidity, and a driven, almost frantic quality to movement. See also: Compulsive Exercise.
Hypermetabolism
An elevated metabolic rate that occurs during the refeeding phase of eating disorder recovery — particularly in anorexia. The body, recalibrating after restriction, may require significantly more calories than typical to restore weight and physiological function. Hypermetabolism is a real, documented phenomenon and is why caloric needs in early recovery can be far higher than expected.

I

Identity and the Eating Disorder
For many people, the eating disorder becomes deeply intertwined with identity — being "the one who doesn't eat much," "the disciplined one," "the healthy eater." Recovery often involves a profound identity disruption: who am I if I am not my eating disorder? Therapy creates space to explore and build a self that is not organized around food, body, and control.
Interoception
The ability to perceive and interpret the body's internal signals — hunger, fullness, temperature, heartbeat, pain, emotion in the body. Interoception is frequently disrupted in eating disorders: restriction blunts hunger signals, purging overrides fullness, and trauma disconnects people from body sensation. Recovery often involves slowly rebuilding the ability to trust and respond to the body's signals.
Intuitive Eating
A framework developed by dietitians Evelyn Tribole and Elyse Resch built on 10 principles including rejecting the diet mentality, honoring hunger, making peace with food, discovering the satisfaction factor, and respecting the body. Intuitive eating is often a long-term recovery goal but requires foundational work first — it is not an appropriate starting point for people whose hunger cues are unreliable or whose restriction is severe.
Intensive Outpatient Program (IOP)
A level of eating disorder treatment providing several hours of support per day, multiple days per week, while the person continues to live at home. IOP typically includes group therapy, individual therapy, dietitian support, and sometimes supported meals. It is more intensive than weekly outpatient therapy and less intensive than PHP. See also: Levels of Care.
IPT (Interpersonal Therapy)
An evidence-based treatment that addresses how interpersonal problems — grief, role transitions, relationship conflicts, social isolation — contribute to and maintain an eating disorder. IPT is particularly effective for binge eating disorder and bulimia nervosa, where emotional eating is tied to relational stress. IPT does not directly focus on food and eating but works on the emotional triggers beneath disordered behavior.

J

Joyful Movement
A concept from the Health at Every Size and intuitive eating frameworks that encourages moving the body in ways that feel genuinely pleasurable rather than punitive. Joyful movement stands in contrast to compulsive, guilt-driven exercise. In eating disorder recovery, rebuilding a relationship with movement that is not about compensation or punishment is an important part of healing.

L

Lanugo
A fine, soft layer of hair that grows on the body — typically the face, arms, and torso — as a physiological response to malnutrition and low body temperature. The body grows lanugo in an attempt to insulate itself when there is insufficient body fat to do so. Lanugo is a visible medical sign of severe restriction and is associated with the starvation response.
Laxative Misuse / Laxative Abuse
The repeated use of laxatives beyond their medically intended purpose — to compensate for eating, to reduce scale weight, or to feel "empty." Laxatives do not meaningfully prevent calorie absorption (most absorption occurs before the large intestine), but they cause dehydration, electrolyte imbalances, laxative dependence, and long-term damage to the colon. Stopping laxatives after chronic misuse requires gradual, medically supervised tapering.
Levels of Care
Eating disorder treatment exists on a continuum of intensity. From least to most intensive: outpatient (weekly individual and/or group therapy), IOP (intensive outpatient, 3–5 days/week for 3 hours/day), PHP (partial hospitalization, 5 days/week for 6–8 hours/day), residential (24-hour care in a non-hospital setting), and inpatient medical (hospital-based stabilization for medical emergencies). The right level of care depends on medical status, behavioral severity, support system, and functioning.

M

Masking (in ED context)
Concealing eating disorder behaviors or their severity — from family, clinicians, or oneself. This includes eating normally in public while restricting privately, hiding food, underreporting symptoms, showing up to appointments appearing well when not, and intellectualizing symptoms without emotional connection. Masking is extremely common and is one reason eating disorders are so often underestimated.
Meal Support
A therapeutic intervention — most common in higher levels of care — in which a trained clinician, dietitian, or support person is present during meals to provide emotional co-regulation, reduce anxiety, and support meal completion. For many people with eating disorders, eating is experienced as terrifying. Meal support offers a relational presence during one of the most challenging parts of recovery.
Medical Complications of Eating Disorders
Eating disorders cause serious, wide-ranging medical harm. Complications include cardiac arrhythmias and heart failure, electrolyte imbalances, bone density loss (osteopenia/osteoporosis), amenorrhea and fertility impairment, dental erosion, gastrointestinal damage, kidney dysfunction, lanugo, edema (swelling from refeeding), brain atrophy, peripheral neuropathy, and death. Eating disorders are among the most medically serious psychiatric conditions — not all of which are visible from the outside.
Mindful Eating
A practice of bringing non-judgmental, present-moment attention to the experience of eating — noticing hunger, fullness, taste, texture, pace, and emotional states without self-criticism. Mindful eating can be a useful recovery tool, but is typically introduced after foundational work is in place, as heightened body awareness can initially increase distress for some clients.
Muscle Dysmorphia ("Bigorexia")
A form of body dysmorphic disorder — and an emerging eating disorder presentation — characterized by obsessive preoccupation with being insufficiently muscular, despite often being highly muscular. Muscle dysmorphia involves rigid exercise and eating regimens, extreme distress when workouts are missed or food plans deviated from, and social withdrawal in service of training and diet. It is more common in men but affects people of all genders and is frequently unrecognized.

N

Night Eating Syndrome (NES)
A pattern of minimal appetite in the morning, very high caloric intake in the evening and nighttime, and frequent nighttime awakenings accompanied by eating. NES is associated with depression, insomnia, and anxiety, and differs from binge eating in that nighttime eating involves smaller, less out-of-control portions. NES is recognized in the DSM-5 under OSFED.
Nutritional Rehabilitation
The process of restoring adequate nutrition after restriction, malnutrition, or disordered eating — typically in collaboration with a registered dietitian. Nutritional rehabilitation addresses not just calories consumed but also variety, meal structure, the adequacy of key nutrients (iron, calcium, vitamin D, zinc, B vitamins), and the psychological relationship with food. In cases of severe restriction, nutritional rehabilitation must be medically monitored to avoid refeeding syndrome.

O

Orthorexia
An obsessive preoccupation with eating "purely" or "correctly" — characterized by rigid, escalating food rules, intense distress when rules are violated, increasing social isolation around food, and significant restriction of what is deemed acceptable to eat. Orthorexia is not yet a formal DSM diagnosis but is widely recognized clinically. It often goes undetected because it masquerades as health-consciousness. The harm is real: physical malnourishment and profound psychological suffering. Learn about treatment.
OSFED (Other Specified Feeding or Eating Disorder)
A DSM-5 diagnostic category for eating disorders that cause significant distress or impairment but do not meet the full criteria for anorexia, bulimia, or BED. OSFED includes atypical anorexia, subthreshold bulimia, subthreshold BED, purging disorder, and night eating syndrome. OSFED is not a lesser or milder category — presentations here are often severe and require treatment at the same level as the major diagnoses.
Over-Exercise
Informal term for compulsive or excessive exercise used as an eating disorder behavior. Characterized by exercising despite illness or injury, inability to take rest days without extreme guilt, exercising to compensate for food eaten or to "earn" the right to eat, and exercise serving as the primary way of managing emotions or anxiety. See also: Compulsive Exercise, Hypergymnasia.

P

Perfectionism (as an ED driver)
A pervasive pattern of holding oneself to impossibly high standards, experiencing intense distress when those standards are not met, and basing self-worth heavily on achievement and performance. Perfectionism is one of the most robustly supported risk factors for eating disorders — the same drive that makes someone restrict eating to "perfect" control shows up in many domains of life. Perfectionism is a direct target in CBT-E.
Perinatal Eating Disorders
Eating disorders that arise during pregnancy or the postpartum period — or that re-emerge or worsen during this time. Perinatal eating disorders are underrecognized and underscreened. They are associated with preterm birth, low birth weight, postpartum depression, and difficulty with breastfeeding. Pregnancy involves profound, unavoidable body changes that can intensify distress for people with existing or latent body image concerns. Learn about perinatal support.
PHP (Partial Hospitalization Program)
A full-day structured treatment program (typically 6–8 hours per day, 5 days per week) for people who need more support than IOP but do not require 24-hour residential care. PHP typically includes group therapy, individual therapy, dietitian sessions, psychiatry, and supported meals. It is often used as a step-down from residential or a step-up from IOP when someone is not stable at the lower level. See also: Levels of Care.
Pica
A feeding disorder involving the repeated ingestion of non-food substances — including dirt, clay, chalk, hair, ice, paper, or metal — that have no nutritional value. Pica occurs most commonly in children, pregnant women, and people with intellectual or developmental disabilities. Medical complications vary widely depending on what is consumed. Pica can cause intestinal blockages, poisoning, and parasitic infection.
Purging
Any behavior intended to rid the body of food already consumed — most commonly self-induced vomiting, but also laxative misuse, diuretic misuse, or enema use. Purging is medically dangerous: it causes electrolyte imbalances, cardiac arrhythmias, esophageal damage, Mallory-Weiss tears, parotid gland swelling, tooth erosion, and Russell's Sign. It is addictive in its function — many people describe purging as intensely relieving in the moment, which reinforces its use. See also: Compensatory Behaviors.
Purging Disorder
An OSFED presentation in which purging (vomiting, laxatives, or other means) occurs after eating normal or small amounts of food — without the binge-eating episodes that define bulimia. Purging disorder is frequently under-recognized because the person may have no visible food or weight-related struggles outside of this pattern. The medical risks are equivalent to those of bulimia nervosa.
Psychoeducation
Providing clinically accurate information about eating disorders, nutrition, the body's response to restriction and purging, and the psychological mechanisms that maintain disordered behaviors. Psychoeducation is a foundational component of eating disorder treatment. Understanding why the body craves food after restriction, what binge-restrict cycles are physiologically, or why hunger cues become unreliable helps clients make sense of their experiences and reduces shame.

R

Refeeding Syndrome
A potentially life-threatening metabolic complication caused by reintroducing nutrition too rapidly after prolonged restriction or starvation. It involves dangerous shifts in phosphate, potassium, and magnesium — leading to cardiac arrhythmias, neurological symptoms, respiratory failure, and death. Medical monitoring is essential in the early stages of refeeding, particularly in severe anorexia.
Relapse
A return to disordered eating behaviors after a period of recovery. Relapse is common and does not mean recovery has failed — it is a signal that something needs attention. Understanding the emotional, relational, or circumstantial triggers that precede relapse is a central part of long-term recovery planning. Many people experience multiple relapses on the way to sustained recovery.
Residential Treatment
A 24-hour, non-hospital eating disorder treatment program in which clients live on-site while receiving intensive therapeutic and nutritional care. Residential treatment is appropriate when someone is medically stable but requires more support than PHP can provide. Programming typically includes individual therapy, group therapy, dietitian support, psychiatric care, meal support, and skills building.
Restricting Subtype (Anorexia)
One of two subtypes of anorexia nervosa. In the restricting subtype, weight loss is achieved primarily through restriction and/or excessive exercise, without regular binge-eating or purging. See also: Binge-Purge Subtype.
Restriction
The deliberate limitation of food intake — by reducing quantity, eliminating food groups, restricting timing, or following rules about what is "allowed." Restriction is the defining behavior of anorexia nervosa but appears across eating disorder presentations. Physiologically, restriction triggers the body's starvation response — slowing metabolism, intensifying food preoccupation, and priming the brain for bingeing. Psychologically, restriction often functions as a coping mechanism for anxiety, trauma, or the need for control.
Rumination Disorder
A feeding disorder involving the repeated, involuntary regurgitation of recently swallowed food — which is then re-chewed, re-swallowed, or spat out. Rumination is not accompanied by nausea or effort and is often not within the person's conscious control. It is frequently mistaken for reflux or vomiting. Rumination disorder can occur at any age and causes significant nutritional and social impairment.
Russell's Sign
Calluses or scarring on the knuckles or back of the hand, caused by repeated contact with the upper teeth during self-induced vomiting. Russell's Sign is a clinical indicator that a clinician or medical provider may notice during a physical examination. Named after psychiatrist Gerald Russell, who described it in research on bulimia nervosa.

S

Safe Foods
Foods that someone with an eating disorder feels comfortable eating — typically perceived as "acceptable," low-risk, or controllable within the eating disorder's rule system. Safe foods can provide a starting point in treatment, but an increasingly narrow range of safe foods is a sign of illness progression. Recovery involves gradually expanding what feels safe. See also: Fear Foods.
Set Point Theory
The theory that each body has a genetically influenced weight range it is physiologically predisposed to maintain, regulating metabolism, hunger, and fullness cues to return to that range when disrupted. Set point theory helps explain why restriction leads to intense hunger, why metabolism slows during dieting, and why weight lost through restriction is typically regained. It is an important framework in eating disorder recovery and in challenging the cultural assumption that bodies can and should be controlled through diet.
Shame (as a core ED driver)
The painful belief that "I am fundamentally wrong, bad, or not enough" — distinct from guilt ("I did something wrong"). Shame is one of the most powerful drivers of eating disorder behavior. Shame about the body, about having needs, about "failing" food rules, about purging — and the shame spiral that follows a binge — maintains the eating disorder cycle. Trauma-informed, shame-sensitive therapy directly targets the shame beneath disordered behavior. Learn about trauma-informed care.
Somatic Therapy
Body-based therapeutic approaches that work with physical sensation, nervous system activation, and the body's stored experience. In eating disorder recovery, somatic work helps clients rebuild trust in body signals, process trauma held in the body, and develop a relationship with physical sensation that is not mediated by judgment or fear. Learn more about somatic approaches.
Starvation Response / Minnesota Starvation Experiment
The body's biological response to insufficient food intake — slowing metabolism, increasing food preoccupation, causing irritability and fatigue, impairing concentration, and driving obsessive food-related thoughts and behaviors. Documented in Ancel Keys' 1940s Minnesota Starvation Experiment, which showed that previously healthy men developed eating disorder-like behaviors after caloric restriction. This research is frequently used in eating disorder psychoeducation to help clients understand that their food obsession is physiological, not a character flaw.
Subclinical Eating Disorder
A presentation in which eating disorder symptoms cause significant distress or impairment but do not meet full diagnostic criteria for a clinical disorder. Subclinical eating disorders are not "less real" — they exist on the same continuum and can progress to diagnosable conditions. Many people struggle for years with subclinical patterns before receiving any support. See also: Disordered Eating.

T

Trauma-Informed Care
A clinical framework that recognizes trauma's widespread impact on mental, physical, and relational health, integrates this understanding into all aspects of treatment, and prioritizes safety, trustworthiness, choice, collaboration, and empowerment. Trauma-informed eating disorder therapy recognizes that disordered eating is often a response to trauma — a way of surviving — and that lasting recovery requires addressing what lies beneath the behavior, not just the behavior itself. Learn about trauma-informed therapy.
Treatment Team (Collaborative Care)
The group of professionals typically involved in eating disorder treatment — often including a therapist, registered dietitian, primary care physician or psychiatrist, and sometimes a higher-level-of-care program. Eating disorders affect every body system and require coordinated care. A collaborative treatment team communicates across disciplines to ensure physical and psychological safety and to avoid working at cross-purposes.
Trigger
A stimulus — internal (emotion, thought, body sensation) or external (person, place, event, media) — that provokes eating disorder urges or behaviors. Identifying personal triggers is a key component of relapse prevention work. Common eating disorder triggers include weigh-ins, body comments, stressful events, social eating situations, certain foods or food environments, and exposure to diet culture content.

U

UFED (Unspecified Feeding or Eating Disorder)
A DSM-5 diagnostic category used when a clinician determines that a feeding or eating disorder is present but does not have enough information to specify which disorder, or when a presentation does not fit any specific category. UFED replaces the former EDNOS diagnosis for presentations without enough clinical detail. Like OSFED, UFED does not indicate a less serious condition.

W

Weight Cycling
A repeated pattern of losing and regaining weight — typically as a result of chronic dieting. Also called "yo-yo dieting." Weight cycling is associated with metabolic changes, increased food preoccupation, emotional distress, and a higher risk of developing eating disorders. Research suggests weight cycling may carry more health risks than maintaining a stable higher weight.
Weight Restoration
The process of returning to a physiologically healthy weight as part of eating disorder recovery — most commonly in the treatment of anorexia nervosa. Weight restoration is medically necessary in cases of medical compromise and creates the physiological conditions for psychological recovery: cognitive function, emotional regulation, and the capacity for therapy all improve significantly as the brain and body are adequately nourished. Weight restoration is the beginning of recovery, not the end.
Weight Stigma / Weight Bias
Negative attitudes, stereotypes, and discrimination directed at people in larger bodies — including in healthcare settings. Weight stigma causes measurable harm: people in larger bodies receive worse medical care, are less likely to seek treatment, and are more likely to experience depression, anxiety, and disordered eating as a result of stigmatizing experiences. Weight-stigmatizing healthcare environments can directly prevent people with atypical anorexia or BED from getting appropriate treatment.
Wellness Culture
A broad cultural trend that frames extreme food restriction, cleanses, detoxes, and body optimization as self-care and health. Wellness culture can be a direct pathway into disordered eating — it provides socially acceptable justifications for restriction, moralized language around food ("clean," "toxic," "inflammatory"), and community reinforcement for behaviors that are actually harmful. It is particularly insidious because it is praised rather than questioned.

Slang, Abbreviations & Informal Language Patients Often Use

People living with eating disorders — particularly those who have spent time in online communities, treatment programs, or inpatient settings — often share a distinct vocabulary. Some of this language is shorthand among people in recovery; some originated in pro-eating-disorder communities and is used to talk about the disorder in ways that avoid detection. Understanding this language helps clinicians, families, and loved ones recognize what is being communicated — and what may not be said directly.

Note on pro-ED language: Some terms below — particularly "Ana," "Mia," and the weight abbreviations (CW, GW, UGW) — originated in pro-eating-disorder communities that actively promote disordered behaviors. Recognizing these terms matters. If someone you love is using them, especially online, it is worth a conversation with a treatment provider.

"Ana" / "Anna"
A personification of anorexia nervosa — giving the eating disorder a name and treating it as a separate entity or inner voice. "Ana" is used both in therapeutic contexts (to externalize the disorder) and in pro-eating-disorder communities (where "Ana" is treated as a friend, guide, or identity). The dual use matters: in therapy, naming the ED voice can be helpful; in pro-Ana communities, it reinforces the disorder as a desired lifestyle.
"Mia"
The pro-eating-disorder community's personification of bulimia nervosa — parallel to "Ana." "Mia" appears in online communities, journals, and conversations as a way of referring to purging behaviors. As with Ana, the use of "Mia" can signal connection to pro-ED communities where bulimia is promoted as a lifestyle choice.
"ED"
Shorthand for "eating disorder" — used universally in clinical and community contexts. "My ED" or "the ED" is a common way of externalizing and naming the disorder. Also used as a descriptor: "ED behaviors," "ED thoughts," "ED voice."
"B/P" or "BP"
Shorthand for binge/purge — used in clinical notes, therapy conversations, and community spaces to describe a binge-purge episode. "I B/P'd last night" is common patient language. Clinicians use B/P as a quick notation for bulimia nervosa symptom tracking.
"Bad food" / "Good food"
Language that assigns moral value to food — dividing it into acceptable and unacceptable categories. "Good" foods are typically low-calorie, "clean," or diet-approved; "bad" foods are high-calorie, processed, or pleasurable. This language is a hallmark of food morality and eating disorder thinking, and is so embedded in diet culture that many people do not notice how loaded it is. See also: Food Morality.
"Before and after" / "Body goals"
Social media content — and personal comparison — centered on transformation images or body ideals used as aspirational targets. "Body goals" sets someone else's body as the standard for one's own. Both are associated with triggering or reinforcing body dissatisfaction and disordered eating.
"Bigorexia"
Informal term for muscle dysmorphia — the obsessive preoccupation with being insufficiently muscular. See: Muscle Dysmorphia in the clinical terms section.
"Challenge food"
Used interchangeably with fear food. A food that causes anxiety or avoidance, often targeted in therapeutic exposure work. "We're doing a challenge food today" is common language in higher levels of care.
"Cheat day" / "Cheat meal"
Diet culture terms for planned deviations from a restrictive eating plan. The concept of "cheating" on a diet frames eating enjoyable food as rule-breaking and moral failure — reinforcing guilt and the restrict-reward cycle that underlies disordered eating.
"Chew and spit"
The act of chewing food for sensory experience and then spitting it out without swallowing. Used as an attempt to enjoy food "without consequences." It is associated with significant psychological harm, does not prevent caloric absorption as much as users believe, and is considered a disordered eating behavior.
"Clean eating"
A wellness culture term for eating primarily whole, minimally processed foods. In an eating disorder context, "clean eating" often describes rigid purity rules that function as restriction. The term implies other foods are "dirty" — moral language that fuels disordered thinking. See also: Orthorexia.
"CW / GW / HW / LW / UGW"
Weight abbreviations widely used in pro-eating-disorder communities online: CW = current weight, GW = goal weight, HW = highest weight, LW = lowest weight, UGW = ultimate goal weight. These abbreviations appear in social media profiles, forum posts, and direct messages. Seeing these in a young person's online activity is a significant warning sign. In clinical settings, discussing goals in terms of behaviors and health — never weight numbers — is intentional practice.
"Detox" / "Detox tea" / "Skinny tea"
"Detox" is used to justify restriction or purging under the guise of health. "Detox teas" and "skinny teas" are laxative-containing products marketed as wellness products, particularly to young women and on social media. They cause diarrhea and electrolyte imbalances and are a form of laxative misuse. The concept of dietary "detoxing" is not supported by medical evidence.
"ED brain" / "ED voice"
Informal terms for the stream of thoughts, rules, urges, and self-criticism that feel like they come from the eating disorder rather than the authentic self. Externalizing the eating disorder — giving it a name and recognizing it as separate from who you are — is a therapeutic technique used in many treatment approaches. "That's my ED brain talking" is a sign of recovery progress.
"Earning it" / "Merit eating"
The belief that food must be earned through exercise, productivity, restriction, or good behavior. Merit eating is one of the most common disordered eating patterns and is deeply rooted in diet culture. Food is a human need, not a reward. See also: Food Morality.
"Fasting" / "IF" (Intermittent Fasting)
Intermittent fasting (IF) is a dietary pattern involving alternating windows of eating and fasting. While presented as neutral or health-promoting in wellness culture, IF is contraindicated in eating disorder recovery — it reinforces restriction, disrupts hunger cue relearning, and can rapidly accelerate disordered patterns. "I'm just doing IF" is sometimes used to mask restriction.
"Fitspiration" / "Fitspo"
Social media content that frames exercise, low body fat, and dietary restriction as aspirational. Fitspo content is associated with increased body dissatisfaction and disordered eating, particularly in people who are already vulnerable. It often contains thinly veiled pro-restriction messaging packaged as health and fitness motivation.
"Food baby"
Slang for the bloated, uncomfortable feeling after eating — sometimes used humorously, but in an eating disorder context often used to catastrophize normal post-meal fullness and justify purging or restriction. Bloating and fullness after eating are physiologically normal, and in early recovery they are often more pronounced due to slowed gastric motility.
"Guilty pleasure"
A cultural phrase that frames pleasurable food as something to feel guilty about. Guilt-pleasure framing is a direct expression of food morality — the idea that eating something enjoyable is a moral transgression. In recovery, the goal is eating food that tastes good without shame or guilt.
"Head hunger"
Colloquial term for the desire to eat in the absence of physical hunger — driven by emotion, habit, or craving. Useful as a concept in recovery, but frequently weaponized by the eating disorder to dismiss legitimate physical hunger as "not real." Note: In early restriction recovery, all hunger is real and should be honored.
"IP"
Shorthand for inpatient — used in clinical notes and community conversation to refer to inpatient medical or psychiatric hospitalization for eating disorder treatment. "I was IP for three weeks" is common patient language in describing treatment history.
"Numbers"
A euphemism for the specific weights, calorie counts, clothing sizes, or measurements that carry enormous emotional weight for someone with an eating disorder. Recovery communities intentionally avoid sharing "numbers" to prevent comparison and competition. Goal-setting in treatment is focused on behaviors, not numbers.
"OMAD" (One Meal a Day)
An extreme intermittent fasting protocol involving eating all daily calories in a single meal. OMAD is promoted in some diet and wellness communities but is a significant restriction pattern that mirrors eating disorder behaviors. "I'm just doing OMAD" is sometimes used to frame severe restriction as a health choice.
"Ortho"
Informal shorthand for orthorexia. "My ortho is really bad right now" or "she seems really ortho" — used in recovery communities and sometimes in clinical settings. See: Orthorexia.
"Pro-Ana" / "Pro-Mia"
Online communities and content that treat anorexia and bulimia as lifestyle choices rather than life-threatening illnesses — providing tips for restriction, purging, hiding behaviors from treatment providers, and reaching dangerous weights. Pro-Ana and Pro-Mia spaces are medically dangerous and psychologically harmful. If someone you care about is engaging with this content, it warrants immediate conversation with a treatment provider.
"Recovery body"
A term used in recovery communities to describe the weight changes, bloating, and body shifts that occur in early recovery — particularly during refeeding. Developing acceptance of a "recovery body" is a significant emotional challenge and an important part of the work. The body in recovery is healing, not "out of control."
"Restrict"
Used as both a verb and a noun — "I've been restricting," "going into a restrict," "in a restricting phase." In patient language, restricting can mean caloric limitation, skipping meals, cutting food groups, or simply eating less than the body needs. See: Restriction.
"Safe foods" / "Unsafe foods"
Used colloquially to describe foods that feel permissible vs. those that provoke fear or avoidance within the eating disorder's rule system. See: Safe Foods and Fear Foods in the clinical terms section.
"Slip"
A single return to a disordered eating behavior during a period of recovery — used to distinguish a temporary lapse from a full relapse. Many recovery communities encourage the language of "slip" to reduce shame and catastrophization: one difficult moment does not erase progress. What matters is what happens after the slip.
"Thinspiration" / "Thinspo"
Social media images and content featuring extremely thin bodies, shared with the intent to inspire restriction. Thinspo is directly associated with pro-eating-disorder communities and linked to worsening eating disorder symptoms. Many platforms have policies against this content; it continues to circulate through coded language, tags, and private accounts.
"Triggered"
Used broadly to mean something activated eating disorder urges, anxiety, or difficult emotions. In eating disorder treatment, identifying personal triggers — internal or external — is a key component of relapse prevention. See: Trigger.
"Void eating"
Eating in a dissociated or checked-out state — consuming food without awareness, sensory engagement, or emotional presence. Void eating is often a response to the anxiety of eating, a dissociative coping pattern, or a way of avoiding the experience of nourishment. It is distinct from binge eating in that quantity is not the central feature — absence of presence is. See also: Dissociation.
"WR" (Weight Restoration)
Shorthand for weight restoration — used in clinical settings and recovery communities. "She's in WR" or "I'm in the WR phase" is common language when describing the early stages of anorexia recovery. See: Weight Restoration.
"Wellness" (as a mask)
The wellness industry's language is frequently used to make restriction, purging, and disordered eating behaviors sound healthy and intentional. "I'm just eating clean." "I'm doing a gut reset." "I'm being wellness-focused." When wellness language escalates or becomes increasingly rigid, it can signal disordered eating being framed as self-care. See also: Wellness Culture.

A Note on Language in Recovery

Language in eating disorder recovery is never neutral. The words we use — about food, bodies, hunger, and worth — carry histories and beliefs that either reinforce the eating disorder or gently loosen its hold. Part of the work in therapy is paying attention to the language you live inside, and asking what it reveals.

If something on this page resonated — a term you recognized in yourself, language you use privately, a pattern you have not had words for — that recognition matters. It can be the beginning of a conversation.
