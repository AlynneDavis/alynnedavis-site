Healing From Trauma Is Possible — Even When It Doesn’t Feel Like It

Many women come to trauma therapy feeling like they’ve already “dealt with” their past. They’ve talked about it, maybe read about it, maybe even been in therapy before. And yet something still feels off — a low hum of anxiety they can’t trace, a distance from themselves they can’t close, patterns they keep repeating no matter how hard they try to break them.

They’re highly functional and quietly falling apart. They startle easily, disconnect from their bodies, feel shame they can’t explain, or find themselves in the same painful dynamics over and over.

If that’s you, you’re not broken. Your nervous system is doing exactly what it learned to do. And it can learn something different.

I’m Alynne Davis, LCMHC, a trauma therapist in Charlotte, NC serving women in Charlotte and throughout North Carolina and South Carolina via telehealth. My work is trauma-informed, body-centered, and rooted in the belief that healing is possible — even when you’ve stopped believing it yourself.

You Might Be Looking for a Trauma Therapist If…

Trauma doesn’t always look like a single dramatic event. For many women, it’s the accumulation of smaller things — a childhood that never felt quite safe, relationships that left marks, a body that braced itself for too long. You might be ready for trauma therapy if:

  • Something happened (or many things happened) that you’ve never fully processed
  • You feel numb, disconnected, or like you’re watching your life from a distance
  • You’re exhausted from hypervigilance — always waiting for the next bad thing
  • Your body holds tension, pain, or reactivity that doesn’t match the current moment
  • Relationships feel scary, or you keep ending up in the same painful dynamic
  • You’ve been told you’re “too sensitive” or “too much”
  • You feel shame that doesn’t make rational sense
  • You had a “normal” childhood on the outside, but something still feels wounded

You don’t need a diagnosis or a story that “counts.” If your past is showing up in your present — that’s enough.

Trauma Therapy Approaches I Use

Trauma lives in the body, in the nervous system, and in the parts of us that learned to protect us when the world felt unsafe. My approach brings together several evidence-informed modalities to meet trauma where it actually lives — not just in the story you tell, but in the way you hold your breath, brace your body, and quietly brace for impact.

Expressive Arts Therapy

Trauma often lives deeper than language. The words for what happened — or the way it felt — may not be fully accessible yet. Expressive arts therapy creates another doorway in. Through writing, visual art, imagery, and creative process, we give your inner world a way to speak without forcing it into a narrative it isn’t ready for. This work can access what talk therapy alone can’t always reach. Learn more about expressive arts therapy and how it works.

Somatic and Body-Based Approaches

Trauma lives in the body. The tight chest, the shallow breath, the flinch that doesn’t match the present moment — these aren’t random. They’re your nervous system doing its job. Somatic approaches help you slow down and notice what your body is actually holding, and gently begin to work with those patterns rather than against them. We use breath, sensation awareness, and body-based tools to help your system learn that the threat has passed. Read more about somatic therapy for trauma.

IFS — Internal Family Systems

Internal Family Systems (IFS) offers a compassionate map for understanding why you do things you don’t want to do — why part of you shuts down, another part rages, another part works constantly to keep everything together. IFS helps you understand the protective parts that developed in response to painful experiences, and gently access the wounded places underneath them. This work builds an internal relationship with yourself rooted in curiosity rather than self-criticism. Explore IFS therapy for trauma.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

Trauma shapes the way we think about ourselves, others, and the world. CBT helps identify the beliefs and thought patterns that developed as survival strategies — “I’m not safe,” “I can’t trust anyone,” “It was my fault” — and gently examine whether they still serve you. This isn’t about positive thinking. It’s about updating the operating system that formed under impossible circumstances.

This Isn’t About Retelling Your Story

A common fear about trauma therapy is that it means rehashing every painful detail over and over. That’s not how I work.

Healing doesn’t require you to narrate your worst moments in clinical detail. What it requires is helping your nervous system, body, and mind reconnect — so that the past stops running the present. The goal isn’t to talk about what happened more. It’s to help you feel differently when you think about it. To have a body that doesn’t brace. To have a present that actually feels present.

Pacing matters deeply in this work. We don’t go faster than your nervous system can tolerate. We build safety first — inside the therapy relationship and inside your own experience — before we go anywhere tender.

Trauma-informed also means this: your survival responses are intelligent. They are not character flaws, overreactions, or signs that something is wrong with you. They are the adaptations of a system that was doing its best. My job is to honor that, and help you build something more spacious in its place.

Types of Trauma I Work With

Trauma takes many forms. I work with women navigating:

  • Childhood trauma and adverse childhood experiences (ACEs)
  • Complex PTSD and developmental trauma
  • Relational and attachment trauma
  • Sexual trauma and assault
  • Medical trauma
  • Grief, loss, and traumatic loss
  • Trauma connected to eating disorders or body image

If you’re not sure whether what you experienced “counts” as trauma — it doesn’t need a label to be worth healing. Read more about how trauma connects to eating and body image, and explore our guide on what trauma-informed therapy actually looks like.

What Trauma Therapy Looks Like With Me

Your First Session

Your first session is a space to tell your story — as much or as little as feels right. I’ll ask questions and listen closely, and we’ll begin to understand what’s been happening and what you’re hoping for. There’s no intake assessment to rush through. It’s a conversation, and it moves at your pace.

Weekly Sessions

Sessions are 50 minutes, typically weekly. Trauma work asks something real of you, and the container needs to be steady enough to hold it — which means we move at a pace that feels both honest and safe. Not so slow that nothing shifts, but not so fast that you feel flooded. Your window of tolerance guides the pace.

In Person & Telehealth

I practice in Charlotte, NC and offer telehealth to clients throughout North Carolina and South Carolina. Many women doing trauma work find that telehealth provides a layer of comfort — you’re in your own space, on your own terms, with no commute after a session that went somewhere deep.

Free 15-Minute Consultation

Before you commit to anything, a free consultation is available. It’s a chance to ask questions, share a little about where you are, and get a sense of whether we feel like a good fit. No pressure, no forms. Just a conversation.
